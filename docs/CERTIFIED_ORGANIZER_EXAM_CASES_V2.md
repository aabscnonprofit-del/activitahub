# Certified Organizer Exam — Case Bank v2

Revised case bank for the experienced-organizer test-out exam. Built from
CERTIFIED_ORGANIZER_EXAM_CASES_V1, the findings in
CERTIFIED_ORGANIZER_CASE_AUDIT_V1, and the roles/competencies in
ACTIVLIFE_HUB_ORGANIZER_FOUNDATION_V1.

Cases only. No questions, no scoring, no answer keys.

**Each case is tagged by type:**
- **[Judgment]** — open; two strong candidates may answer differently and both be right. These *rank* skill.
- **[Gate]** — a safety or ethics floor with one defensible stance. These *exclude*; they are not meant to separate good from great, only safe from unsafe.

Each case keeps the five fields: **Scenario · Competency tested · What a strong
organizer notices · What a weak organizer misses · Why it matters.** Provenance
tags (e.g. *was V1 C4*) are for traceability only.

## Section A — Understanding Client Intent

### A1 — "A royal day for my daughter" [Judgment] *(V1 C1)*
- **Scenario:** A client asks for "a royal day" for their daughter. No age, guest count, budget, or date.
- **Competency tested:** Understanding client intent.
- **Strong organizer notices:** "Royal" is a feeling, kept as context (she wants her daughter to feel special), not a spec. Age changes everything; they resist planning until they know it.
- **Weak organizer misses:** Locks onto "royal" and prices castles and thrones before learning the girl's age or budget.
- **Why it matters:** The costliest mistake is planning confidently in the wrong direction. Reading intent is where the chain starts.

### A2 — "Something fun for my team" [Judgment] *(V1 C2)*
- **Scenario:** A manager wants "something fun for my team" — no headcount, ages, fitness, or definition of fun.
- **Competency tested:** Understanding client intent.
- **Strong organizer notices:** "Fun" differs across teams; some love physical challenges, others dread them. They treat "fun" as a question, not an answer.
- **Weak organizer misses:** Books a default crowd-pleaser half the team can't or won't do.
- **Why it matters:** "Fun" that excludes people becomes a complaint to the manager — the opposite of the goal.

### A3 — The parents whose situation has changed [Judgment] *(V1 C3, revised)*
- **Scenario:** A client wants "a wonderful, unforgettable evening" for their parents' anniversary. In passing they mention their father "tires more easily these days." Nothing else about health, timing, or guest list.
- **Competency tested:** Understanding client intent; seniors.
- **Strong organizer notices:** The throwaway line about the father is the most important thing said. A long evening event may be wrong; they read the unspoken decline and gently explore stamina, timing of day, seating, and pace before designing anything.
- **Weak organizer misses:** Hears only "wonderful unforgettable evening" and plans a late, high-energy event the guests of honor can't enjoy.
- **Why it matters:** The real constraint was hidden inside warm language and a half-sentence; missing it ruins the event being celebrated.

## Section B — Discovery

### B1 — "Forty invited" [Judgment] *(V1 C4, revised)*
- **Scenario:** For a child's party the client says "I've invited 40." That's the only number given.
- **Competency tested:** Discovery; children.
- **Strong organizer notices:** "Invited" is not "attending." They ask expected turnout, RSVPs so far, the kids-to-adults split, and how many supervising adults — and plan for a realistic range, not the invite list.
- **Weak organizer misses:** Takes "40" as the headcount and plans to it, over- or under-providing and miscounting supervision.
- **Why it matters:** A number that sounds precise can be the wrong number; real discovery questions what a figure actually means.

### B2 — The client who never mentions budget [Judgment] *(V1 C5)*
- **Scenario:** A client describes a vivid vision but never names a number, and asking feels awkward.
- **Competency tested:** Discovery; budget.
- **Strong organizer notices:** Budget is the most-skipped, most-important fact. They raise it early, kindly and clearly, because everything depends on it.
- **Weak organizer misses:** Avoids the money talk, builds a beautiful plan, watches it collapse on price.
- **Why it matters:** A plan the client can't afford wastes everyone's time and damages trust.

### B3 — "An intimate dinner for 60" [Judgment] *(V1 C6)*
- **Scenario:** The client asks for "a small, intimate dinner," then mentions "about 60 people."
- **Competency tested:** Discovery; unclear requests.
- **Strong organizer notices:** The two facts contradict each other. They flag it and ask which matters more — the intimate feel or the 60 guests — rather than silently choosing.
- **Weak organizer misses:** Plans for 60 and drops "intimate," or plans intimate and is blindsided by the count.
- **Why it matters:** Unresolved contradictions become a client certain they were misunderstood.

### B4 — The unstated mobility need [Judgment] *(V1 C7)*
- **Scenario:** A client books a 70th birthday at a venue and mentions only that "Grandma will be there."
- **Competency tested:** Discovery; seniors, accessibility.
- **Strong organizer notices:** A guest may need step-free access, seating, or restroom access. They ask directly about anyone needing it.
- **Weak organizer misses:** Books a venue with stairs and no accessible restroom because no one said "wheelchair."
- **Why it matters:** The client isn't responsible for knowing what to disclose; the organizer is responsible for asking.

### B5 — The team event's hidden constraints [Judgment] *(V1 C8)*
- **Scenario:** A manager books a team activity, not mentioning two pregnant members and one with a knee injury.
- **Competency tested:** Discovery; conflicting participant needs.
- **Strong organizer notices:** "Whole team" events need a question about anyone with physical limits, dietary needs, or exclusions — before choosing the activity.
- **Weak organizer misses:** Picks a high-intensity activity, then scrambles when three people can't take part.
- **Why it matters:** An activity that sidelines colleagues defeats the purpose and embarrasses the client.

### B6 — The unspoken cultural and religious needs [Judgment] *(new — cultural/religious)*
- **Scenario:** A client books a large family celebration. They don't mention that the guests observe religious dietary rules, that alcohol would offend many of them, and that there will be a prayer time during the event window.
- **Competency tested:** Discovery; cultural and religious needs.
- **Strong organizer notices:** Family and community events often carry dietary, alcohol, gender, and timing expectations the client assumes are "obvious." They ask, respectfully and without assuming, about dietary rules, drink expectations, and any timing the schedule must respect.
- **Weak organizer misses:** Plans a standard menu and open bar and an unbroken timeline, then offends the very guests the event is meant to honor.
- **Why it matters:** Cultural and religious needs are frequently unsaid because the client takes them for granted; missing them can wreck an event and a reputation.

### B7 — The participant who communicates differently [Judgment] *(new — neurodiverse/language inclusion)*
- **Scenario:** Booking a group activity, the client mentions that one attendee "doesn't speak much English" and another "gets overwhelmed in loud crowds."
- **Competency tested:** Discovery; inclusion (neurodiverse / language).
- **Strong organizer notices:** Both are real accommodations, not footnotes. They ask what each person needs — a quieter space or sensory breaks, written or translated instructions, a buddy — and build it in without singling anyone out.
- **Weak organizer misses:** Treats these as the participants' problem and runs a loud, fast, English-only activity that leaves two people lost.
- **Why it matters:** Inclusion isn't only ramps and diets; recognizing sensory and language needs is core to an event that works for everyone present.

## Section C — Concept Formation

### C1 — Turning facts into a concept [Judgment] *(V1 C9)*
- **Scenario:** Discovery done: mum's 60th, ~40 adults, mid-July, at the client's home, ~$3,000, "relaxed and warm."
- **Competency tested:** Concept formation.
- **Strong organizer notices:** The facts fit a realistic concept (a relaxed catered garden gathering for 40 with a July weather plan). They assemble it coherently and plan to confirm before building.
- **Weak organizer misses:** Jumps to logistics with no shared concept, or proposes something off-tone like a formal seated banquet.
- **Why it matters:** The concept is what the client says "yes" to; skipping it means planning something never agreed.

### C2 — The champagne concept on a lemonade budget [Judgment] *(V1 C10)*
- **Scenario:** Client wants a wedding-style outdoor evening reception for 80 — open bar, live band, plated dinner — on a budget that realistically covers a casual afternoon BBQ.
- **Competency tested:** Concept formation; unrealistic expectations, budget.
- **Strong organizer notices:** Concept and budget don't meet. They shape an honest, achievable concept that protects what matters most to the client and say plainly what the budget will and won't buy.
- **Weak organizer misses:** Promises the wedding event anyway, or silently strips it down without saying what changed.
- **Why it matters:** A concept built on a budget that can't support it fails on the day; honesty here protects everyone.

### C3 — The concept that drifts from the client [Judgment] *(V1 C11)*
- **Scenario:** Client wants a small home celebration for their father; the organizer, used to bigger events, finds a restaurant buyout easier to deliver.
- **Competency tested:** Concept formation.
- **Strong organizer notices:** "At home, small" is part of the want, not an inconvenience. They build around it rather than substituting what's easier for them.
- **Weak organizer misses:** Quietly swaps the home celebration for a venue.
- **Why it matters:** Substituting the organizer's preference for the client's intent breaks trust even when the result is "nicer."

### C4 — One event, children and grandparents [Judgment] *(new — mixed children & seniors)*
- **Scenario:** A family reunion will include young children and elderly grandparents together, all day, outdoors.
- **Competency tested:** Concept formation; conflicting participant needs (children + seniors).
- **Strong organizer notices:** The two groups need almost opposite things — kids need space to run and energy outlets; seniors need shade, seating, calm, and easy access. They design a concept that holds both (zones, pacing, rest areas) instead of optimizing for one.
- **Weak organizer misses:** Builds around one group — a kid-centric romp that exhausts the elders, or a sedate gathering that bores the children.
- **Why it matters:** Many real events serve mixed populations at once; a concept that ignores the tension fails half the room.

## Section D — Converting Requests into OPE-Ready Inputs

### D1 — The class you can't plan yet [Judgment] *(V1 C12 + C13 merged, reframed)*
- **Scenario:** A confirmed concept: a recurring children's class for ~15 kids at a community hall. No one has said whether a teacher already exists or needs finding, whether the kids bring their own supplies or they're provided, or how many adults will supervise.
- **Competency tested:** Converting a concept into a complete, plannable request.
- **Strong organizer notices:** You simply can't plan or cost a class without knowing whether there's a teacher, whether supplies exist, and the adult-to-child supervision. They gather these real-world essentials and hand over a complete picture — not a half-filled one.
- **Weak organizer misses:** Pushes an incomplete request forward and guesses the gaps, so the resulting plan is built on assumptions.
- **Why it matters:** A plan is only as good as the request behind it; the skill is recognizing what's genuinely missing to plan well — not reciting any field name.

### D2 — The invented headcount [Gate] *(V1 C14)*
- **Scenario:** The client never confirmed a headcount, but to move the request along the organizer writes "30."
- **Competency tested:** Integrity in converting requests; not inventing facts.
- **Strong organizer notices:** A required value that's a guess is not a fact. They get the real number rather than fabricating one to complete the request.
- **Weak organizer misses:** Submits the invented number, so the whole plan is built around something the client never said.
- **Why it matters:** Inventing inputs breaks the link between the plan and the real event — a disqualifying failure.

## Section E — Communication Judgment

### E1 — The angry message [Judgment] *(V1 C15)*
- **Scenario:** Three days out, the client sends an angry message accusing the organizer of ignoring them — though the organizer replied two days ago.
- **Competency tested:** Communication judgment.
- **Strong organizer notices:** The client is stressed, not necessarily right. They respond calmly, acknowledge the worry, restate where things stand, and de-escalate — without getting defensive.
- **Weak organizer misses:** Fires back to prove they did reply, turning a nervous client into an adversary.
- **Why it matters:** How a tense moment is handled decides whether a worry becomes a blow-up.

### E2 — The pressure to over-promise [Gate] *(V1 C16)*
- **Scenario:** A client pushes: "Just promise it'll be perfect and I'll book now." The organizer knows one detail (a specific florist) can't be guaranteed.
- **Competency tested:** Honesty in communication.
- **Strong organizer notices:** Winning the booking by promising the un-guaranteeable is a trap. They commit warmly to what they can guarantee and stay honest about the one thing they can't.
- **Weak organizer misses:** Says "yes, perfect, I promise," and sets up a guaranteed disappointment.
- **Why it matters:** Dishonesty to close a booking is a disqualifying failure and destroys trust.

### E3 — The client who goes silent [Judgment] *(V1 C17)*
- **Scenario:** After a proposal is sent, the client goes quiet for a week.
- **Competency tested:** Communication judgment; follow-up.
- **Strong organizer notices:** Silence usually means hesitation or sticker shock, not rejection. They follow up once, politely and usefully (offering to adjust, answering likely concerns) — not pestering.
- **Weak organizer misses:** Either gives up immediately or sends a string of anxious messages.
- **Why it matters:** Most lost bookings are lost in the silence; good follow-up judgment is real skill without being pushy.

### E4 — The client who keeps adding [Judgment] *(new — scope creep)*
- **Scenario:** After the concept and price are agreed, the client keeps adding "small" requests — a photographer, a second cake, extended hours, more guests — expecting the price to hold.
- **Competency tested:** Communication judgment; boundaries.
- **Strong organizer notices:** Each addition has a real cost in time, money, or feasibility. They welcome the ideas but hold the line clearly and warmly — naming what each change means for price and scope — instead of silently absorbing it all.
- **Weak organizer misses:** Either says yes to everything and quietly eats the cost (resentment, a worse event), or pushes back so bluntly the client feels nickel-and-dimed.
- **Why it matters:** Scope creep, handled badly, wrecks either the margin or the relationship; handling it well protects both.

### E5 — The request for another guest's details [Gate] *(new — participant privacy)*
- **Scenario:** One attendee messages the organizer asking for another participant's phone number and address, saying they "just want to coordinate."
- **Competency tested:** Communication judgment; participant privacy.
- **Strong organizer notices:** Participants' personal details aren't the organizer's to hand out. They decline to share without consent and instead offer to pass a message or connect the two with permission.
- **Weak organizer misses:** Shares the details to be helpful, exposing a participant who never agreed to it.
- **Why it matters:** People trust the platform and the organizer with their data; handing it out without consent breaks that trust and can endanger someone.

## Section F — Safety Awareness

### F1 — The bouncy castle and missing supervision [Gate] *(V1 C18)*
- **Scenario:** A kids' birthday for 25 children with a bouncy castle; the client has arranged two adults total, one of them the busy host.
- **Competency tested:** Safety awareness; children.
- **Strong organizer notices:** 25 children plus an inflatable needs dedicated supervision and clear rules; one distracted host is not a plan. They fix the ratio before the day.
- **Weak organizer misses:** Treats supervision as the client's problem and proceeds.
- **Why it matters:** Child injuries are the most serious failure mode; under-supervision is how they happen.

### F2 — The seniors' walk in a heatwave [Judgment] *(V1 C19)*
- **Scenario:** An outdoor walk for seniors is scheduled for midday in forecast 34°C heat.
- **Competency tested:** Safety awareness; seniors, weather.
- **Strong organizer notices:** Heat is dangerous for older participants. They move it to early morning, add shade, water, rest stops, and seating — or reconsider the activity. (Several valid mitigations exist.)
- **Weak organizer misses:** Runs it at noon because "it's just a walk."
- **Why it matters:** The same activity carries different risk for different people — core safety judgment.

### F3 — The wheelchair user and the upstairs venue [Gate] *(V1 C20)*
- **Scenario:** A participant uses a wheelchair; the venue's event room and only restroom are up a flight of stairs with no lift.
- **Competency tested:** Safety awareness; accessibility, disabilities.
- **Strong organizer notices:** This venue excludes the participant and must change or be adapted — step-free access and an accessible restroom are non-negotiable.
- **Weak organizer misses:** Plans to "carry the chair up" or assumes the participant will manage — unsafe and humiliating.
- **Why it matters:** Accessibility failures endanger and exclude people; the organizer owns the duty to prevent that.

### F4 — The allergy the caterer can't guarantee [Gate] *(V1 C21)*
- **Scenario:** A child attending has a severe peanut allergy; the caterer "can't guarantee a nut-free kitchen."
- **Competency tested:** Safety awareness; allergies, children.
- **Strong organizer notices:** "Can't guarantee" + "severe allergy" + "child" is a real risk. They change caterer or menu, confirm controls, and make sure responsible adults know the emergency plan.
- **Weak organizer misses:** Accepts "we'll be careful" and moves on.
- **Why it matters:** A mishandled allergy can be life-threatening; casual reassurance is not enough.

### F5 — The rain forecast with no easy backup [Judgment] *(V1 C22, revised)*
- **Scenario:** An outdoor party for 50 is two days out with 70% rain forecast. The venue has no indoor space, and the budget is already tight.
- **Competency tested:** Safety awareness; weather risks; planning under constraint.
- **Strong organizer notices:** "Have a backup" isn't free here. They weigh real options — renting a tent, shifting the date, moving to a covered alternative, trimming elsewhere to fund cover — and make a defensible call with the client, rather than hoping it holds.
- **Weak organizer misses:** Has no plan and "will decide on the day," or assumes a backup exists when it doesn't.
- **Why it matters:** Weather is foreseeable; the test is sound judgment when the safe option costs something, not just knowing a backup is wise.

### F6 — The unsupervised lake swim [Gate] *(V1 C23)*
- **Scenario:** A client insists on an unsupervised lake swim during a children's outdoor party — no lifeguard, no one trained.
- **Competency tested:** Safety awareness; children. Knowing when to refuse.
- **Strong organizer notices:** Some requests must be refused or fundamentally changed. They will not run an unsupervised child water activity and say so clearly, offering a safe alternative.
- **Weak organizer misses:** Treats "the client asked" as permission and runs it.
- **Why it matters:** Knowing when to say no is core safety competence; willingness to run a clearly unsafe event is disqualifying.

### F7 — The client who wants a corner cut [Gate] *(new — safety vs client pressure)*
- **Scenario:** To save money, the client pushes the organizer to skip the qualified lifeguard at a pool party, or to drop the accessible-transport arrangement a disabled guest needs — "it'll be fine, just this once."
- **Competency tested:** Safety awareness under client pressure.
- **Strong organizer notices:** A safety or access measure is not a line-item to trade away for budget. They hold firm, explain why it stays, and find the saving elsewhere — or decline the event as framed.
- **Weak organizer misses:** Caves to the paying client and removes the protection, putting someone at real risk.
- **Why it matters:** This is where safety is actually lost — not from ignorance, but from pressure. Trading away a safety measure to please a client is disqualifying.

### F8 — The event that's too big to run safely [Gate] *(new — refusing unsafe over-capacity)*
- **Scenario:** A client wants to pack 200 people into a space and a plan the organizer knows can only be run safely for around 120 — exits, supervision, and staffing won't cover it.
- **Competency tested:** Safety awareness; refusing unsafe over-capacity.
- **Strong organizer notices:** Capacity isn't a preference; it's a safety limit. They refuse to overfill the space and offer a safe alternative (larger venue, fewer guests, more staff) rather than take on what they can't run safely.
- **Weak organizer misses:** Says yes to the bigger number and the bigger fee, and runs an overcrowded, unsafe event.
- **Why it matters:** Overcrowding is a classic, preventable disaster; the willingness to refuse it is part of the job.

## Section G — Planning Judgment

### G1 — The understaffed plan [Judgment] *(V1 C24)*
- **Scenario:** A plan for a 120-guest event lists the same staffing as a 40-guest one.
- **Competency tested:** Planning judgment.
- **Strong organizer notices:** Staffing doesn't scale with headcount — service, safety, and setup will fall short. They catch and adjust it before the plan goes out.
- **Weak organizer misses:** Forwards the plan as-is because it "looked complete."
- **Why it matters:** The plan may be generated, but the organizer owns the result; sense-checking against reality is the job.

### G2 — The budget that won't stretch [Judgment] *(V1 C25)*
- **Scenario:** The plan is lovely, but catering alone consumes the whole budget, leaving nothing for the venue and rentals it also lists.
- **Competency tested:** Planning judgment; budget.
- **Strong organizer notices:** The numbers don't reconcile. They rework the plan to fit the real budget and have an honest trade-off conversation before committing. (Many valid reworks exist.)
- **Weak organizer misses:** Presents an unaffordable plan and finds the gap too late.
- **Why it matters:** A plan that doesn't add up is a failure waiting to happen.

### G3 — The plan that fits everyone but this client [Judgment] *(V1 C26, revised)*
- **Scenario:** A plan for an adult birthday is internally sound and complete — but it's a formal seated evening dinner, and this client specifically wanted a casual daytime garden gathering where people mingle.
- **Competency tested:** Planning judgment; holding the client's concept.
- **Strong organizer notices:** "Complete and correct" isn't the same as "right for this client." They measure the plan against the agreed concept and rework the format, not just the details.
- **Weak organizer misses:** Accepts the plan because nothing in it is technically wrong.
- **Why it matters:** A flawless plan for the wrong event is still the wrong event; planning judgment means keeping the client's concept in view, not just checking for errors.

## Section H — Execution Judgment

### H1 — The caterer who doesn't show [Judgment] *(V1 C27)*
- **Scenario:** On the day, the caterer is two hours late with no answer; 60 guests arrive in 90 minutes.
- **Competency tested:** Execution judgment.
- **Strong organizer notices:** They work the problem immediately — chase, line up a fallback, adjust the timeline — and keep client and guests from feeling the panic.
- **Weak organizer misses:** Freezes, or broadcasts the crisis and loses the room.
- **Why it matters:** A good plan still has to survive the day; staying in control under failure is its own skill.

### H2 — The weather that turns mid-event [Judgment] *(V1 C28)*
- **Scenario:** An outdoor event is underway when the sky opens unexpectedly.
- **Competency tested:** Execution judgment; weather.
- **Strong organizer notices:** They move calmly to the backup, get guests dry and comfortable, and keep the event flowing.
- **Weak organizer misses:** Has nowhere to move people and lets the event dissolve.
- **Why it matters:** Execution judgment is tested precisely when the plan meets reality.

### H3 — The guest count that doubled [Judgment] *(V1 C29)*
- **Scenario:** The plan was for 40; about 75 people walk in.
- **Competency tested:** Execution judgment.
- **Strong organizer notices:** They triage on the spot — stretch food and seating, adjust flow, get help — and manage the client's expectations honestly about what's now possible.
- **Weak organizer misses:** Pretends nothing's wrong until food and seating visibly run out.
- **Why it matters:** Real events go off-script; adapting without losing composure is execution competence.

### H4 — The disruptive guest [Judgment] *(new — disruptive participant)*
- **Scenario:** Mid-event, one guest is drunk and loud, making others uncomfortable and starting to be unsafe.
- **Competency tested:** Execution judgment; participant behavior.
- **Strong organizer notices:** The duty is to the other guests' safety and experience. They intervene calmly and discreetly — de-escalate, involve the right people, arrange a safe exit if needed — without turning it into a scene.
- **Weak organizer misses:** Ignores it and hopes, or confronts the guest publicly and makes it worse.
- **Why it matters:** Protecting the room from one person's behavior, calmly and safely, is a real and common execution test.

### H5 — The last-minute change [Judgment] *(new — late client change)*
- **Scenario:** Hours before the event, the client demands a significant change — a new running order, a different headcount, a venue layout switch.
- **Competency tested:** Execution judgment; handling change under time pressure.
- **Strong organizer notices:** They quickly assess what's actually possible in the time left, tell the client honestly what can and can't be done, and execute the achievable changes without destabilizing the rest.
- **Weak organizer misses:** Either refuses flatly, or says yes to everything and unravels the whole plan trying.
- **Why it matters:** Late changes are normal; the skill is sorting the possible from the impossible under pressure and staying in control.

## Section I — Post-Event Judgment

### I1 — The late complaint about an incident [Judgment] *(V1 C30)*
- **Scenario:** A week after a kids' event, a parent messages that their child was hurt on equipment and the organizer "didn't seem to care."
- **Competency tested:** Post-event judgment; ethics, safety aftermath.
- **Strong organizer notices:** This needs a serious, non-defensive response — acknowledge, find out exactly what happened, own what's theirs, and fix the process so it can't recur.
- **Weak organizer misses:** Gets defensive, disputes the parent, or goes silent.
- **Why it matters:** How a safety incident is handled afterward decides whether it's resolved or becomes lasting damage.

### I2 — The quiet client and the mixed feedback [Judgment] *(V1 C31)*
- **Scenario:** The event went mostly well but had a rough patch. The client thanks the organizer briefly and goes quiet; a couple of guests left lukewarm comments.
- **Competency tested:** Post-event judgment.
- **Strong organizer notices:** A short "thanks" after a bumpy event can signal unspoken disappointment. They follow up warmly, invite honest feedback, address the rough patch, and learn from it.
- **Weak organizer misses:** Takes the brief thanks at face value and never closes the loop.
- **Why it matters:** Aftercare decides whether a one-time client returns; the work isn't done when the event ends.

### I3 — The payment dispute after the event [Judgment] *(new — post-event payment dispute)*
- **Scenario:** After the event the client withholds part of the payment, saying one thing "wasn't what they expected," though most of the event went well.
- **Competency tested:** Post-event judgment; money disputes.
- **Strong organizer notices:** They separate a fair grievance from an unfair one, look at what was actually agreed, and aim for a resolution that's fair to both — neither caving reflexively nor digging in — keeping the relationship and their reputation intact.
- **Weak organizer misses:** Either eats the loss to avoid conflict regardless of fairness, or fights every dollar and burns the relationship and their reviews.
- **Why it matters:** How money disputes are handled after the fact shapes reputation, repeat business, and whether disagreements stay civil.
