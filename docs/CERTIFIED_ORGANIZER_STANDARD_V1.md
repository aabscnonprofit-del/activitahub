# Certified Organizer — Competency Standard v1

> **Purpose:** define the **minimum competency standard** required to become a Certified Organizer on
> ActivLife Hub. It answers one question: **"What must a Certified Organizer be capable of doing?"**
> **This is a competency standard.** It is *not* a curriculum, a lesson plan, an exam, or a philosophy
> document — those exist separately and serve this standard.
> **Foundational philosophy (the bar):** a Certified Organizer is **not a perfect organizer — a
> trustworthy one.** The goal is not perfection; it is **consistent, reliable, safe delivery** of
> real-world activities.
> **Built on:** `ACTIVLIFEHUB_ORGANIZER_PHILOSOPHY_V1.md` (Organizer Standard / F3),
> `ORGANIZER_ACADEMY_MODULES_V1.md`, `MASTER_PRODUCT_DECISIONS.md` (§3, §10.4).
> **Date:** 2026-06-06

---

## 1. Purpose

The standard exists so that **certification means the same thing every time**: that a client, a
participant, and the platform can trust a Certified Organizer to plan and deliver a real activity
**safely and reliably**, without supervision. It is the shared definition of "good enough to be
trusted" — the line the Academy trains toward and the exam validates against. It protects participants,
protects the reputation every organizer shares (Philosophy #8), and keeps the credential credible.

---

## 2. Certified Organizer Definition

**What certification represents:** the holder can **independently, consistently, and safely** deliver
typical real-world activities to a professional standard, operate within the ActivLife Hub ecosystem,
and uphold the Organizer Standard.

**What certification does NOT represent:**
- Not a guarantee of **perfection** — every organizer still makes mistakes.
- Not **expert or master** status — certification is the *competent and trustworthy* baseline, not the
  ceiling (see §7).
- Not a mark of **artistic taste, popularity, or scale** — it certifies reliability and safety, not flair.
- Not a **legal license or insurance** — it is a competency credential, not a regulatory permission.

---

## 3. Core Competency Areas

A Certified Organizer is assessed across **seven competency domains**:

- **A. Planning & Preparation**
- **B. Communication & Participants**
- **C. Vendors & Resources**
- **D. Event Operations**
- **E. Safety & Responsibility** *(the anchor domain — see §5)*
- **F. Platform & OPE Usage**
- **G. Professional Conduct**

Each domain is defined below as **Knowledge · Skills · Behaviors** — what they understand, what they can
do, and how they consistently act.

---

## 4. Competency Requirements

### A. Planning & Preparation
- **Knowledge:** understands the event lifecycle, the planning process, resources, dependencies, and
  budgeting basics.
- **Skills:** can produce a structured plan, timeline, checklist, and a defensible budget for a typical
  activity.
- **Behaviors:** plans ahead, builds in buffers, and confirms key details **before** they become
  problems (prevention).

### B. Communication & Participants
- **Knowledge:** understands expectation-setting, clear communication, conflict dynamics, and how
  reviews work.
- **Skills:** can communicate clearly and promptly, set honest expectations, de-escalate conflict, and
  earn good reviews.
- **Behaviors:** keeps people informed, sets realistic expectations, treats participants with respect,
  and responds reliably.

### C. Vendors & Resources
- **Knowledge:** understands venues, contractors, service providers, negotiation basics, and the value
  of documentation.
- **Skills:** can source, brief, and compare vendors, negotiate fair terms, reconcile them to the
  budget, and record what was agreed.
- **Behaviors:** confirms scope clearly, avoids risky over-reliance on a single vendor, and verifies
  vendor suitability.

### D. Event Operations
- **Knowledge:** understands execution, run-of-show, and on-the-day coordination.
- **Skills:** can execute a plan, coordinate people and vendors, keep an event on track, and adapt
  calmly when things shift.
- **Behaviors:** runs to a timeline, stays composed, solves issues without alarming guests, and delivers
  what was promised.

### E. Safety & Responsibility *(anchor — minimum standard detailed in §5)*
- **Knowledge:** understands risk assessment, duty of care, the heightened risks of children / sports /
  outdoor / water / transportation, and emergency thinking.
- **Skills:** can run a basic risk assessment, design preventive safeguards, and prepare an emergency
  plan.
- **Behaviors:** addresses risks **before** execution, never lowers the safety bar for convenience or
  cost, and puts people's safety first (prevention over recovery).

### F. Platform & OPE Usage
- **Knowledge:** understands the marketplace, Event Requests, organizer profiles, reviews,
  certification/subscription, and OPE.
- **Skills:** can use **OPE** to produce a plan, budget, and client proposal; operate a profile; respond
  to Event Requests; and manage bookings and payments.
- **Behaviors:** uses the platform honestly and professionally, keeping profile and proposals accurate.

### G. Professional Conduct
- **Knowledge:** understands the Organizer Standard (F3) and how trust and reputation are built and lost.
- **Skills:** can represent themselves accurately, deliver on commitments, and uphold standards under
  pressure.
- **Behaviors:** promises only what they can deliver, respects people's time, protects the shared trust
  of all organizers, and acts with integrity.

## 5. Safety Standard

*Special section. Anchored in **Prevention Over Recovery** (Philosophy #4, #7).* The minimum
expectation in every setting is the same shape: **identify the risk → prevent it → be prepared if
prevention fails.** A Certified Organizer also **recognizes the limits of their own competence** and
declines or escalates an activity that exceeds them — that is itself a trustworthy behavior.

- **General (all activities):** a basic risk assessment and a prevention plan **before** the event; basic
  emergency awareness.
- **Children:** appropriate supervision and safeguarding; a heightened standard of care; awareness of
  who is responsible for whom at all times.
- **Sports:** mitigation of physical-activity risk; awareness of participant suitability; appropriate
  supervision and conditions.
- **Outdoor activities:** weather and environment contingencies; terrain and access awareness; a way to
  communicate and respond if conditions change.
- **Water activities:** the highest duty of care; appropriate safeguards and supervision; honesty about
  competence limits — **never improvise beyond capability.**
- **Transportation:** safe, reliable arrangements; verification that transport and drivers are suitable
  for the participants and the journey.

The minimum standard is **not** "can handle an emergency heroically." It is **"prevents the emergency
from happening"** — and, where risk is high or beyond the organizer's competence, has the judgment to
add safeguards, bring in expertise, or not run the activity.

## 6. Professional Standard

**Trustworthy organizers do:**
- Communicate honestly and set realistic expectations.
- Show up prepared and on time.
- Confirm and record what was agreed.
- Put safety first and plan for prevention.
- Ask for help — or decline — when an activity is beyond their competence.
- Follow through and deliver what they promised.

**Trustworthy organizers do not:**
- Overpromise to win work.
- Hide or surprise people with costs.
- Cut safety corners for convenience, cost, or momentum.
- Go silent or leave people guessing.
- Blame participants for the organizer's gaps.
- Take on activities they are not competent to run safely.
- Do anything that damages the trust every organizer shares.

## 7. Certification Threshold

The threshold is **competent and trustworthy** — explicitly **not** expert and **not** master.

A candidate meets the standard when they can **independently and reliably plan and deliver a *typical*
real-world activity** — safely, to a professional standard, with honest communication, correct use of
OPE and the platform, and consistent adherence to the Organizer Standard. The deciding question is:

> **"Would we trust this person with a real client's event, unsupervised?"** If yes → certify.

- **Below the line:** can't plan/deliver reliably, can't run a basic risk assessment, or doesn't uphold
  the standard → not yet certified.
- **At the line (certify):** consistently competent and trustworthy on typical activities.
- **Above the line (beyond certification):** expertise, specialties, scale, and mastery are **growth
  beyond** the credential (future specialty certifications and a Master tier — curriculum §6), not part
  of the minimum standard.

Consistency matters more than brilliance: a reliable organizer who is never spectacular passes; a
talented one who is unreliable or unsafe does not.

## 9. Relationship to the Certification Exam

The exam **validates** the standard (it does not redefine it):
- **Knowledge** components confirm understanding across the domains.
- **Practical demonstration** confirms the **Skills** (e.g., producing a plan, budget, and client
  proposal via OPE, and a risk assessment) — capability, not theory.
- **Behaviors** are evidenced through practical work and acceptance of the Organizer Standard.
- **Safety (Domain E) is non-negotiable:** mastery is required regardless of overall performance.

The exam confirms the candidate is **competent and trustworthy** (§7) — it does not test for perfection
or expertise.

## 10. Relationship to the Organizer Philosophy

Philosophy and competency are two halves of one credential:
- **Philosophy = the *why* and the character** — trustworthy, people-first, prevention-minded, safety
  always, trust is shared (`ACTIVLIFEHUB_ORGANIZER_PHILOSOPHY_V1.md` / Foundational F3).
- **Competency = the *what* and the capability** — the domains and requirements in this document.

They depend on each other:
- **Competency without philosophy** = a technician who *can* deliver but may cut corners — not
  trustworthy.
- **Philosophy without competency** = good intentions that fail on the day — not reliable.

A Certified Organizer is required to have **both**: someone who **can** deliver a real activity safely
and reliably (this standard) **and will** do so responsibly (the philosophy). That combination — capable
*and* trustworthy — is exactly what certification certifies.
