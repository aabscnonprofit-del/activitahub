# Verified Organizer Exam — Structure v1

**Source of truth:** ActivLife Hub Organizer Foundation v1

## What this document is

This is the **structure** of the Verified Organizer Exam — its sections, what
each one tests, why it matters, and what counts as evidence of competence. It is
not exam questions, lessons, certification rules, scores, pricing, or UI. It
defines the shape of the exam and the logic used to evaluate it.

> **Naming note:** this document keeps its original filename
> (`VERIFIED_ORGANIZER_EXAM_STRUCTURE_V1`), but the status it grants is
> **Certified Organizer**, not Verified. See "How this exam fits certification"
> below. The "Verified" in the filename is historical.

## What this exam is for

This exam exists so that an **experienced organizer can reach Certified
Organizer status without going through the ActivLife Hub training program**. It
is a test-out path to **certification**. It assumes the candidate has already
organized real events in the real world and asks them to demonstrate that
judgment directly.

Because of that, the exam tests **transferable organizer judgment** — the
thinking a good organizer brings from anywhere — not ActivLife Hub terminology
and not the ActivLife Hub platform's specific screens.

## How this exam fits certification

The Foundation defines two organizer statuses:

- **Verified Organizer** — a **trust and identity status**. It confirms the
  person is real and accountable. It is not a craft status and is not what this
  exam grants.
- **Certified Organizer** — proof of the full organizer craft (discovery,
  concept, OPE plan, execution, aftercare).

**Certified Organizer can be reached two ways:**

1. Completing ActivLife Hub training and passing certification; **or**
2. Passing **this** experienced-organizer competency exam.

This exam is route 2. It tests the craft (Foundation competencies 5–9, plus the
communication and safety competencies 3–4) so that an experienced organizer can
demonstrate the same competence the training-and-certification route proves.
Both routes lead to the same Certified status. This exam does **not** grant, set,
or alter Verified status — Verified remains a separate trust-and-identity check.

Two competencies are deliberately **out of scope** for this exam:

- **Competency 1 (Understands ActivLife Hub and acts as part of it)** — this is
  ActivLife-specific knowledge. It can't be proven by an outside organizer's
  experience; it comes from onboarding.
- **Competency 2 (Operates the platform confidently)** — this requires using the
  actual platform. No scenario exam can prove it. It is handled at onboarding,
  not here.

So passing this exam proves *transferable competence*. Platform-specific
operation and ActivLife-specific trust knowledge are still completed at
onboarding before the organizer works with real clients. The rest of this
document assumes that boundary.

## Exam sections

Each section tests one evaluation area through **real-world scenarios and
decision-making** — never definitions. The candidate is shown a realistic
situation and must decide, choose, or produce something. Wording like "what is a
budget / a participant / risk management" is explicitly disallowed.

### Section A — Understanding Client Intent

- **Competency tested:** the front half of Competency 5 — reading what a client
  actually wants underneath what they said.
- **Why it matters:** clients describe events in feelings ("a royal day,"
  "something fun for my team"). An organizer who takes those at face value plans
  the wrong event. Reading intent correctly is where the whole chain starts.
- **Evidence of competence:** given a vague, emotional request, the candidate
  correctly identifies what kind of event this really is, who it's for, and what
  the client is really after — and separates the *feeling* (keep as context)
  from the *facts* (still to be discovered). They don't collapse "unforgettable
  birthday" into a single assumed picture.

### Section B — Discovery

- **Competency tested:** Competency 5 — discovering the information that matters.
- **Why it matters:** the client is not responsible for knowing what's
  important; the organizer is. The most common real-world failure is guessing
  instead of asking.
- **Evidence of competence:** given a partial request, the candidate identifies
  exactly which required facts are missing (e.g. no headcount, no budget, no
  date) and what they would ask to get them — without interrogating, and without
  filling gaps with their own assumptions. They catch contradictions ("intimate
  dinner for 60") and flag them.

### Section C — Concept Formation

- **Competency tested:** the first half of Competency 6 — turning discovered
  facts into a concept the client would recognize and confirm.
- **Why it matters:** facts alone aren't a shared plan. The concept is the thing
  the client says "yes" to before any planning happens.
- **Evidence of competence:** given a set of client facts, the candidate
  assembles a coherent, realistic concept (who, how many, when, where, rough
  budget, the feel of it) that fits those facts, stays inside the budget, and
  doesn't quietly substitute something easier for what the client asked for.
  They show that they would confirm it before planning.

### Section D — Converting Requests into OPE-Ready Inputs

- **Competency tested:** the second half of Competency 6 — expressing the
  concept as a structured request OPE can plan from.
- **Why it matters:** OPE is only as good as the structured request it's given.
  This is the bridge between the client and the engine.
- **Evidence of competence:** given a confirmed concept, the candidate produces
  a complete, structured request — every required input present and consistent,
  situational inputs included when the event type calls for them, nothing
  invented, nothing left as a feeling. A missing or guessed required value means
  the bridge wasn't built.

### Section E — Communication Judgment

- **Competency tested:** Competency 3 — communicating professionally and
  building trust.
- **Why it matters:** most of an organizer's reputation is built in
  conversations. How they handle a tense or awkward exchange decides whether a
  problem stays small.
- **Evidence of competence:** given a realistic message or situation (an upset
  client, an unclear demand, an awkward budget conversation, a client going
  silent), the candidate chooses the response that is clear, honest, respectful,
  and de-escalating — and avoids over-promising to end discomfort. This is
  judgment about *what to do and say*, not etiquette trivia.

### Section F — Safety Awareness

- **Competency tested:** Competency 4 — the safety and responsibility baseline.
- **Why it matters:** one avoidable injury or mishandled emergency can end an
  organizer and harm the platform. Some participants are vulnerable.
- **Evidence of competence:** given an event scenario (a kids' party, a seniors'
  gathering, an outdoor event, an emergency mid-event), the candidate identifies
  the real risks, the added duties for children/seniors/outdoor settings, the
  right response when something goes wrong, and recognizes when an event should
  not run as asked. This is the section where being wrong is most serious.

### Section G — Planning Judgment

- **Competency tested:** Competency 7 — building a real, workable plan with OPE.
- **Why it matters:** OPE produces a plan, but the organizer owns the result. A
  plan that looks fine on paper can be undeliverable in reality.
- **Evidence of competence:** given a plan (or an OPE-style plan) for a
  scenario, the candidate spots what doesn't fit reality — the assumption that's
  wrong for this client, the budget that won't stretch, the piece that's
  missing — and decides the right adjustment. They demonstrate they would
  sense-check rather than forward output blindly.

### Section H — Execution Judgment

- **Competency tested:** Competency 8 — executing the plan on the day.
- **Why it matters:** a good plan still has to survive contact with a live event
  and real people. Execution is its own skill.
- **Evidence of competence:** given an on-the-day situation (something runs
  late, a supplier doesn't show, the weather turns, a guest count is wrong), the
  candidate chooses the response that keeps the event on track and the guests
  unaware of the scramble — staying in control rather than freezing or
  over-reacting.

### Section I — Post-Event Judgment

- **Competency tested:** Competency 9 — closing out and learning from the event.
- **Why it matters:** aftercare decides whether a one-time client returns and
  whether a problem gets resolved or becomes damage.
- **Evidence of competence:** given an after-the-event situation (a late
  complaint, mixed feedback, a client who's gone quiet, something that went
  wrong on the day), the candidate chooses how to close out and follow up
  fairly, handles the complaint without getting defensive, and shows what they'd
  carry into the next event.

## Evaluation logic

### Passing philosophy

A candidate passes by demonstrating **sound organizer judgment across the whole
chain**, not by scoring points in isolation. The question each section answers
is: *would this person make a good real decision here?* Passing means yes,
consistently — they read intent, discover what's missing, form a concept that
fits, build a clean structured request, communicate well, keep people safe, and
make sound calls during planning, execution, and aftercare.

Competence is judged on **practical decisions**, not vocabulary. A candidate who
can define every term but makes poor calls in scenarios has not passed. A
candidate who uses plain language but consistently makes the right calls has.

### Failure philosophy

Failure is not "got some answers wrong." Failure means the candidate's judgment
is **not yet safe or reliable for real clients** — they guess instead of
discovering, substitute their own idea for the client's, miss real risks, hand
OPE incomplete or invented inputs, or mishandle people under pressure. Failing
is a signal to go through training, not a verdict on the person. The framing is:
*not yet*, not *not good enough*.

### What should disqualify a candidate

Some failures are not "needs more practice" — they mean the candidate should not
be Verified on this route at all, regardless of how well they do elsewhere:

- **Any serious safety failure** — choosing an action that would put
  participants (especially children or seniors) at real risk, or being willing
  to run an event that clearly shouldn't run as asked. Safety is pass/fail on its
  own; strength elsewhere does not buy it back.
- **Dishonesty toward the client** — choosing to over-promise, hide a problem,
  or mislead to win or keep business.
- **Inventing facts** — repeatedly substituting assumptions for the client's
  real answers and presenting them as the client's intent.
- **Hostile or unprofessional handling of people** — responding to a difficult
  client with contempt, blame, or aggression.

These are disqualifiers because they break the platform's trust model directly,
which no amount of planning skill compensates for.

### Automatic vs. human review

The exam is evaluated in two ways, by the nature of what's being tested.

**Can be evaluated automatically** (the answer is checkable against a defined
correct set):

- **Section D — OPE-Ready Inputs:** completeness and consistency of a structured
  request can be checked field by field — every required input present, no
  contradictions, situational inputs included when the type demands them.
- **Section F — Safety Awareness:** recognizing required precautions, the right
  emergency response, and "this should not run as asked" can be tested as
  scenario decisions with defined correct answers.
- **Section B — Discovery (partly):** *identifying which required facts are
  missing* from a request is checkable. Whether the candidate would ask for them
  well is not.

**Requires human review** (the answer is judgment, not a fixed key):

- **Section A — Understanding Client Intent:** reading what a client really
  means is interpretive.
- **Section C — Concept Formation:** whether a concept genuinely fits the client
  and the budget is a judgment call.
- **Section E — Communication Judgment:** tone, honesty, and de-escalation are
  judged, not keyed.
- **Section G — Planning Judgment:** spotting the wrong assumption and choosing
  the right adjustment is reasoning, not lookup.
- **Section H — Execution Judgment** and **Section I — Post-Event Judgment:**
  both are situational judgment under pressure.
- **The disqualifiers** (especially safety and honesty) always get human review
  before they are applied to a person, even where the underlying section is
  auto-scored.

The general rule: **structure and safety can be checked by machine; judgment
about people and plans needs a human.** The auto-evaluated sections can screen
quickly; the human-reviewed sections decide whether the candidate truly has
organizer judgment.
