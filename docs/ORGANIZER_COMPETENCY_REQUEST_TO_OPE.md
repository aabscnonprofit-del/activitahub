# Organizer Competency: Converting Client Requests into OPE Inputs

## What this document is

This defines a single organizer competency — turning what a client *says* they
want into something OPE can actually plan from. It is not a lesson, an exam, a
certification rule, or a platform feature. It exists so we can agree on **what
this skill is** before we build training or tests around it.

The whole skill, in one line:

```
Client idea  →  Clarification  →  Structured request  →  OPE input  →  High-quality event plan
```

The organizer's job is to move a request left-to-right along that chain. This
is **not** selling. It is interviewing and translating.

---

## 1. Why this competency exists

Clients describe events with feelings, not facts. They say:

- "I want a great party."
- "I want an unforgettable birthday."
- "I want a royal day."
- "I want something fun for my team."
- "I want a wonderful event for my parents."

Every one of these is real and important to the person saying it. And every
one of them is useless to a planner as written. "Royal day" is not a guest
count, a budget, a date, or a place. OPE cannot plan "unforgettable."

So there has to be a step where a human takes the feeling and pulls out the
facts underneath it. That step is this competency. Without it, OPE has nothing
to work with — or worse, it works with guesses.

---

## 2. Why it is critical to ActivLife Hub

ActivLife Hub's promise is that an organizer plus the platform produces a
**good, real plan** for a real event. OPE is the engine that produces that
plan — but OPE is only as good as what it's given. Garbage in, garbage out.

That makes this competency the **gate** between the client and the engine:

- If the organizer extracts the right facts, OPE produces a plan that fits.
- If the organizer guesses or skips, OPE produces a confident plan for the
  wrong event — wrong size, wrong budget, wrong place — and the client loses
  trust in both the organizer and the platform.

Every other organizer skill — planning, execution, follow-up — depends on this
one being done right first. This is where most events are quietly won or lost,
before anything is booked.

---

## 3. What mistakes inexperienced organizers make

Real, common failures:

- **They take the words at face value.** Client says "small party," organizer
  plans for 15 — client meant 40. "Small" is a feeling, not a number.
- **They never ask the boring questions.** Date, headcount, budget, location.
  These feel awkward to ask, so beginners skip them and guess.
- **They assume.** "Birthday" → they assume a kids' party, when it's the
  client's 60-year-old mother. "Team event" → they assume 10 people and a
  restaurant, when it's 80 people and a field.
- **They let the client stay vague because vague feels nice.** "Leave it to
  you, make it amazing" is flattering and dangerous. It is not permission to
  skip the facts.
- **They mistake budget talk for rudeness** and never pin it down, so the plan
  is unaffordable and the deal dies.
- **They collect feelings instead of facts.** They come away with "she wants it
  elegant and warm" and still don't know how many people are coming.
- **They ask everything at once** like an interrogation, or ask nothing and
  hope. Both lose the client.
- **They feed OPE their own assumptions** dressed up as the client's answers.

The through-line: beginners treat the client's emotional language as the spec.
It isn't. It's the starting point.

---

## 4. What a competent organizer must be able to do

A competent organizer can:

- **Hear the feeling and keep it** — "royal day" tells you the client wants to
  feel special and impressed; that's useful context, not a plan. Don't discard
  it, but don't plan from it either.
- **Translate feeling into questions** — turn "unforgettable birthday" into:
  whose birthday, what age, how many guests, what date, what budget, where.
- **Ask the boring questions without making them feel boring** — get headcount,
  date, budget, and location naturally, as part of a normal conversation.
- **Notice what's missing** — know the short list of facts OPE needs and feel
  the gap when one is absent.
- **Resolve contradictions** — "tiny intimate dinner for 60" needs a follow-up,
  not a guess.
- **Confirm before planning** — read the facts back to the client in plain
  terms ("So: your mum's 60th, about 40 adults, mid-July, around $3,000, at
  your home — right?") and get a yes.
- **Stop when they have enough** — recognize when they have a complete,
  structured request and hand it to OPE, rather than over-interviewing.

The output of a competent organizer is not "a sense of what the client wants."
It is a **structured request** that maps cleanly onto OPE's inputs.

---

## 5. What information must be extracted before OPE can produce a useful plan

This is the practical core. OPE needs specific, structured facts. The organizer
must come away from the client able to fill in **all of the required ones** and
as many optional ones as matter. Mapped to what OPE actually consumes:

**Required — OPE cannot plan well without these:**

- **What kind of event it is** — not the mood, the *type*. A kid's birthday, an
  adult birthday, an anniversary, a graduation, a family reunion, a BBQ, a
  networking meetup, a fitness/yoga/dance class, an art class, a language
  class, a workshop. ("Royal day" must become one of these.)
- **How many people** — a real headcount, not "a few" or "small."
- **Where** — at minimum the city and country; the area it will happen in.

**Strongly needed — these shape the plan heavily:**

- **Adults vs. kids** — the split, not just the total. 40 adults and 40
  children are completely different events.
- **Budget** — a real number or a real range. The single most-skipped fact and
  one of the most important.
- **Venue type** — is it at a home/backyard, or a public park? (More broadly:
  where physically does this happen.)
- **Date / timing** — when, and whether it's a one-off or repeats.

**Situational — needed when the event type calls for it:**

- **Special requirements** — dietary needs, accessibility, a theme that drives
  real logistics, anything the client insists on.
- **For class-type events specifically:** is there already an instructor or is
  one needed; are materials provided or do guests bring their own.
- **Recurrence** — for anything ongoing (a weekly class, a monthly meetup):
  how often.

**The translation, shown on a real example:**

| Client says | Organizer must extract |
|---|---|
| "I want a great party" | type (which kind of party), headcount, adults/kids split, date, budget, location, venue |
| "Unforgettable birthday" | whose birthday + age (→ kids' birthday or adult birthday), guest count, date, budget, city, home or park |
| "Something fun for my team" | networking/meetup or workshop?, number of people, date, budget, location, any dietary/access needs |
| "A wonderful event for my parents" | occasion (anniversary? milestone birthday?), guest count, mostly seniors?, date, budget, accessibility needs, venue |
| "A royal day" | what's the actual occasion and type, how many guests, budget, date, place — keep "feel special" as context only |

If the organizer can fill the required rows with real values and has handled the
situational ones the event type demands, OPE has what it needs. If any required
value is still a feeling or a guess, the competency hasn't been performed yet.

---

## 6. What evidence would prove competence

We don't define the test here, but we name what we'd have to *see* to believe an
organizer has this skill:

- **Given a vague request, they end up with a complete structured request.**
  Hand them "I want a royal day for my daughter," and they come back with event
  type, headcount, adults/kids, date, budget, and location — all real values.
- **They got those facts by asking, not assuming.** The questions they asked
  are visible and reasonable, and the answers came from the client.
- **They caught the missing fact.** Given a request that conveniently leaves out
  budget or headcount, a competent organizer notices and asks, instead of
  filling it in themselves.
- **They didn't over-translate.** They kept the client's intent intact and
  didn't quietly swap "her 60th at home" for "a venue hall" because it was
  easier.
- **They confirmed.** There's a moment where they read the facts back and the
  client agreed before anything was planned.
- **The OPE plan that results actually fits the client's real event** — right
  size, right budget band, right setting — and the client recognizes it as
  theirs.

In short: vague input in, structured-and-confirmed request out, and the plan
OPE produces from it matches the real event. That's the proof.

## 7. Where this competency belongs in the Organizer Learning Structure

This belongs at the **start of Section 5 — Event Planning Fundamentals**, and it
is the **bridge** from the platform skills into the planning skills.

The reason it sits there, and not earlier:

- It depends on **Section 2 (Using the Platform)** — specifically on the
  organizer already understanding requests, OPE, and how a request becomes a
  plan on the platform. You can't translate *into* OPE inputs if you don't yet
  know what OPE is.
- It depends on **Section 3 (Client Communication)** — the facts get extracted
  through a conversation. The organizer needs the communication skills first,
  then points them at a specific job: pulling out the planning facts.
- It is the **first real act of planning**. Everything else in Section 5 —
  building the actual plan — assumes you already have a clean, structured
  request to plan from. This competency is how you get that.

So the order is: understand the platform → learn to communicate → then, as the
opening competency of planning, learn to convert a client's request into OPE
inputs. It is the hinge the whole planning section turns on.

---

## The point of all this

Before we write a single lesson or test question, this is the skill we're
training: take "I want something wonderful for my parents" and turn it into a
real, structured, confirmed request that OPE can plan — without losing what made
it matter to the client, and without inventing facts the client never gave.
