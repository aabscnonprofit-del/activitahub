# Certified Organizer Training Structure v1

The complete training path for a person with **no prior event-organizing
experience** — the training route to Certified Organizer. (The other route, for
people who already organize real events, is the test-out exam in
VERIFIED_ORGANIZER_EXAM_STRUCTURE_V1; both reach the same Certified status.)

This document defines the **module structure and learning progression only** — no
lessons, lesson content, quizzes, exam questions, or certification rules.

## Sources and what they each give this structure

- **ACTIVLIFE_HUB_ORGANIZER_FOUNDATION_V1** — the roles, the principles, and the
  learning order this sequence follows.
- **ACTIVLIFE_HUB_ORGANIZER_COMPETENCIES_V1** — the nine competencies each module
  builds toward (referenced as C1–C9 below).
- **VERIFIED_ORGANIZER_EXAM_STRUCTURE_V1** — what competence has to look like at
  the end, so training aims at the same bar certification tests.
- **CERTIFIED_ORGANIZER_EXAM_CASES_V2** — the kinds of real situations a trained
  organizer must be able to handle; used to ground each module's learning
  outcomes in real judgment, not theory.

## The principles the whole path is built on

- The client brings a **dream**, not a plan.
- The organizer helps turn it into a **concept**.
- OPE helps turn the concept into a **plan**.
- The organizer **executes** the plan.
- The client is **not** responsible for knowing what information matters.
- The organizer **is** responsible for discovering it.

The modules below walk a beginner along exactly that chain: first understand the
platform and the engine, then learn to operate them, then learn to handle people
safely, and only then learn to take a dream all the way to a delivered event.

## The nine competencies (reference)

- **C1** Understands ActivLife Hub and acts as part of it
- **C2** Operates the platform confidently
- **C3** Communicates professionally and builds trust
- **C4** Meets the safety and responsibility baseline
- **C5** Discovers the information that matters
- **C6** Turns a dream into a concept and a structured request
- **C7** Builds a real, workable plan with OPE
- **C8** Executes the plan on the day
- **C9** Closes out and learns from the event

## Module 1 — Understanding ActivLife Hub

1. **Name:** Understanding ActivLife Hub
2. **Purpose:** Give the beginner the ground everything else stands on — what the
   platform is for, who it serves, and what an organizer is responsible for.
3. **Competencies covered:** C1.
4. **Learning outcomes:** The learner can explain the platform's mission, the
   roles of clients/participants/organizers, the trust model, and what would
   break it; and can describe the dream → concept → plan → execution chain and
   where the organizer owns the work — including discovering what the client
   never thought to say.
5. **Prerequisites:** None (entry module).
6. **Why this position:** Nothing else makes sense without it. A learner who
   doesn't understand the platform or their responsibility inside it will
   misread every later module.

## Module 2 — Understanding OPE

1. **Name:** Understanding OPE
2. **Purpose:** Explain what OPE is, what it does, and — just as important — what
   it does *not* do, before the learner ever touches it.
3. **Competencies covered:** Foundational for C6 and C7 (no competency completed
   here; this is the conceptual groundwork for them).
4. **Learning outcomes:** The learner can describe OPE's role (it turns a
   structured request into a workable plan), what it needs to work well, and the
   garbage-in/garbage-out reality — that OPE is only as good as the request it's
   given, and that the organizer, not OPE, talks to the client and owns the
   result.
5. **Prerequisites:** Module 1.
6. **Why this position:** OPE is central to how the platform creates plans.
   Understanding its role early frames everything that follows — platform
   operation, discovery, concept, and planning all point toward feeding and
   using it well. It comes before hands-on platform work so the learner knows
   what the engine is for before learning to drive it.

## Module 3 — Operating the Platform

1. **Name:** Operating the Platform
2. **Purpose:** Make the beginner fluent and confident in the actual platform, so
   it's a tool they trust rather than an obstacle.
3. **Competencies covered:** C2.
4. **Learning outcomes:** The learner can move through every working area —
   dashboard, activities, requests, proposals, bookings, participants,
   messaging, calendar, OPE, and sourcing tools — and carry a single piece of
   business from first contact to a confirmed booking using the platform alone,
   without workarounds.
5. **Prerequisites:** Modules 1–2.
6. **Why this position:** All later real work happens through the platform. The
   learner must be able to operate it before they practise communication,
   discovery, planning, or execution inside it. It follows Understanding OPE so
   they recognise the engine when they meet it on screen.

## Module 4 — Client Communication

1. **Name:** Client Communication
2. **Purpose:** Teach the beginner to be clear, reliable, and trustworthy in
   every exchange — the foundation of how interest becomes a booking and how
   problems stay small.
3. **Competencies covered:** C3.
4. **Learning outcomes:** The learner can hold a respectful, clear conversation,
   set honest expectations, de-escalate a tense or difficult exchange, hold a
   boundary warmly, and communicate to a consistent standard — never
   over-promising to end discomfort. (Grounded in the kinds of situations in the
   exam's communication cases.)
5. **Prerequisites:** Modules 1–3.
6. **Why this position:** Communication is the vehicle for everything that
   follows. Discovery, concept-forming, and aftercare all happen *through*
   conversation, so the learner needs this before pointing it at a specific job.

## Module 5 — Safety and Responsibility

1. **Name:** Safety and Responsibility
2. **Purpose:** Make sure the beginner knows their duty of care and their limits
   before they shape or design any event.
3. **Competencies covered:** C4.
4. **Learning outcomes:** The learner can identify the everyday risks in an
   activity, the added duties for children, seniors, and outdoor settings, the
   right response in an emergency, and what they're liable for — and will refuse
   or change an event that can't be run safely, including when a client pressures
   them to cut a safety or access corner.
5. **Prerequisites:** Modules 1–4.
6. **Why this position:** Per the Foundation, an organizer must know their duties
   *before* designing anything that puts people at risk. Safety sits ahead of
   discovery, concept, and planning so it informs every choice made in them — a
   learner should recognise an unsafe concept while forming it, not after.

## Module 6 — Discovery and Information Gathering

1. **Name:** Discovery and Information Gathering
2. **Purpose:** Teach the beginner to pull the facts that matter out of a vague,
   emotional request — the skill that makes "the organizer discovers what
   matters" real.
3. **Competencies covered:** C5.
4. **Learning outcomes:** The learner can hear an emotional brief ("a royal day,"
   "something fun for my team"), keep the feeling as context, and draw out the
   facts beneath it — type, headcount, who's attending, date, budget, location,
   venue, and any special, cultural, accessibility, or inclusion needs the client
   never thought to mention. They notice missing facts, question numbers that
   sound precise, and surface contradictions instead of guessing.
5. **Prerequisites:** Modules 1–5.
6. **Why this position:** Discovery is the first real act of taking on a client's
   event. It needs communication (Module 4) as its method and safety awareness
   (Module 5) so the learner knows which risk-related facts to chase.

## Module 7 — Concept Formation

1. **Name:** Concept Formation
2. **Purpose:** Teach the beginner to turn discovered facts into a concept the
   client recognises and agrees to.
3. **Competencies covered:** C6 (first half — the concept).
4. **Learning outcomes:** The learner can assemble discovered facts into a
   coherent, realistic concept that fits the budget and the client's intent, hold
   competing needs together (e.g. children and seniors at one event), keep the
   concept loyal to what the client wanted rather than what's easiest, and
   confirm it with the client before any planning begins.
5. **Prerequisites:** Modules 1–6.
6. **Why this position:** A concept can only be formed once the facts are
   discovered (Module 6) and the budget and safety realities are understood
   (Module 5). It's the bridge between understanding the client and planning the
   event.

## Module 8 — Converting Concepts into OPE-Ready Requests

1. **Name:** Converting Concepts into OPE-Ready Requests
2. **Purpose:** Teach the beginner to express a confirmed concept as a complete,
   structured request OPE can plan from — without inventing anything.
3. **Competencies covered:** C6 (second half — the structured request).
4. **Learning outcomes:** The learner can turn a confirmed concept into a
   complete request with every needed fact present and consistent, recognise when
   something essential is still missing and go get it, and never fabricate a value
   to fill a gap. The focus is the real-world judgment of "do I have what I need
   to plan this well," not memorising any form or field.
5. **Prerequisites:** Modules 2 and 7 (the OPE understanding from Module 2 plus a
   confirmed concept from Module 7).
6. **Why this position:** This is the hinge between the client and the engine. It
   needs the concept already formed and confirmed, and the understanding of what
   OPE needs built back in Module 2.

## Module 9 — Event Planning with OPE

1. **Name:** Event Planning with OPE
2. **Purpose:** Teach the beginner to produce a real, workable plan with OPE and
   to own the result rather than forwarding it blindly.
3. **Competencies covered:** C7.
4. **Learning outcomes:** The learner can use OPE to generate a plan from a
   structured request, sense-check it against reality (staffing that scales, a
   budget that reconciles, a format that actually fits *this* client), spot what
   doesn't fit, and adjust it into something genuinely deliverable.
5. **Prerequisites:** Modules 1–8.
6. **Why this position:** Planning only works once there's a complete structured
   request (Module 8) to plan from, and the safety judgment (Module 5) to check
   the plan against.

## Module 10 — Event Execution

1. **Name:** Event Execution
2. **Purpose:** Teach the beginner to carry out the plan on the day and stay in
   control when reality doesn't cooperate.
3. **Competencies covered:** C8.
4. **Learning outcomes:** The learner can prepare in the final stretch, run the
   event on the day, manage participants, staff, and timing, and handle the
   things that go wrong live — a no-show supplier, a weather turn, a doubled
   headcount, a disruptive guest, a last-minute change — without losing the room.
5. **Prerequisites:** Modules 1–9.
6. **Why this position:** You can only execute a plan you've learned to build.
   Execution is a distinct skill from planning and naturally follows it.

## Module 11 — Post-Event Management

1. **Name:** Post-Event Management
2. **Purpose:** Teach the beginner to close an event well and turn it into a
   returning client and a better next event.
3. **Competencies covered:** C9.
4. **Learning outcomes:** The learner can close out an event cleanly, follow up
   with clients and participants, handle feedback, complaints, payment disputes,
   and the aftermath of an incident fairly and without getting defensive, and
   carry the lessons into the next event.
5. **Prerequisites:** Modules 1–10.
6. **Why this position:** Aftercare comes last because it acts on a completed
   event — but it closes the loop and feeds the next one, so it's part of the
   full cycle, not an afterthought.

## Module 12 — Integrated Practice: Dream to Delivered Event

1. **Name:** Integrated Practice: Dream to Delivered Event
2. **Purpose:** Bring every competency together by carrying one event through the
   whole chain — the way real work happens, not module by module.
3. **Competencies covered:** C1–C9 in combination.
4. **Learning outcomes:** The learner can take a single vague client dream and
   move it end to end — discover, form and confirm a concept, build the
   structured request, plan it with OPE, execute it, and close it out — handling
   the people, safety, and judgment calls that arise along the way, and producing
   a client who'd come back.
5. **Prerequisites:** Modules 1–11.
6. **Why this position:** The earlier modules teach the competencies one at a
   time; real events demand them woven together under pressure. This capstone is
   where separate skills become one capable organizer, and where the learner is
   ready for certification. (It defines no certification rules.)

## Learning progression at a glance

| # | Module | Competency | Stage of the chain |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | Understanding ActivLife Hub | C1 | Foundation |
| 2 | Understanding OPE | (groundwork for C6/C7) | Foundation |
| 3 | Operating the Platform | C2 | Foundation |
| 4 | Client Communication | C3 | People |
| 5 | Safety and Responsibility | C4 | Duty |
| 6 | Discovery and Information Gathering | C5 | Dream → facts |
| 7 | Concept Formation | C6 (concept) | Facts → concept |
| 8 | Converting Concepts into OPE-Ready Requests | C6 (request) | Concept → request |
| 9 | Event Planning with OPE | C7 | Request → plan |
| 10 | Event Execution | C8 | Plan → event |
| 11 | Post-Event Management | C9 | Event → next event |
| 12 | Integrated Practice | C1–C9 | Whole chain |

**The shape of the path:** understand the platform and the engine (1–3), learn
to handle people and your duty of care (4–5), then learn to take a dream all the
way to a delivered event and beyond (6–11), and finally put it all together (12).
Each module assumes the ones before it. The order is fixed — a later module
relies on the competence built earlier.

This is the training route to Certified Organizer. Lessons, quizzes, exam
questions, and certification rules are defined elsewhere, on top of this
structure, and must not contradict it or the Foundation.
