# Module 2 — Understanding OPE (Organizer Planning Engine)

*Training content. For a person with no event-organizing experience.*

In Module 1 you met the four roles, and one of them was **OPE — the Organizer
Planning Engine** — the tool that helps you turn a concept into a plan. This
module is all about that tool: what it is, what it can and can't do, and how to
work with it well.

A quick promise up front: this is **not** technical training. You don't need to
know anything about software, and we won't explain how OPE works on the inside.
You'll learn OPE the way a good driver learns a car — not by studying the engine,
but by knowing what it does, what it needs from you, and when to trust it. Read
the lessons in order; each builds on the one before.

**What this module builds toward:** the planning skills you'll use later
(turning a concept into a request, and building a real plan). For now, the goal is
simpler — to understand OPE as a working tool so that when you start using it, you
already know how to get the best out of it and where your own judgment still
matters.

---

## Lesson 2.1 — What OPE Is and What It Does

**Learning objective:** Explain, in plain terms, what OPE is and what it produces
for you.

**Lesson content**

OPE — the Organizer Planning Engine — is the planning tool built into ActivLife
Hub. Think of it as a very knowledgeable planning assistant that never gets
tired. You describe an event to it clearly, and it gives you back a real,
organized plan for that event.

What does that plan include? The practical bones of an event:

- **People** — roughly how many staff or helpers the event needs, and what kind.
- **Resources** — the things the event needs: tables, chairs, food, equipment,
  and so on.
- **Timing** — how the event is shaped across the day.
- **Budget** — what it's likely to cost, broken down so you can see where the
  money goes.

The value of this is enormous, especially when you're starting out. Working all
of that out by hand — for every event, every time — is exactly the part beginners
find hardest and get wrong. OPE does that heavy lifting quickly and consistently,
so you can focus on the parts only a person can do: understanding the client,
getting the details right, and running a great event.

So in one sentence: **OPE takes a clear description of an event and turns it into
an organized plan of people, resources, timing, and budget.** That's its job, and
it's good at it.

**Real examples**

- You describe a child's birthday — say, 15 kids and 10 adults, at a backyard,
  an afternoon, with a modest budget. OPE returns a plan: how many helpers to keep
  an eye on the children, the tables and chairs and food quantities, a rough
  schedule for the afternoon, and a cost breakdown.
- You describe a relaxed 70th birthday lunch for 30 mostly older guests. OPE
  gives you a plan scaled to that: seating for everyone (older guests won't want
  to stand), gentler timing, the catering, and the budget.

**Common mistakes**

- *Imagining OPE is something mysterious or technical.* It isn't — it's a
  planning assistant. You give it a clear description; it gives you a plan.
- *Expecting OPE to invent the event for you.* It plans the event you describe.
  The describing is your job.

**Knowledge check**

1. In one sentence, what does OPE do?
   *(It takes a clear description of an event and turns it into an organized plan of people, resources, timing, and budget.)*
2. Name two of the four things an OPE plan covers.
   *(Any two of: people, resources, timing, budget.)*

## Lesson 2.2 — What OPE Does Not Do

**Learning objective:** Identify the things OPE cannot do, so you never expect the
tool to do your job.

**Lesson content**

Knowing what a tool *can't* do is just as important as knowing what it can. OPE is
powerful, but it has clear limits — and every one of those limits is a place where
*you* are needed.

Here's what OPE does **not** do:

- **It doesn't talk to the client.** OPE never has a conversation. It can't ask a
  question, hear a worry, or pick up on a hint. Every word the client says, and
  every word they hear back, goes through you.
- **It doesn't discover anything.** OPE doesn't find out the guest count, the
  budget, or that a child has an allergy. It only knows what you tell it.
- **It doesn't know your specific people.** OPE plans for the event you describe.
  It doesn't know that one guest uses a wheelchair, or that Grandma tires easily,
  unless you've found that out and included it.
- **It doesn't make the human judgment calls.** Whether an event is safe to run,
  whether a plan actually fits this particular client, whether something feels
  off — those are yours.
- **It doesn't run the event.** On the day, OPE isn't there. You are.

None of this is a weakness in OPE — it's simply the line between what a planning
tool does and what an organizer does. OPE plans. You understand people, discover
the truth, judge what's right, and deliver. The tool handles the structure; you
handle everything human.

**Real examples**

- A client casually mentions a guest "in a wheelchair." OPE will never ask about
  step-free access or an accessible restroom — it can't. If the venue has stairs,
  only *you* catching that prevents a guest from being excluded.
- A parent doesn't mention their child's severe nut allergy. OPE won't ask, and
  its plan won't account for it. The safety of that child depends on you
  discovering the allergy, not on the tool.
- A client asks for a lakeside party for kids with swimming. OPE can plan the
  party — but it won't tell you that unsupervised children near water is unsafe.
  That judgment is yours.

**Common mistakes**

- *Assuming the plan is complete because OPE produced it.* OPE's plan only
  reflects what it was told. If you left something out, the plan leaves it out
  too.
- *Expecting OPE to flag a safety problem.* It plans what you describe; it doesn't
  judge whether the event is wise or safe. That's your job.

**Knowledge check**

1. Name two things OPE does not do.
   *(Any two of: talk to the client, discover information, know your specific people, make judgment calls, run the event.)*
2. A client never mentions a guest's allergy. Will OPE's plan account for it? Why
   or why not? *(No — OPE only knows what it's told, and it can't discover or ask. Catching the allergy is the organizer's job.)*

## Lesson 2.3 — Why OPE Needs Information (and Why Better Inputs Make Better Plans)

**Learning objective:** Explain why OPE depends on the information you give it, and
why your discovery work directly decides the quality of the plan.

**Lesson content**

In Module 1 you learned the phrase *garbage in, garbage out.* This lesson is where
you really understand why it's true — and why your discovery work matters so much.

OPE builds its plan entirely from the description you give it. It has no other
source of information. So the description is everything. If your description is
clear, true, and complete, OPE's plan will fit the real event. If your description
is vague, guessed, or missing pieces, OPE will still produce a confident-looking
plan — but it will be a plan for the wrong event.

Think about how much the right details change a plan:

- **Numbers change everything.** A plan for 20 guests and a plan for 80 guests are
  completely different — different staff, food, seating, and cost. If you guess
  "about 30" when it's really 60, every number in the plan is wrong.
- **Who's attending changes everything.** "15 adults" and "15 young children"
  need very different plans — children need more supervision and a different kind
  of event. Seniors need seating and gentler timing. The same headcount, very
  different plans.
- **Special needs change everything.** An allergy, a wheelchair user, a guest who
  needs a quiet space — each of these changes what the plan must include. OPE can
  only build them in if you've discovered them and told it.

This is exactly why **you must still do discovery**, even with a powerful planning
tool. OPE doesn't reduce the need to find out what matters — it *increases* the
value of doing it well. Every good question you ask the client becomes a better
input, and every better input becomes a better plan. The discovery you do is what
turns OPE from a generic planner into a planner for *this* event.

Put simply: OPE doesn't make discovery less important. It makes your discovery the
thing that decides whether the plan is any good.

**Real examples**

- A client says "a birthday party, maybe 30 people." You don't stop there. You ask
  and learn it's actually 18 adults and 22 children — 40 people, mostly kids.
  Because you discovered that, OPE plans the right supervision, food, and setup.
  Had you entered "30," the plan would have been wrong in every direction.
- A client booking a family lunch mentions in passing that "a couple of the
  older folks don't get around easily." You ask more and learn two guests use
  walkers. You include it, and OPE's plan reflects seating and easy access. The
  detail came from your question, not from the tool.
- A client requests a kids' party and says nothing about food restrictions. You
  ask anyway, and learn one child is severely allergic to nuts. Now that fact is
  in the plan. Without your question, neither you nor OPE would have known.

**Common mistakes**

- *Feeding OPE a guess to "get a plan quickly."* A fast plan built on a guess is
  just a fast wrong plan.
- *Thinking the tool will make up for thin information.* It can't. Thin
  information gives a thin, wrong plan.
- *Treating discovery as optional now that a tool does the planning.* It's the
  opposite — discovery is what makes the tool worth using.

**Knowledge check**

1. Why does OPE depend so heavily on the information you give it?
   *(Because it builds its plan entirely from your description — it has no other source of information.)*
2. A client says "around 30 guests" but it's really 60. What happens to the plan?
   *(It's built for the wrong size — staff, food, seating, and cost all come out wrong.)*
3. Does using OPE make discovery less important? *(No — it makes good discovery the thing that decides whether the plan is any good.)*

## Lesson 2.4 — When to Trust OPE and When to Question It

**Learning objective:** Judge when to rely on an OPE plan and when to stop and
question it, instead of accepting every plan automatically.

**Lesson content**

OPE is a tool you'll come to rely on — but relying on it well means knowing when
to trust its plan and when to look twice. A good organizer never forwards a plan
without a glance, and never ignores a plan just to do it their own way. The skill
is judgment.

**When you can trust OPE:**

- When you gave it **complete, true information.** A plan built on good inputs is
  usually a good plan.
- For the **routine structure** of an event — sensible quantities, a reasonable
  schedule, a cost breakdown for a normal event. This is exactly what OPE is good
  at, and it's faster and more consistent than doing it by hand.

**When you should question OPE:**

- When something in the plan **doesn't match reality you know.** If you know the
  venue, the client, or the situation and the plan feels off, trust your knowledge
  and check it.
- When the plan was built on **information you're not sure of.** If you guessed a
  number to get started, treat the plan as a draft, not a final answer.
- When the event has a **special factor** — a vulnerable group, a safety risk, an
  access need, an allergy. OPE plans the structure; it doesn't judge whether the
  structure is *safe* or *right* for these particular people. That judgment is
  always yours.
- When the numbers **don't add up to you.** If the budget seems impossible or the
  staffing looks thin for the crowd, stop and reason it through.

The way to hold this: OPE's plan is a strong first draft, not a verdict. Trust it
for the heavy lifting, and bring your own judgment to whether it truly fits the
real event and the real people in it. That's why OPE doesn't replace your
judgment — it gives you something good to apply your judgment *to*.

**Real examples**

- You enter a 120-guest event and the plan lists the same handful of helpers you'd
  expect for 40 guests. You question it — that staffing won't cover the crowd —
  and adjust before it goes out.
- The plan for a children's party looks neat, but you know this particular
  backyard is small and near an unfenced pond. OPE can't see that. You question
  whether the event is safe as planned and change it.
- You get a tidy plan for a seniors' afternoon, but it schedules the main activity
  in the midday heat. You know that's hard on older guests, so you question the
  timing and move it earlier — something the plan alone wouldn't flag.
- The plan looks fine and you gave it solid, complete information for a standard
  adult gathering. Here, you can trust it and move on. Questioning everything for
  the sake of it wastes the tool's value.

**Common mistakes**

- *Accepting every plan because the tool produced it.* The tool is only as right
  as your inputs and can't judge safety or fit.
- *Distrusting every plan and redoing it by hand.* That throws away exactly what
  OPE is good for. Trust it where it's strong; question it where your judgment is
  needed.
- *Not knowing the difference.* The whole skill is telling "trust it" from
  "question it" — and that comes from your own knowledge of the event and the
  people.

**Knowledge check**

1. Give one situation where you can reasonably trust an OPE plan.
   *(When you gave it complete, true information and it's a routine event structure.)*
2. Give one situation where you should question an OPE plan.
   *(When it conflicts with what you know, was built on a guess, involves a safety/access/vulnerable factor, or the numbers don't add up.)*
3. Why doesn't OPE replace your judgment?
   *(Because it plans the structure but can't judge whether that structure is safe or right for these specific people — that's the organizer's job.)*

## Lesson 2.5 — How You and OPE Work Together

**Learning objective:** Describe how an organizer and OPE work as a team, each
doing the part they're best at.

**Lesson content**

By now the picture should be coming together. OPE isn't your replacement and it
isn't your boss — it's your planning partner. You each do the part you're best at,
and the event is better because of both.

Here's the teamwork, start to finish:

1. **You** talk to the client, listen, and discover what really matters — the
   numbers, the people, the budget, the special needs.
2. **You** shape that into a clear, true, complete description of the event.
3. **OPE** takes that description and builds the plan — people, resources, timing,
   budget — quickly and consistently.
4. **You** read the plan with judgment: does it fit reality? Is it safe and right
   for these people? You adjust what needs adjusting.
5. **You** run the event on the day, with a solid plan behind you.

Notice the pattern: the human parts — understanding, discovering, judging,
delivering — are all yours. The structured planning in the middle is OPE's. You
bring the people skills and the judgment; OPE brings speed and consistency. Used
this way, OPE makes you faster and more reliable without ever taking over the
parts that need a person.

That's the whole relationship, and it's worth remembering as one idea: **good
discovery in, good plan out, good judgment over the top.** Do your part well and
OPE becomes one of the most useful tools you have. Skip your part, and no tool can
save the event.

**Real examples**

- A client arrives with "a fun day for my daughter and her friends." You discover
  it's eight 6-year-olds, a small budget, a weekday afternoon, one child with a
  dairy allergy. You describe that clearly; OPE plans the party; you check that
  the supervision is right for small children and the food avoids dairy; you run
  it. Each of you did your part.
- A client wants a reunion lunch for an extended family, young and old together.
  You discover the mix — toddlers and grandparents in one room. You describe it;
  OPE plans the catering and setup; you apply judgment to keep the little ones
  safe and the older guests comfortable, things the plan alone wouldn't balance.
  The result works because you and OPE each contributed.

**Common mistakes**

- *Leaning on OPE for the parts that are yours* — discovery, judgment, running the
  day. The tool can't carry those.
- *Doing everything yourself and ignoring OPE* — that's slower and less reliable,
  and wastes a tool built to help you.
- *Forgetting that your part comes first.* The plan can only be as good as the
  discovery and description you bring to it.

**Knowledge check**

1. In the teamwork between you and OPE, which parts are yours and which is OPE's?
   *(Yours: talking to the client, discovering, describing the event, judging the plan, running the day. OPE's: building the structured plan from your description.)*
2. Complete the idea: "Good discovery in, good plan out, ___."
   *(Good judgment over the top.)*

## Module 2 wrap-up

You now understand OPE as a working tool:

- **What OPE is** — the Organizer Planning Engine, a planning assistant built into
  the platform.
- **What it does** — turns a clear description of an event into an organized plan
  of people, resources, timing, and budget.
- **What it does not do** — it doesn't talk to the client, discover information,
  know your specific people, make judgment calls, or run the event.
- **Why it needs information** — it builds its plan entirely from what you give it,
  so better inputs make better plans, and your discovery is what decides the
  quality.
- **When to trust it and when to question it** — trust it for routine structure
  built on good inputs; question it when it conflicts with what you know, rests on
  a guess, or involves safety, access, or vulnerable people.
- **How you work together** — you discover, describe, judge, and deliver; OPE
  builds the plan in the middle. Good discovery in, good plan out, good judgment
  over the top.

The one sentence to carry forward: **OPE is a powerful planning partner, but it
only ever plans what you give it — your discovery and your judgment are what make
the plan real.**
