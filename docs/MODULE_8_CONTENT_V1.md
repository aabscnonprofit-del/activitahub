# Module 8 — Converting Concepts into OPE-Ready Requests

*Training content. For a person with no event-organizing experience.*

In Module 7 you learned to turn discovery into a confirmed concept — the agreed
vision of the event. In this module you learn to turn that concept into a
**request**: the clear, complete, organized description that OPE can actually plan
from. This is the bridge between the client and the planning engine, and getting
it right is what makes OPE's plan fit the real event.

Hold these principles the whole way through — they're the soul of this module:

- **OPE can only plan from what it understands.** Remember "garbage in, garbage
  out" from Module 2. The request *is* the input.
- **A good concept is not automatically a good request.** A beautiful vision in
  your head is useless to OPE until it's expressed clearly and completely.
- **A request must preserve the intent of the concept.** If the meaning gets lost
  in translation, OPE plans a different event than the one you agreed.
- **Structure exists to help planning, not to satisfy a form.** You're not filling
  in boxes — you're giving OPE a true, complete picture.
- **Think about meaning, not field completion.** The question is never "did I fill
  everything in?" It's "does this give OPE everything it needs to plan *this*
  event well?"

That last point matters so much that this whole module is built on it. You will
**not** learn to memorize platform fields or fill in forms by rote. You'll learn
to think about *what information an event genuinely needs to be planned well* — and
make sure your request carries all of it, clearly and truthfully.

**What this module builds toward:** Competency 6 — *Turns a dream into a concept
and a structured request.* Module 7 was the concept; this is the request. Read the
lessons in order.

## Lesson 8.1 — Concept vs Request: What Changes

**Learning objective:** Explain the difference between a concept and a request, and
why a concept must be translated before planning.

**Lesson content**

A **concept** and a **request** describe the same event, but they're aimed at
different audiences and they do different jobs.

A **concept** is the vision — the shape and feel of the event, expressed for *the
client* so they can see it and agree to it. "A relaxed summer garden gathering for
40, warm and unfussy, where mum is the heart of it." It's emotional, it's a
picture, and it's perfect for getting a "yes" from a person.

A **request** is that same event expressed for *planning* — clear, complete, and
organized so OPE can turn it into a real plan. It's not emotional; it's
informational. It carries everything OPE needs to know: how many people and who
they are, the budget, the place, the timing, the participant needs, the safety
requirements — all stated plainly.

Here's why you can't skip the translation: a concept is built to be *agreed to*, not
to be *planned from*. "Warm and unfussy, where mum is the heart of it" tells a client
exactly what their event will feel like — and tells OPE almost nothing it can plan
with. OPE can't plan a feeling. It needs the feeling *translated* into the concrete
information that produces it. That translation is this module's whole job.

So the move is: take the confirmed concept, and re-express it as a request that holds
all the information planning needs — without losing what the concept was *for.* Same
event, different form, aimed at a different reader.

**Real examples**

- *Concept (for the client):* "A cosy 80th birthday tea — close family, gentle
  afternoon, everyone seated and relaxed, where Grandma feels surrounded by love."
  *Request (for planning):* about 20 guests, mostly older adults, mid-afternoon, a
  comfortable seated indoor space with easy access, modest budget, one guest uses a
  walker, gentle pace. Same event — but now it's something OPE can plan.
- *Two organizers, same concept:* Given the identical confirmed concept, two
  organizers might write slightly different requests — one notes the weather backup
  explicitly, the other phrases the timing differently. Both can be good, *as long as
  both carry all the information planning needs.* The variation is fine; missing
  information is not.

**Common mistakes**

- *Trying to plan straight from the concept,* feeding OPE a feeling it can't use.
- *Thinking the concept and request are the same thing in different words* — they
  serve different jobs for different readers.

**Knowledge check**

1. Who is a concept written for, and who is a request written for?
   *(A concept is written for the client to agree to; a request is written for planning, so OPE can plan from it.)*
2. Why can't OPE plan directly from a concept?
   *(A concept describes the feeling and vision; OPE needs that translated into the concrete information that produces it.)*

## Lesson 8.2 — Preserving Intent

**Learning objective:** Translate a concept into a request without losing the
meaning, feeling, or important findings behind it.

**Lesson content**

The biggest danger in translation is **losing the intent.** When you turn a rich
concept into an organized request, it's frighteningly easy to drop the very things
that made the event what it was — and end up with a request that's technically
about the same event but has lost its soul or, worse, lost a crucial fact.

Intent gets lost in two ways:

**The feeling gets flattened.** A concept of "an intimate, unhurried gathering where
the family finally has time to actually talk" can become a request that just says
"dinner for 12" — losing the *intimate* and *unhurried* that were the whole point.
OPE will then plan a perfectly ordinary dinner, not the one you agreed. Preserving
intent means carrying the *meaning* across: the request must still communicate that
this is unhurried, intimate, conversation-centred — because those shape the plan.

**The discovered needs get dropped.** This is the dangerous one. You discovered, back
in Module 6, that Grandma uses a walker, that a child has a nut allergy, that two
guests need a quiet space. If those don't make it into the request, they vanish — OPE
never knew them, and the plan won't include them. Every important thing you
discovered has to survive the translation and land in the request.

The test for preserving intent is simple: **after you've written the request, would
it still produce the event you and the client agreed to — including the things that
matter most and the needs you discovered?** If something important wouldn't survive,
you've lost intent, and you fix it before planning.

This is why a *strong concept can still produce a weak request*: the concept was
right, but the translation dropped what mattered.

**Real examples (a strong concept producing a weak request)**

- *Lost feeling:* A lovely concept — "a warm, relaxed celebration where nobody feels
  rushed" — gets translated into a bare request: "party, 30 people, 3 hours, $2,000."
  The intent is gone. OPE plans a generic, possibly tightly-scheduled party — the
  opposite of "relaxed and unrushed." The concept was strong; the request betrayed it.
- *Lost discovery:* The concept and discovery clearly included a child's severe nut
  allergy and a guest using a wheelchair. The request that goes to planning mentions
  neither. The plan comes back with no allergy control and a venue with stairs — both
  potentially serious — because the findings didn't survive translation.

**Common mistakes**

- *Flattening the feeling* into bare logistics, so the planned event loses its
  character.
- *Dropping discovered needs* — allergies, access, quiet spaces — in the move from
  concept to request.
- *Assuming OPE "knows" what you meant.* It only knows what the request says.

**Knowledge check**

1. What's the test for whether you've preserved intent?
   *(Would the request still produce the agreed event, including what matters most and the needs you discovered?)*
2. Why can a strong concept still produce a weak request?
   *(Because the translation can drop the feeling or the discovered needs, even when the concept itself was right.)*

## Lesson 8.3 — Checking the Information: Missing, Ambiguous, Conflicting

**Learning objective:** Find the gaps, vagueness, and contradictions in your
information before it goes to planning.

**Lesson content**

Before a request is ready, you check it for three kinds of problem. Each one, left
in, produces a bad plan.

**Missing information.** Is anything OPE needs simply not there? A request with no
real headcount, no budget, or no clear sense of who's coming has holes — and OPE
will fill those holes with assumptions or produce a plan that's wrong. Your job is
to notice the hole and go get the fact. And here's a rule that matters enormously:
**never invent a missing fact to "complete" the request.** If you don't know the
headcount, you find it out — you do not make up a number to fill the gap. A guessed
fact is worse than an obvious blank, because it looks true.

**Ambiguous information.** Is anything stated in a way that could mean two things?
"A few kids" — how many? "Sometime in summer" — when? "A modest budget" — what does
modest mean to this client? Ambiguity gets resolved by OPE guessing, which means
it's resolved wrongly as often as not. Replace vague with specific before planning.

**Conflicting information.** Do any two facts contradict each other? "An intimate
dinner" and "60 guests." "A tight budget" and "a lavish theme." You met these in
discovery; they can also survive into a request if you're not careful. Two
conflicting facts can't both be planned for — so you resolve the conflict (usually
by going back to the client) rather than letting OPE pick one.

Run all three checks on every request. Most weak requests fail one of them — a hole,
a vagueness, or a contradiction that nobody caught.

**Real examples**

- *Missing — and the temptation to invent:* You're writing the request and realize
  the client never confirmed a headcount. The wrong move is to put "30" so the
  request looks done. The right move is to go get the real number. An invented
  headcount means every quantity and cost in the plan is built on a fiction.
- *Ambiguous:* The request says "an afternoon event." OPE doesn't know if that's a
  1-hour drop-in or a 5-hour party — wildly different plans. You pin it down before
  it goes anywhere.
- *Conflicting:* A request carries both "small and intimate" and "about 60 people."
  You can't plan both. You go back to the client and resolve which matters more,
  then fix the request.

**Common mistakes**

- *Inventing a fact to fill a gap* — the most dangerous translation error.
- *Leaving vague words* ("a few," "modest," "soon") for OPE to interpret.
- *Letting a contradiction through* and assuming the plan will sort it out.

**Knowledge check**

1. What should you never do about a missing fact?
   *(Never invent it to complete the request — find the real answer instead.)*
2. Name the three kinds of information problem you check for.
   *(Missing, ambiguous, and conflicting information.)*

## Lesson 8.4 — Communicating What Matters, Clearly

**Learning objective:** Organize a request so that each kind of important
information — needs, safety, budget, location, timing — is communicated clearly.

**Lesson content**

A request isn't just a bucket of facts; it's information *organized so planning can
use it.* The goal is clarity: anyone (or OPE) reading the request should understand
the event without guessing. Beyond the basic facts, there are five kinds of
information you must make sure come through clearly, because each one strongly
shapes the plan.

- **Participant needs.** The specific things particular people require — an allergy,
  a wheelchair, a quiet space, seating for older guests. These must be stated plainly
  and attached to the event, not buried or left out. A need that isn't communicated
  is a need that won't be planned for.
- **Safety requirements.** Anything the event needs to be safe — supervision for
  children, a weather backup for an outdoor event, trained eyes on any water,
  capacity limits. If safety needs aren't in the request, the plan won't protect
  people. Safety is never assumed; it's stated.
- **Budget constraints.** What the client can actually spend, clearly expressed.
  Without it, OPE can't keep the plan affordable, and you're back to a plan the
  client can't pay for. Budget isn't optional information — it shapes everything.
- **Location constraints.** Where it's happening and what that place means — indoors
  or out, the venue's realities, access issues, a weather dependency. The plan has to
  fit the real place.
- **Timing constraints.** When it happens, how long, and anything timing-dependent —
  midday heat for older guests, a hard end-time for a children's party, a date that
  can't move.

Communicating clearly doesn't mean using fancy structure or filling boxes. It means
expressing each of these so its *meaning* lands. Ask yourself, for each: "if someone
planned only from what I've written, would they get this right?" If not, make it
clearer.

**Real examples**

- A request for a children's party clearly states: 18 children aged 4–6 and 12
  adults, so the *plan* knows to include real supervision — instead of just "30
  people," which hides how much care this event needs.
- A request for an outdoor summer event for older guests communicates the timing
  constraint plainly — "mid-morning to avoid the midday heat, with shade and seating"
  — so the safety-and-comfort need is built into the plan, not lost.

**Common mistakes**

- *Stating a headcount but not who the people are,* hiding the participant needs.
- *Leaving safety requirements implicit,* assuming the plan will include them.
- *Communicating facts but not their meaning* — "outdoors" without "needs a weather
  backup," "older guests" without "needs seating and gentle timing."

**Knowledge check**

1. Name the five kinds of information that must come through clearly in a request.
   *(Participant needs, safety requirements, budget constraints, location constraints, timing constraints.)*
2. What's the test for whether something is communicated clearly enough?
   *(If someone planned only from what you wrote, would they get it right?)*

## Lesson 8.5 — Determining Planning Readiness

**Learning objective:** Judge whether a request is genuinely ready to plan from —
not just whether it looks complete.

**Lesson content**

The final check before planning is **readiness**: is this request good enough that
OPE can produce a plan that truly fits the event? This is not the same as "does it
look finished." A request can look complete and still not be ready — and that gap is
where a lot of bad plans come from.

A request is **planning-ready** when:

- It carries all the core facts (real headcount and who, budget, place, timing) —
  none missing, none guessed.
- It communicates the participant needs and safety requirements clearly.
- It has no ambiguity or contradictions left in it.
- It still preserves the concept's intent — it would produce the agreed event.

A request that *looks* complete but isn't ready usually has a hidden problem: a fact
that's actually a guess, a vague word everyone read past, a discovered need that
quietly got dropped, or a contradiction nobody resolved. It reads smoothly, so it
fools you — which is exactly why you check readiness deliberately instead of trusting
appearances.

The mindset is the one from the whole module: don't ask "is the form filled in?" ask
"does this genuinely give OPE everything it needs to plan *this* event well?" If yes,
you're ready. If there's a hidden hole, you're not — fix it first. It's far cheaper to
catch an unready request now than to discover the problem in a finished plan, or worse,
on the day.

**Real examples (a request that appears complete but is not planning-ready)**

- A request looks thorough — headcount, budget, date, venue, all present. But the
  headcount was never actually confirmed by the client; it's the organizer's guess. It
  *looks* ready and isn't, because a core fact is fiction. The plan it produces will be
  built on a number nobody verified.
- A request reads cleanly and seems complete, but the child's allergy discovered
  earlier isn't in it — it got lost in translation. Nothing in the request *looks*
  wrong, which is exactly the danger. It isn't ready, because a safety-critical fact is
  missing.
- A request for a class-type event looks done, but it never says whether there's
  already someone to lead it or whether that still needs arranging — a real-world
  essential you can't plan or cost without. It's not ready until that's resolved (and
  the point isn't to name a field — it's that you genuinely can't plan the event
  without knowing this).

**Common mistakes**

- *Mistaking "looks complete" for "is ready."* Smooth-reading requests can hide holes.
- *Trusting appearance instead of checking* the facts, needs, and intent deliberately.
- *Pushing an unready request into planning* because it's quicker than fixing it.

**Knowledge check**

1. What's the difference between "looks complete" and "planning-ready"?
   *(Looks complete is about appearance; planning-ready means it genuinely has every fact, need, and intent OPE needs — no hidden holes, guesses, or dropped findings.)*
2. What's the readiness mindset question?
   *(Not "is the form filled in?" but "does this give OPE everything it needs to plan this event well?")*

## Lesson 8.6 — Weak Requests and How to Fix Them

**Learning objective:** Recognize the common signs of a weak request and improve it
into a planning-ready one.

**Lesson content**

By now you can see what makes a request weak. This lesson pulls it together: the
common translation mistakes, how to spot a weak request, and how to fix it.

**The common translation mistakes** (the ways requests go wrong):

- **Losing intent** — the feeling or the agreed character of the event gets
  flattened out.
- **Dropping discovered needs** — allergies, access, quiet spaces, seating vanish in
  translation.
- **Inventing facts** — filling a gap with a guess instead of getting the real
  answer.
- **Leaving things vague** — "a few," "modest," "sometime" that OPE has to interpret.
- **Letting contradictions through** — two facts that can't both be planned for.
- **Hiding meaning** — stating "outdoors" or "30 people" without what they imply for
  weather or supervision.

**Recognizing a weak request.** A weak request is one that *won't* produce the right
plan — because something important is missing, hidden, vague, invented, or
contradictory. The tricky part is that weak requests often look fine on the surface;
a weak request frequently *hides* its missing information rather than showing an
obvious blank. So you recognize weakness not by whether it reads smoothly, but by
running the checks: intent preserved? needs and safety communicated? nothing missing,
guessed, ambiguous, or conflicting? A "no" anywhere means it's weak.

**Improving a weak request.** Fixing it is usually straightforward once you've found
the problem: restore the lost intent, put back the dropped need, replace the invented
fact with the real one (go get it), make the vague specific, resolve the
contradiction, and surface the hidden meaning. You don't rewrite from scratch — you
repair the specific weakness. Then re-check readiness. A request you've improved this
way is what earns a plan that actually fits.

**Real examples (a weak request hiding important information)**

- A request reads as a tidy, complete description of a party — but it quietly omits
  that several guests have mobility needs you discovered. Nothing looks missing; the
  information is simply *hidden* by absence. You catch it by checking "are the
  discovered needs in here?" — and add them.
- *Different requests, same concept:* Two organizers translate the same confirmed
  concept. One produces a planning-ready request — intent intact, needs and safety
  communicated, nothing vague. The other produces a weak one that drops the weather
  backup and leaves the headcount fuzzy. Same concept, very different requests — and
  only one will produce a good plan. The difference is entirely in the quality of the
  translation.

**Common mistakes**

- *Judging a request by how smoothly it reads* instead of by the checks.
- *Rewriting everything* when you only need to repair one weakness.
- *Fixing a weak request without re-checking readiness* afterward.

**Knowledge check**

1. Why are weak requests often hard to spot?
   *(They frequently hide missing information rather than showing an obvious blank, so they read as complete.)*
2. How do you improve a weak request?
   *(Find the specific weakness — lost intent, dropped need, invented or vague or conflicting fact — repair it, then re-check readiness.)*

## Module 8 wrap-up

You now know how to turn a concept into a request OPE can plan from:

- **Concept vs request** — same event, different jobs; the concept is agreed by the
  client, the request is planned from.
- **Preserving intent** — carry the feeling *and* the discovered needs across, so the
  request would still produce the agreed event.
- **Checking the information** — find what's missing, ambiguous, or conflicting, and
  never invent a fact to fill a gap.
- **Communicating clearly** — make participant needs, safety, budget, location, and
  timing land plainly, with their meaning intact.
- **Planning readiness** — judge whether it's genuinely ready, not just whether it
  looks complete.
- **Weak requests** — recognize and repair the common translation mistakes, because
  weak requests hide their holes.

And above all, the principle this whole module rests on: **you are translating
meaning, not filling in fields.** The question is always whether the request gives
OPE a true, complete picture of *this* event — not whether a form looks done.

The one sentence to carry forward: **a good request is the concept re-expressed for
planning — complete, clear, honest, and faithful to everything you agreed and
discovered — so that what OPE plans is the event you actually promised.**

make it real: **Module 9 — Event Planning with OPE.**
