# ActivLife Hub Organizer Competencies v1

## What this document is

This is the **minimum set of competencies** an ActivLife Hub organizer must
have. It is not lessons, exams, certifications, or platform features. It only
names the competencies, says what each one is, shows what doing it well and
badly looks like, and marks which organizer level it's required for.

## The principles this is built on

- A client brings a **dream**, not a plan.
- The organizer helps turn that dream into a **concept**.
- OPE helps turn the concept into a **plan**.
- The organizer **executes** the plan.
- The client is **not** responsible for knowing what information matters.
- The organizer **is** responsible for discovering the information that matters.

Read those two halves together: the client owns the *want*, the organizer owns
the *work* — including the work of finding out what the client never thought to
say.

## The two levels (so the right-hand column makes sense)

- **Verified Organizer** — safe to operate on the platform. Identity is real,
  they understand the platform and how it works, they communicate honestly, and
  they meet the safety baseline. They can take and handle real business without
  causing harm.
- **Certified Organizer** — proven the full professional craft: discovery,
  concept, planning, execution, and aftercare. This is the organizer the
  platform stands behind for real events.

Foundational and trust/safety competencies are required for **Both**. The
planning-and-delivery craft is required for **Certified**.

## Competency 1 — Understands ActivLife Hub and Acts as Part of It

**Purpose**
So the organizer behaves like part of the platform participants trust, not a
freelancer using a tool. Everything else sits on this.

**What the organizer must be able to do**
- Explain what ActivLife Hub is for and who it serves.
- State plainly what an organizer is responsible for and what would break the
  trust participants place in the platform.
- Make decisions that protect that trust, even when a shortcut is easier.

**Good behavior**
- Turns down or reshapes a request they can't deliver well, instead of taking
  the money and hoping.
- Treats a participant's data, money, and family as a responsibility, not a
  transaction.

**Bad behavior**
- "It's just my gig, the platform's their problem."
- Overpromises to win a client, knowing they can't deliver.

**Required for:** Both

## Competency 2 — Operates the Platform Confidently

**Purpose**
The organizer does all real work through the platform. If they can't run it,
they can't do anything else.

**What the organizer must be able to do**
- Move through the platform without guessing — find requests, respond with
  proposals, manage bookings and participants, message, use the calendar.
- Carry one piece of business from first contact to a confirmed booking using
  the platform alone, without workarounds.
- Use the platform's planning and sourcing support as part of normal work.

**Good behavior**
- A new request comes in; they respond inside the platform, on time, with the
  right next step.
- Keeps bookings, participants, and dates accurate so nothing falls through.

**Bad behavior**
- Takes the conversation off-platform "because it's faster," losing the record
  and the protection it gives both sides.
- Loses track of who's booked or when an event is because they never learned
  the tools.

**Required for:** Both

## Competency 3 — Communicates Professionally and Builds Trust

**Purpose**
Most of an organizer's reputation is built in conversations, not events. This is
how interest becomes a booking and how problems stay small.

**What the organizer must be able to do**
- Be clear, reliable, and respectful in every exchange.
- Stay calm and fair when a client is upset, confused, or difficult.
- Communicate consistently enough that any participant feels the same
  dependable standard, whoever they're talking to.

**Good behavior**
- Replies promptly and clearly, sets honest expectations, follows through.
- De-escalates an angry message instead of matching its tone.

**Bad behavior**
- Goes quiet for days, answers in one-word fragments, or over-promises to end
  an awkward conversation.
- Gets defensive or rude when challenged.

**Required for:** Both

---

## Competency 4 — Meets the Safety and Responsibility Baseline

**Purpose**
Events happen in the real world with real people, some of them vulnerable. One
avoidable injury or fumbled emergency can end an organizer and damage the
platform.

**What the organizer must be able to do**
- Spot the everyday safety risks in an activity and plan around them.
- Recognize the extra duties for children, seniors, and outdoor settings.
- Know what to do in an emergency and what they are personally liable for.
- Refuse to run something they can't run safely.

**Good behavior**
- Plans supervision, access, and weather backup *before* the day.
- Stops or changes an event when a real safety problem appears, even at a cost.

**Bad behavior**
- Treats a kids' or seniors' event like any other crowd.
- "It'll be fine" as a safety plan; no idea who's liable if it isn't.

**Required for:** Both

## Competency 5 — Discovers the Information That Matters

**Purpose**
The client isn't responsible for knowing what's important — the organizer is.
This is the competency that makes that principle real. The client brings a
dream; the organizer digs out the facts hidden inside it.

**What the organizer must be able to do**
- Hear an emotional request ("a royal day," "something fun for my team") and
  keep the feeling while pulling out the facts beneath it.
- Ask the boring-but-essential questions — type of event, headcount, adults vs.
  kids, date, budget, location, venue, special needs — naturally, without
  interrogating.
- Notice when a required fact is missing and chase it instead of assuming.
- Catch and resolve contradictions ("intimate dinner for 60").

**Good behavior**
- Turns "I want an unforgettable birthday" into: whose birthday, what age, how
  many guests, what date, what budget, where — all real values.
- Notices the client never mentioned budget, and asks, kindly but clearly.

**Bad behavior**
- Takes "small party" at face value and plans for 15 when the client meant 40.
- Fills the gaps with their own assumptions and never checks them.

**Required for:** Certified

---

## Competency 6 — Turns a Dream into a Concept and a Structured Request

**Purpose**
Discovery gives you facts; this turns them into something real and shared — a
concept the client recognizes and a structured request OPE can plan from. This
is the hinge between the client and the engine.

**What the organizer must be able to do**
- Shape the discovered facts into a clear concept of the event ("your mum's
  60th, ~40 adults, mid-July, at home, around $3,000, relaxed garden feel").
- Read it back and get the client's agreement *before* planning.
- Express it as a clean, structured request that maps onto what OPE needs —
  without losing what made it matter to the client, and without inventing facts.

**Good behavior**
- Confirms the concept in plain words and gets a clear "yes" before building.
- Hands OPE a complete, structured request, so the plan it produces fits the
  real event.

**Bad behavior**
- Jumps straight to planning on a vague idea, never confirming.
- Quietly swaps "her 60th at home" for "a rented hall" because it's easier, and
  feeds OPE that instead.

**Required for:** Certified

---

## Competency 7 — Builds a Real, Workable Plan with OPE

**Purpose**
A confirmed concept still has to become a plan that actually holds up — people,
place, things, time, money. OPE helps, but the organizer owns the result.

**What the organizer must be able to do**
- Use OPE to produce a complete plan from the structured request.
- Sense-check what OPE returns against reality, and adjust where the real
  situation needs it.
- Make sure the plan accounts for everything the event genuinely needs and
  stays within the client's real budget.

**Good behavior**
- Reviews the plan critically, fixes the one assumption that doesn't fit this
  client, and confirms the result is deliverable.
- Catches that the budget won't cover the concept and resolves it honestly with
  the client.

**Bad behavior**
- Forwards OPE's output untouched without checking it makes sense.
- Presents a plan they already know they can't deliver for the money.

**Required for:** Certified

---

## Competency 8 — Executes the Plan on the Day

**Purpose**
A good plan still has to be carried out while people are present and things are
live. Execution is its own skill.

**What the organizer must be able to do**
- Prepare properly in the final stretch before the event.
- Run the event on the day, keep it on schedule, and manage participants, staff,
  and timing.
- Handle problems and last-minute changes without losing control of the room.

**Good behavior**
- Arrives ready, keeps the event flowing, adapts calmly when something slips.
- Solves an on-the-day problem quietly without the guests ever feeling it.

**Bad behavior**
- A solid plan falls apart because nobody ran it.
- Freezes or panics when one thing goes wrong, and the whole event wobbles.

**Required for:** Certified

---

## Competency 9 — Closes Out and Learns from the Event

**Purpose**
The work isn't done when the event ends. Aftercare decides whether a one-time
client returns and whether problems get resolved or become damage.

**What the organizer must be able to do**
- Close the event out cleanly.
- Follow up with the client and participants.
- Handle feedback, complaints, and anything that surfaces afterward, fairly.
- Take something from each event into the next one.

**Good behavior**
- Follows up, thanks the client, handles a late complaint without getting
  defensive.
- Notes what to do differently and actually does it next time.

**Bad behavior**
- Vanishes the moment the event ends.
- Ignores or argues with after-the-fact feedback instead of resolving it.

**Required for:** Certified
