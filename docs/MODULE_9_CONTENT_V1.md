# Module 9 — Event Planning with OPE

*Training content. For a person with no event-organizing experience.*

You've discovered the facts, formed a concept, and turned it into a planning-ready
request. Now OPE does its part: it takes your request and produces a **plan** —
the people, resources, timing, and budget that make the event real. This module is
about what you do *with* that plan. Because here's the thing beginners get wrong:
OPE producing a plan is not the end of your work. It's the start of a new piece of
it.

Hold these principles the whole way through:

- **OPE creates plans. You remain responsible for the plan you present.** When the
  plan goes to the client, it's *your* plan now, not the tool's.
- **A generated plan is a draft until you've reviewed it.** Never treat OPE's first
  output as final.
- **Understand a plan before you present it.** You can't stand behind something you
  haven't actually read and grasped.
- **Find the weaknesses before the client does.** Every problem you catch now is a
  problem that never reaches the client; every one you miss, they find for you — at
  the worst possible time.

This builds directly on Module 2, where you learned OPE plans only from what it's
given, and that its plan is "a strong first draft, judgment over the top." This
module is that judgment, in detail.

**What this module builds toward:** Competency 7 — *Builds a real, workable plan
with OPE.* Read the lessons in order.

## Lesson 9.1 — The Plan Is a Draft, and You're Responsible

**Learning objective:** Explain why every OPE plan is a draft you own, not a
finished answer you forward.

**Lesson content**

When OPE hands you a plan, it can look finished — neat, organized, complete. That
appearance is a trap. The plan is a **draft**: a strong starting point built from
your request, but not yet checked against the full reality only you can see. Your
job is to turn that draft into something you've reviewed, understood, improved
where needed, and can genuinely stand behind.

Why does this matter so much? Because the moment you present a plan to a client, it
becomes **your** plan. The client doesn't see "OPE's plan" — they see *your*
proposal, your promise. If it's understaffed, unsafe, over budget, or wrong for
their event, that's on you, not the tool. "OPE generated it" is never an excuse,
and the client would never accept it anyway. You own everything you present.

This is also why you must *understand* a plan before presenting it. If a client
asks "why this many helpers?" or "can we trim this cost?", you need to actually
know what the plan says and why — not be reading it for the first time in front of
them. Understanding the plan is part of owning it.

And the most valuable habit of all: **find the weaknesses before the client does.**
A plan almost always has something to catch — a thin spot, a missed need, a number
that doesn't sit right. If you catch it, you quietly fix it and the client never
knows there was a problem. If you don't, the client finds it — and now you look
careless, or worse, it surfaces on the day. Reviewing the draft is how you stay the
professional in the room.

**Real examples**

- An organizer receives a tidy-looking plan and forwards it to the client without
  reading it closely. The client spots that there's no provision for the wheelchair
  user they'd discussed. The organizer is embarrassed and scrambling — a problem
  that a five-minute review would have caught privately.
- A careful organizer reads every plan as a draft. On one, they notice the staffing
  looks light for the crowd, fix it before sending, and present a plan they can fully
  explain. The client never sees a flaw, because there wasn't one by the time it
  reached them.

**Common mistakes**

- *Treating OPE's first output as final* and forwarding it unread.
- *Thinking "OPE made it" reduces your responsibility.* It doesn't — you own what
  you present.
- *Presenting a plan you don't actually understand,* so you can't answer for it.

**Knowledge check**

1. Whose plan is it once you present it to the client?
   *(Yours — you're responsible for it, not OPE.)*
2. Why is catching a plan's weakness before the client a big deal?
   *(If you catch it, you fix it privately and they never see a flaw; if you miss it, they find it — making you look careless or causing problems on the day.)*

## Lesson 9.2 — Reading and Understanding a Plan

**Learning objective:** Read an OPE plan and understand what each part of it is
telling you.

**Lesson content**

Before you can judge a plan, you have to actually *read* it and understand it — not
skim it. Reading a plan well means going through it part by part and making sure you
understand what each one says and why it's there.

A plan is built from the same kinds of pieces you met in Module 2:

- **People** — how many staff or helpers, and what kind. Ask: does this match the
  size and type of event?
- **Resources** — the things the event needs: seating, food, equipment, and so on.
  Ask: is everything the event actually needs here?
- **Timing** — how the event is shaped across the available time. Ask: does this flow
  make sense for these people?
- **Budget** — what it's likely to cost, and where the money goes. Ask: does this add
  up, and does it fit what the client can spend?

Reading well means turning each part into understanding. Not just "there are four
helpers" but "four helpers for 30 children — is that enough?" Not just "$2,000 total"
but "where is that $2,000 going, and does it leave room for everything?" You're
building a picture in your head of the whole event the plan describes, so you can
tell whether that event is the right one.

Here's a good sign you've understood a plan: you could explain it to the client in
plain words, justify each major part, and answer "why?" about any of it. If you
can't, you haven't finished reading — go back until you can.

**Real examples (OPE creates a good plan)**

- For a well-described 40-adult garden party, OPE returns a plan: a sensible number
  of helpers, the right catering quantities, a relaxed afternoon flow, a budget that
  reconciles. You read it part by part, understand each, and can explain all of it.
  This is a good plan — and you know that *because* you read it properly, not because
  it looked tidy.
- You read a plan and pause on the timing — then realize it's actually well-shaped for
  the older guests, with a gentle pace. Understanding it confirmed it was good. Reading
  isn't only about finding faults; it's about genuinely grasping the plan.

**Common mistakes**

- *Skimming instead of reading,* so you never actually understand the plan.
- *Reading the numbers without questioning them* — "four helpers" without asking "for
  how many people?"
- *Not being able to explain the plan* in plain words — a sign you haven't understood
  it.

**Knowledge check**

1. What are the main kinds of parts that make up a plan?
   *(People, resources, timing, and budget.)*
2. How do you know you've understood a plan?
   *(You could explain it to the client plainly and answer "why?" about each major part.)*

## Lesson 9.3 — Evaluating Quality: Missing and Unrealistic Elements

**Learning objective:** Judge a plan's quality by spotting what's missing and what's
unrealistic.

**Lesson content**

Once you understand a plan, you evaluate it — is this actually a *good* plan? Two of
the most common quality problems are things that are **missing** and things that are
**unrealistic.**

**Missing elements.** A plan can be missing something the event genuinely needs. This
often happens when a need didn't make it clearly into the request, or when the event
has a requirement that isn't obvious from the basic facts. The fix in the moment is to
add it — but notice the lesson: a missing element in the plan often points back to a
gap in your request, so it's worth asking *why* it's missing. Common missing elements:
a weather backup for an outdoor event, supervision sized for the children, an access
provision, an allergy control.

**Unrealistic elements.** A plan can include something that *looks* fine on paper but
won't work in reality. The classic is staffing that doesn't scale: the same handful of
helpers listed for 120 guests as for 40. Or quantities that are too thin, a timeline
that's too tight to actually run, a cost that's been underestimated. These are the
elements that read smoothly but fall apart on the day.

Evaluating quality means holding the plan up against the real event and asking, for
each part: *is anything the event needs not here? and is anything here not actually
realistic?* A plan passes when nothing important is missing and nothing in it would
break under real conditions.

**Real examples (an incomplete plan, and an unrealistic one)**

- *Incomplete:* A plan for an outdoor party reads well but has no weather backup at
  all. For an outdoor event two days out with rain possible, that's a missing element
  that matters enormously. You add it — and note that the request should have made the
  weather dependency explicit.
- *Unrealistic:* A plan for a 120-guest event lists the same small number of helpers
  you'd expect for 40. On paper it looks complete; in reality the service, setup, and
  safety would all fall short. You catch the unrealistic staffing and fix it before it
  goes out.

**Common mistakes**

- *Assuming a plan is complete because it looks complete* — missing elements don't
  announce themselves.
- *Not scaling a sanity-check to the size* — letting understaffing or thin quantities
  through.
- *Treating a missing element as just a plan fix,* without noticing it may reveal a gap
  in your request.

**Knowledge check**

1. What two quality problems are you specifically looking for?
   *(Missing elements — things the event needs that aren't there — and unrealistic elements — things that look fine but won't work.)*
2. A plan lists the same staffing for 120 guests as for 40. What kind of problem is
   that? *(An unrealistic element — staffing that doesn't scale to the real event.)*

## Lesson 9.4 — Checking the Plan Against People and Realities

**Learning objective:** Test a plan against participant needs, safety, budget, and
timing, and catch where it conflicts with any of them.

**Lesson content**

A plan can be complete and realistic and *still* be wrong — because it doesn't fit the
real people or the real constraints. This lesson is the focused check against four
things that matter most. For each, you ask: does the plan honour this, or conflict
with it?

- **Participant-impact issues.** Does the plan actually work for the people coming?
  A plan can quietly conflict with participant needs — no quiet space for the guest who
  needs one, an activity the injured colleague can't join, no seating for older guests.
  These are the needs you discovered; check that the plan serves them, not an average
  guest.
- **Safety concerns.** Does the plan keep people safe? A plan can conflict with safety —
  too little supervision for the children, no backup for bad weather, water with nobody
  watching, a space filled past what's safe. Safety problems in a plan are the most
  serious to catch, and the least acceptable to miss.
- **Budget concerns.** Does the plan fit what the client can actually spend? A plan can
  look lovely and quietly not reconcile — one part eating the whole budget, or a total
  the client can't afford. Catching this now lets you rework it honestly; missing it
  means an awkward, late conversation.
- **Timing concerns.** Does the timing work for these people and this event? A plan can
  schedule the main activity in the midday heat for older guests, run too late for young
  children, or pack too much into too little time. Timing is often where a plan quietly
  fails the people.

When the plan conflicts with one of these, you don't accept it and "manage around it" —
you fix the plan. A plan that endangers, excludes, overspends, or mistimes is not a plan
you present.

**Real examples (a plan that conflicts with participant needs, and one that conflicts
with safety)**

- *Conflicts with participant needs:* A plan for a family event looks complete, but you
  know two guests use walkers and the plan has them in a space with poor access and no
  seating near the activity. It conflicts with their needs. You rework it so they're
  genuinely included.
- *Conflicts with safety:* A children's party plan looks neat but provides only a couple
  of adults to supervise 25 kids around play equipment. That's a safety conflict — too
  little supervision. You fix the ratio before this plan goes anywhere near the client.
- *Budget:* A plan reads beautifully, but adding it up, the catering alone consumes the
  whole budget, leaving nothing for the venue and rentals it also lists. The numbers
  don't reconcile; you rework it to fit.
- *Timing:* A plan schedules a seniors' garden activity at midday in summer. You catch the
  timing-and-heat conflict and move it earlier.

**Common mistakes**

- *Checking the plan in the abstract* instead of against the real people you discovered.
- *Spotting a safety or access conflict and "managing around it"* instead of fixing the
  plan.
- *Not adding the budget up,* and presenting a plan that doesn't reconcile.

**Knowledge check**

1. What four things do you check a plan against in this lesson?
   *(Participant needs, safety, budget, and timing.)*
2. If a plan conflicts with safety, what do you do?
   *(Fix the plan — never accept an unsafe plan and try to manage around it.)*

## Lesson 9.5 — Improving Plans and Keeping the Concept Intact

**Learning objective:** Improve a plan's weaknesses while keeping it faithful to the
agreed concept.

**Lesson content**

When your review finds a problem — and it usually will — you improve the plan. But
there's a discipline to improving well: you fix the weakness *without* drifting away
from the concept the client agreed to. This is **maintaining concept integrity**, and
it's one of the most important ideas in this module.

Improving a plan means making the targeted fix: add the missing weather backup, scale up
the thin staffing, rework the budget to reconcile, adjust the timing for the older
guests, build in the access the plan forgot. You change what's wrong, not what's right.

But here's the trap: a plan can be *internally* perfect — complete, realistic, safe, on
budget — and still be the wrong plan, because it doesn't match the concept this client
agreed to. A flawless plan for a formal seated dinner is the wrong plan if the client's
concept was a relaxed garden gathering where people mingle. So as you improve, you keep
asking: *does this plan still deliver the agreed concept?* If a fix has quietly pushed the
plan away from the concept, that's a new problem, not a solution.

So improving a plan is two things at once: fixing the real weaknesses, *and* guarding the
concept. The plan you end up with should be complete, realistic, safe, affordable,
well-timed — and unmistakably the event the client actually agreed to. Lose either half
and you've failed: a faithful plan that's broken, or a flawless plan for the wrong event.

**Real examples (a plan that's fine but wrong for this client)**

- A plan for an adult birthday is complete, realistic, and on budget — but it's built as
  a formal evening seated dinner, while this client's agreed concept was a casual daytime
  garden gathering where guests mingle. Nothing's technically wrong with the plan; it's
  just the wrong event. Maintaining concept integrity means you rework its *format* to
  match the concept, not just check it for errors.
- You fix a plan's thin staffing and tight budget, and in doing so you almost trim the
  one element the client cared about most. You catch it and protect that element while
  fixing the rest — improving the plan without losing its heart.

**Common mistakes**

- *Fixing a weakness but drifting from the concept* in the process.
- *Accepting a plan because it's internally flawless,* missing that it's the wrong event
  for this client.
- *Trimming to fix a problem and cutting the thing that mattered most.*

**Knowledge check**

1. What does "maintaining concept integrity" mean when improving a plan?
   *(Keeping the plan faithful to the agreed concept — a complete, correct plan is still wrong if it's not the event the client agreed to.)*
2. Can a plan be internally perfect and still be the wrong plan? *(Yes — if it doesn't
   match the client's agreed concept.)*

## Lesson 9.6 — Comparing and Choosing Between Plans

**Learning objective:** Compare more than one valid plan and choose well between them.

**Lesson content**

Sometimes there's more than one good way to deliver the agreed concept, and you'll have
more than one valid plan to weigh. Just as the same discovery can yield several concepts
(Module 7), the same concept can be delivered by several genuinely good plans — differing
in how they spend the budget, how they use the time, or which trade-offs they make. When
that happens, comparing and choosing well is the skill.

Comparing plans means looking at them side by side against what matters: which delivers
the concept best, which serves the people best, which handles the budget and timing best,
which carries the least risk. Rarely is one plan better in *every* way — usually each has
strengths and trade-offs. A plan that's a little cheaper might be a little tighter on
time; a plan that's more relaxed might cost a bit more. The comparison is about which set
of trade-offs best fits *this* client and *these* people.

Choosing between plans is a judgment call, and an honest one. You weigh the trade-offs
against the client's real priorities — what they care about most, which you learned in
discovery. If they most want everyone relaxed and unhurried, the plan that protects that
wins even if it costs a little more. There's often no single "correct" choice, just a
best-fit one — and sometimes the right move is to bring the client a clear choice rather
than decide for them (the next lesson).

**Real examples (OPE creates multiple valid plans)**

- For an agreed 60th-birthday concept, you have two good plans: one a relaxed buffet that
  leaves budget for live music, the other a fuller seated meal with no music. Both deliver
  the concept; they trade differently. You compare them against what the client cares about
  most — and that's what decides it, not which plan is "better" in the abstract.
- Two valid plans for a team day both fit the budget and the group. One is more active,
  one more relaxed. Neither is wrong. You choose by which trade-offs suit *this* team,
  knowing both are genuinely good.

**Common mistakes**

- *Looking for the one "correct" plan* when several valid ones exist with different
  trade-offs.
- *Comparing plans in the abstract* instead of against the client's real priorities.
- *Choosing the plan you prefer* rather than the one that best fits the client and people.

**Knowledge check**

1. Can the same concept be delivered by more than one good plan? *(Yes — differing in
   budget use, timing, and trade-offs.)*
2. What do you weigh trade-offs against when choosing between valid plans?
   *(The client's real priorities and the needs of the people — what they care about most.)*

## Lesson 9.7 — Presenting Plans and Adapting After Feedback

**Learning objective:** Present a plan to a client clearly, and adapt it well when they
give feedback.

**Lesson content**

A reviewed, improved plan still has to be *presented* — and then, often, *adjusted* once
the client responds. Both are part of the job.

**Presenting a plan.** Present it as something you understand and stand behind. Explain it
in plain terms, connect it back to the concept they agreed ("this delivers the relaxed
garden gathering we talked about, for your 40 guests, within your budget"), and be honest
about any trade-offs. Because you reviewed it, you can answer their questions and justify
its parts — which is exactly what builds confidence. If there were several valid plans,
this is where you lay out the real choice clearly and help them weigh it, guiding without
pushing.

**Adapting after feedback.** Clients will react, and that's good — their feedback makes the
plan better. They might want something added, a cost trimmed, a part changed. Adapt
willingly and professionally: make the change they're asking for, but apply the same
discipline you used reviewing the draft. When you adjust, re-check the plan — does the
change keep it complete, realistic, safe, on budget, and *still faithful to the concept?* A
client's requested change can accidentally break something or drift from the concept, and
it's your job to catch that and handle it honestly ("happy to add that — here's what it
means for the budget").

The thread through both: you're the steady professional who understands the plan, presents
it with confidence, welcomes feedback, and keeps the plan sound and faithful through every
change — right up until it's agreed and ready to deliver.

**Real examples**

- You present a plan by tying it to the agreed concept and walking the client through it
  plainly. They ask why a cost is what it is; because you reviewed it, you explain it
  easily, and they feel confident.
- A client loves the plan but asks to add a photographer and extend by an hour. You adapt
  willingly — and re-check: the additions push the budget, so you tell them honestly what
  each means and let them choose. The plan stays sound because you re-reviewed it after the
  change.

**Common mistakes**

- *Presenting a plan you can't explain,* so questions expose that you didn't review it.
- *Resisting feedback* instead of using it to improve the plan.
- *Making a requested change without re-checking* the plan for new problems or concept
  drift.

**Knowledge check**

1. When presenting a plan, what should you connect it back to?
   *(The concept the client agreed to — showing the plan delivers it.)*
2. After you adapt a plan to a client's feedback, what must you do?
   *(Re-check it — is it still complete, realistic, safe, on budget, and faithful to the concept?)*

## Module 9 wrap-up

You now know how to work with an OPE plan, not just receive one:

- **A plan is a draft, and you own it** — review it, understand it, and stand behind
  what you present.
- **Read and understand it** — part by part, until you could explain every piece.
- **Evaluate quality** — catch what's missing and what's unrealistic.
- **Check against people and realities** — participant needs, safety, budget, timing;
  fix conflicts, don't manage around them.
- **Improve while keeping the concept intact** — fix the weakness without drifting from
  the agreed event.
- **Compare and choose** — weigh valid plans by the client's real priorities.
- **Present and adapt** — explain with confidence, welcome feedback, and re-check after
  every change.

The one sentence to carry forward: **OPE builds the draft, but you build the plan —
reading it, testing it against the real people and the agreed concept, fixing what's
weak, and standing behind every plan you put in front of a client.**

Next, with a sound plan in hand, you'll learn to make it real on the day: **Module 10 —
Event Execution.**
