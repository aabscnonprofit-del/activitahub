# ActivLife Hub Organizer Learning Structure v1

## Purpose of this document

This document defines the **learning path** a new ActivLife Hub organizer
follows — the major sections and the order they come in. It does **not**
contain lessons, exams, questions, certifications, or platform feature
specifications. It exists so that we agree on the *structure* of training
before anyone writes a single lesson.

The guiding idea: an ActivLife Hub organizer does **not** work on their own.
They work *through* the platform. So before they learn to plan or run events,
they must understand what ActivLife Hub is and how to operate inside it. The
order below reflects that. We teach the platform first, the people skills
second, the safety duties third, and only then the craft of running events.

Learning order is fixed. A later section assumes the earlier ones are done.

---

## 1. Understanding ActivLife Hub

**Why it exists**
An organizer who doesn't understand what the platform is for will make
decisions that hurt participants, themselves, and the platform's reputation.
Everything else they learn sits on top of this. If they get this wrong, the
rest doesn't matter.

**What the organizer should learn**
- The mission of the platform — what ActivLife Hub is trying to do, and for whom.
- The role of organizers — what they are responsible for and where their authority begins and ends.
- The role of participants — who they are, what they expect, and what they are trusting the organizer with.
- The trust model — why participants trust ActivLife Hub, how that trust is extended to organizers, and how an organizer can lose it.
- Organizer responsibilities — the baseline commitments every organizer accepts by working on the platform.

**Competency expected after completion**
The organizer can explain, in their own words, what ActivLife Hub is, why
participants use it, what is expected of an organizer, and what behavior would
break the trust the platform depends on. They see themselves as part of the
platform, not a freelancer who happens to use it.

---

## 2. Using the Platform

**Why it exists**
The organizer does all real work through the platform. If they can't navigate
it confidently, every later skill — communication, planning, execution — is
blocked. This section turns the platform from an obstacle into a tool they
trust.

**What the organizer should learn**
How to find, read, and act inside each working area of the platform:
- the dashboard — where they start and what it tells them
- activities — how their offerings live on the platform
- requests — how interest from participants reaches them
- proposals — how they respond to that interest
- bookings — how an agreement becomes real
- participants — how they see and manage the people attending
- messaging — how they talk to participants inside the platform
- calendar — how they keep track of what is happening and when
- OPE — how the platform helps them plan an event
- sourcing tools — how the platform helps them find what an event needs

**Competency expected after completion**
The organizer can move through the platform without hesitation, knows where
each kind of work happens, and can carry a single piece of business from first
contact to a confirmed booking using the platform alone — without guessing and
without working around it.

---

## 3. Client Communication

**Why it exists**
Most of an organizer's reputation is built in conversations, not events. How
they talk to participants decides whether interest becomes a booking, whether a
small problem becomes a complaint, and whether someone comes back. The platform
gives them the tools; this section teaches them how to use those conversations
well.

**What the organizer should learn**
- Professionalism — showing up reliably, clearly, and on time in every exchange.
- Politeness — basic respect and tone, even under pressure.
- Trust — how to be the kind of person participants feel safe handing their plans, money, and family to.
- Handling difficult situations — staying calm and fair when someone is upset, confused, or unreasonable.
- Sales and conversion basics — how to turn genuine interest into a real booking without pushing or overpromising.
- Communication standards expected from ActivLife Hub organizers — the consistent way organizers are expected to communicate so the platform feels dependable no matter who the participant talks to.

**Competency expected after completion**
The organizer can carry a respectful, clear conversation from first question to
booking, can calm and resolve a difficult exchange, and communicates in a way
that matches the standard ActivLife Hub participants have come to expect.

---

## 4. Safety and Responsibility

**Why it exists**
Activities happen in the real world with real people, some of them vulnerable.
A single avoidable injury or mishandled emergency can end an organizer and harm
the platform's standing. This section comes before event planning on purpose:
the organizer must know what they are responsible for before they design
anything that puts people in a room, a field, or the water.

**What the organizer should learn**
- Participant safety — the everyday duty of care owed to anyone attending.
- Child activities — the extra care, supervision, and limits required when minors are involved.
- Senior activities — the specific risks and accommodations older participants need.
- Outdoor activities — weather, terrain, and environment risks and how to plan around them.
- Emergency situations — what to do when something goes wrong, and who to involve.
- Organizer liability — what the organizer is personally responsible for, and where the lines sit.

**Competency expected after completion**
The organizer can identify the safety risks in a given activity, knows the
added duties for children, seniors, and outdoor settings, knows how to respond
when an emergency happens, and understands clearly what they are liable for.
They will not run an activity they cannot run safely.

---

## 5. Event Planning Fundamentals

**Why it exists**
Now that the organizer understands the platform, can communicate, and knows
their safety duties, they can learn to plan an event well. Planning is where
most events succeed or fail — before anyone shows up. This section teaches the
thinking that turns an idea into a workable plan.

**What the organizer should learn**
- How to turn a participant's goal into a concrete event.
- What every event needs to actually happen — people, place, things, time, and money.
- How to plan realistically so the event holds up when conditions change.
- How to use the platform's planning support (introduced in Section 2) as part of real planning, not as a separate exercise.

**Competency expected after completion**
The organizer can take a request and produce a realistic, complete plan for an
event — one that accounts for the people, place, resources, timing, and cost —
and can do it using the platform.

---

## 6. Event Execution

**Why it exists**
A good plan still has to be carried out on the day. Execution is a different
skill from planning: it is about staying in control while things are live and
people are present. This section teaches the organizer to run the event they
planned.

**What the organizer should learn**
- How to prepare in the final stretch before the event.
- How to run the event on the day and keep it on track.
- How to manage participants, staff, and timing while the event is happening.
- How to handle problems and changes in the moment without losing the room.

**Competency expected after completion**
The organizer can take a planned event and run it from start to finish, keep it
on schedule, manage the people involved, and adapt calmly when something does
not go to plan.

---

## 7. Post-Event Management

**Why it exists**
The work isn't over when the event ends. What the organizer does afterward
decides whether a one-time participant becomes a repeat one, and whether
problems get resolved or turn into damage. This is the section that closes the
loop and feeds the next event.

**What the organizer should learn**
- How to properly close out an event.
- How to follow up with participants after the event.
- How to handle feedback, complaints, and any issues that surface afterward.
- How to learn from the event so the next one is better.

**Competency expected after completion**
The organizer can close an event cleanly, follow up well, handle what comes
after fairly, and turn each finished event into a better next one and a
participant who wants to come back.

---

## Why this order

1. **Understanding ActivLife Hub** — without this, nothing else lands.
2. **Using the Platform** — they must be able to operate the platform before doing real work in it.
3. **Client Communication** — they must be able to talk to participants before they take them on.
4. **Safety and Responsibility** — they must know their duties before they design anything that puts people at risk.
5. **Event Planning Fundamentals** — now they can plan.
6. **Event Execution** — now they can run what they planned.
7. **Post-Event Management** — now they can close the loop and improve.

Each section assumes the ones before it. This is the structure we agree on
*before* writing lessons, exams, or certifications.
