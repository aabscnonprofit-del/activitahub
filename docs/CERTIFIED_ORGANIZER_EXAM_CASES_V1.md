# Certified Organizer Exam — Case Bank, Draft v1

First draft of practical organizer scenarios for the experienced-organizer
test-out exam. Cases only. No multiple-choice design, no scoring. Each case maps
to a section of VERIFIED_ORGANIZER_EXAM_STRUCTURE_V1 (sections A–I).

For each case: **Scenario · Competency tested · What a strong organizer notices ·
What a weak organizer misses · Why the case matters.**

---

## Section A — Understanding Client Intent

### Case 1 — "I want a royal day for my daughter"
- **Scenario:** A client asks for "a royal day" for their daughter. No age, no guest count, no budget, no date.
- **Competency tested:** Understanding client intent (A).
- **Strong organizer notices:** "Royal" is a *feeling*, not a spec. They keep it as context (she wants her daughter to feel special and celebrated) but recognize they still know nothing operational — age changes everything (a 6-year-old "royal day" and a 16-year-old "royal day" are different events).
- **Weak organizer misses:** Locks onto "royal" and starts pricing castles, tiaras, and thrones before knowing the girl's age or the budget.
- **Why it matters:** The most expensive mistake is planning confidently in the wrong direction. Reading intent correctly is where the whole chain starts.

### Case 2 — "Something fun for my team"
- **Scenario:** A manager wants "something fun for my team." Doesn't say how many people, their ages, fitness, or what "fun" means to this group.
- **Competency tested:** Understanding client intent (A).
- **Strong organizer notices:** "Fun" means different things to a 25-person sales floor and a 6-person engineering team; some teams love physical challenges, others dread them. They treat "fun" as a question to resolve, not an answer.
- **Weak organizer misses:** Books a default crowd-pleaser (paintball, an escape room) that half the team will hate or can't physically do.
- **Why it matters:** Corporate "fun" that excludes people becomes a complaint to the manager — the opposite of the goal.

### Case 3 — "An unforgettable event for my parents"
- **Scenario:** A client wants "a wonderful, unforgettable event" for their parents. Doesn't say the occasion, the parents' ages, health, or who's invited.
- **Competency tested:** Understanding client intent (A).
- **Strong organizer notices:** This is probably a milestone (anniversary, big birthday) for older people — which raises mobility, stamina, hearing, and timing-of-day questions before anything else. They sense the seniors angle inside the warm language.
- **Weak organizer misses:** Plans an evening event with loud music and lots of standing, unsuitable for the actual guests of honor.
- **Why it matters:** The guests of honor's real needs are hidden inside an emotional request; missing them ruins the very event being celebrated.

## Section B — Discovery

### Case 4 — The birthday with no numbers
- **Scenario:** "I want a birthday party for my son." That's the whole brief.
- **Competency tested:** Discovery (B); children.
- **Strong organizer notices:** Required facts are all missing — the boy's age, how many kids, how many supervising adults, date, budget, where. They know exactly which to ask and ask without interrogating.
- **Weak organizer misses:** Assumes a "typical" kids' party size and age and plans from the assumption.
- **Why it matters:** A party sized for 10 when 30 kids show up is a safety and supervision failure, not just a logistics one.

### Case 5 — The client who never mentions budget
- **Scenario:** A client describes a detailed vision enthusiastically but never says a number, and the organizer feels awkward asking.
- **Competency tested:** Discovery (B); budget constraints.
- **Strong organizer notices:** Budget is the single most-skipped and most-important fact. They ask for it kindly and clearly, early, because everything else depends on it.
- **Weak organizer misses:** Avoids the money conversation, builds a beautiful plan, then watches the deal collapse on price.
- **Why it matters:** A plan the client can't afford wasted everyone's time and damages trust.

### Case 6 — "An intimate dinner for 60"
- **Scenario:** The client asks for "a small, intimate dinner" and later mentions "about 60 people."
- **Competency tested:** Discovery (B); unclear requests.
- **Strong organizer notices:** These two facts contradict each other. They flag it and ask which matters more — the intimate feel or the 60 guests — instead of silently picking one.
- **Weak organizer misses:** Plans a 60-person event and quietly drops "intimate," or plans an intimate room and is shocked by the headcount.
- **Why it matters:** Contradictions left unresolved become an unhappy client on the day, certain they were misunderstood.

### Case 7 — The unstated mobility need
- **Scenario:** A client books a 70th birthday at a venue. Mentions in passing that "Grandma will be there." Says nothing about access.
- **Competency tested:** Discovery (B); seniors, accessibility.
- **Strong organizer notices:** A guest of honor's grandmother may have mobility, seating, or restroom-access needs. They ask directly about anyone who'll need step-free access, seating, or special timing.
- **Weak organizer misses:** Books a venue with stairs and no accessible restroom because the client never explicitly said "wheelchair."
- **Why it matters:** The client isn't responsible for knowing what to disclose — the organizer is responsible for asking.

### Case 8 — The team event with a hidden constraint
- **Scenario:** A manager books a team activity. Doesn't mention that two team members are pregnant and one has a knee injury.
- **Competency tested:** Discovery (B); conflicting participant needs.
- **Strong organizer notices:** "Whole team" events need a question about anyone with physical limits, dietary needs, or things that would exclude them. They ask before choosing the activity.
- **Weak organizer misses:** Picks a high-intensity physical activity, then scrambles when three people can't take part.
- **Why it matters:** An activity that sidelines colleagues defeats the purpose and embarrasses the client in front of their team.

## Section C — Concept Formation

### Case 9 — Turning facts into a concept
- **Scenario:** Discovery is done: mum's 60th, ~40 adults, mid-July, at the client's home, budget ~$3,000, "relaxed and warm."
- **Competency tested:** Concept formation (C).
- **Strong organizer notices:** All the pieces fit a realistic concept (a relaxed garden gathering for 40, catered within budget, July weather plan included). They assemble it coherently and plan to confirm it before building.
- **Weak organizer misses:** Either jumps straight to logistics without a shared concept, or proposes something that doesn't match "relaxed and warm" (a formal seated banquet).
- **Why it matters:** The concept is the thing the client says "yes" to. Skipping it means planning something the client never actually agreed to.

### Case 10 — The champagne concept on a lemonade budget
- **Scenario:** Client wants an outdoor evening reception "like a wedding" for 80 guests — open bar, live band, plated dinner — with a budget that realistically covers a casual afternoon BBQ.
- **Competency tested:** Concept formation (C); unrealistic expectations, budget.
- **Strong organizer notices:** The concept and the budget don't meet. They form an honest, achievable concept that keeps what matters most to the client and tell them clearly what the budget will and won't buy.
- **Weak organizer misses:** Either promises the wedding-style event anyway, or silently strips it down to something cheap without telling the client what changed.
- **Why it matters:** A concept built on a budget that can't support it fails on the day; honesty at concept stage protects everyone.

### Case 11 — The concept that drifts from the client
- **Scenario:** Client wants a small home celebration for their father. The organizer, used to bigger events, finds it easier to propose a restaurant buyout.
- **Competency tested:** Concept formation (C).
- **Strong organizer notices:** "At home, small" is part of what the client wants, not an inconvenience. They build the concept around the home setting rather than substituting what's easier for them.
- **Weak organizer misses:** Quietly swaps the home celebration for a venue because it's simpler to deliver.
- **Why it matters:** Substituting the organizer's preference for the client's intent breaks trust even when the event is "nicer."

## Section D — Converting Requests into OPE-Ready Inputs

### Case 12 — The class request missing a key input
- **Scenario:** A confirmed concept: a weekly beginners' yoga class for ~12 people at a community hall. The brief doesn't say whether there's already an instructor or whether one is needed.
- **Competency tested:** Converting to OPE-ready inputs (D).
- **Strong organizer notices:** For a class, "instructor: have or need" is a required input — it changes the plan and the cost completely. They get the answer before handing it to OPE.
- **Weak organizer misses:** Submits the request without it, and OPE plans for the wrong scenario.
- **Why it matters:** OPE is only as good as the structured request; a missing situational input produces a confident but wrong plan.

### Case 13 — The art class and the materials question
- **Scenario:** Concept confirmed: a kids' art class for 15. Nobody has said whether materials are provided or guests bring their own.
- **Competency tested:** Converting to OPE-ready inputs (D); children.
- **Strong organizer notices:** "Materials: provided or BYO" is required for a class and drives both cost and prep. They nail it down, along with adult-to-child supervision, before submitting.
- **Weak organizer misses:** Leaves it blank or guesses "provided," and the budget and prep come out wrong.
- **Why it matters:** Incomplete inputs at the bridge stage quietly corrupt everything downstream.

### Case 14 — The structured request with an invented fact
- **Scenario:** The client never confirmed a headcount, but the organizer needs to submit the request and writes "30" to move things along.
- **Competency tested:** Converting to OPE-ready inputs (D).
- **Strong organizer notices:** A required value that's a guess is not a fact. They go back and get the real number rather than inventing one to complete the form.
- **Weak organizer misses:** Submits the invented headcount; OPE plans and prices around a number the client never gave.
- **Why it matters:** Inventing inputs is one of the disqualifying failures — it breaks the link between the plan and the real event.

## Section E — Communication Judgment

### Case 15 — The angry message
- **Scenario:** Three days before the event, the client sends an angry message accusing the organizer of ignoring them, though the organizer replied two days ago.
- **Competency tested:** Communication judgment (E).
- **Strong organizer notices:** The client is stressed, not necessarily right. They respond calmly, acknowledge the worry, restate where things stand clearly, and de-escalate — without getting defensive or matching the tone.
- **Weak organizer misses:** Fires back to prove they did reply, turning a nervous client into an adversary.
- **Why it matters:** How an organizer handles a tense moment decides whether a small worry becomes a blow-up.

### Case 16 — The pressure to over-promise
- **Scenario:** A client pushes hard: "Just promise me it'll be perfect and I'll book right now." The organizer knows one detail (a specific florist) can't be guaranteed.
- **Competency tested:** Communication judgment (E).
- **Strong organizer notices:** Winning the booking by promising something they can't deliver is a trap. They commit warmly to what they *can* guarantee and stay honest about the one thing they can't.
- **Weak organizer misses:** Says "yes, perfect, I promise," and sets up a guaranteed disappointment.
- **Why it matters:** Dishonesty to close a booking is a disqualifying failure and destroys the platform's trust.

### Case 17 — The client who goes silent
- **Scenario:** After a proposal is sent, the client goes quiet for a week.
- **Competency tested:** Communication judgment (E).
- **Strong organizer notices:** Silence usually means hesitation, distraction, or sticker shock — not rejection. They follow up once, politely, with a useful nudge (offering to adjust, answering likely concerns), not pestering.
- **Weak organizer misses:** Either gives up immediately or sends a string of increasingly anxious messages.
- **Why it matters:** Most lost bookings are lost in the silence; judgment about follow-up is a real conversion skill — without being pushy sales.

## Section F — Safety Awareness

### Case 18 — The bouncy castle and the missing supervision
- **Scenario:** A kids' birthday for 25 children with a bouncy castle. The client has arranged two adults total, including themselves (who'll be busy hosting).
- **Competency tested:** Safety awareness (F); children, safety.
- **Strong organizer notices:** 25 children plus an inflatable needs real, dedicated supervision and clear rules; one distracted host is not a safety plan. They raise it and fix the ratio before the day.
- **Weak organizer misses:** Treats supervision as the client's problem and proceeds.
- **Why it matters:** Child injuries are the most serious failure mode; under-supervision is how they happen.

### Case 19 — The seniors' walk in a heatwave
- **Scenario:** An outdoor walking activity for a group of seniors is scheduled for midday in forecast 34°C heat.
- **Competency tested:** Safety awareness (F); seniors, weather.
- **Strong organizer notices:** Heat is dangerous for older participants. They move the time to early morning, build in shade, water, rest stops, and seating — or reconsider the activity.
- **Weak organizer misses:** Runs it at noon as planned because "it's just a walk."
- **Why it matters:** Recognizing that the same activity carries different risk for different people is core safety judgment.

### Case 20 — The wheelchair user and the upstairs venue
- **Scenario:** A participant uses a wheelchair. The chosen venue's event room is up a flight of stairs with no lift, and the only restroom is also upstairs.
- **Competency tested:** Safety awareness (F); accessibility, disabilities.
- **Strong organizer notices:** This venue excludes the participant and must be changed or adapted — step-free access and an accessible restroom are non-negotiable, not nice-to-haves.
- **Weak organizer misses:** Plans to "carry the chair up" or assumes the participant will manage, which is unsafe and humiliating.
- **Why it matters:** Accessibility failures both endanger and exclude people, and the organizer owns the duty to prevent that.

### Case 21 — The peanut allergy the caterer can't guarantee
- **Scenario:** A child attending has a severe peanut allergy. The chosen caterer says they "can't guarantee a nut-free kitchen."
- **Competency tested:** Safety awareness (F); allergies, children.
- **Strong organizer notices:** "Can't guarantee" plus "severe allergy" plus "child" is a real risk. They change caterer or menu, confirm controls, and make sure responsible adults know the emergency plan.
- **Weak organizer misses:** Accepts "we'll be careful" and moves on.
- **Why it matters:** A mishandled allergy can be life-threatening; this is exactly where casual reassurance is not enough.

### Case 22 — The outdoor event with rain in the forecast
- **Scenario:** An outdoor afternoon party for 50 is two days out, with a 70% chance of rain forecast.
- **Competency tested:** Safety awareness (F); weather risks.
- **Strong organizer notices:** A real weather backup is needed *now* — covered space, a tent, or an indoor alternative — not a hope that it holds off.
- **Weak organizer misses:** Has no plan B and "will decide on the day."
- **Why it matters:** Weather is foreseeable; the failure to plan for it is a planning and safety lapse, not bad luck.

### Case 23 — The activity that shouldn't run as asked
- **Scenario:** A client insists on an unsupervised lake swim as part of a children's outdoor party, with no lifeguard and no one trained.
- **Competency tested:** Safety awareness (F); children, safety.
- **Strong organizer notices:** Some requests should be refused or fundamentally changed. They will not run an unsupervised water activity for children and say so clearly, offering a safe alternative.
- **Weak organizer misses:** Treats "the client asked for it" as permission and runs it.
- **Why it matters:** Knowing when to say no is a core part of the safety competency; willingness to run a clearly unsafe event is disqualifying.

## Section G — Planning Judgment

### Case 24 — The plan that's understaffed
- **Scenario:** A plan for a 120-guest event lists the same staffing as a 40-guest one.
- **Competency tested:** Planning judgment (G).
- **Strong organizer notices:** The staffing doesn't scale with the headcount — service, safety, and setup will all fall short. They catch it and adjust before the plan goes out.
- **Weak organizer misses:** Forwards the plan as-is because it "looked complete."
- **Why it matters:** OPE produces a plan, but the organizer owns the result; sense-checking against reality is the job.

### Case 25 — The budget that won't stretch
- **Scenario:** The plan is lovely, but adding it up, the catering alone consumes the whole budget, leaving nothing for the venue and rentals it also lists.
- **Competency tested:** Planning judgment (G); budget constraints.
- **Strong organizer notices:** The numbers don't reconcile. They rework the plan to fit the real budget and have an honest conversation about trade-offs, before committing.
- **Weak organizer misses:** Presents an unaffordable plan and discovers the gap when it's too late to fix gracefully.
- **Why it matters:** A plan that doesn't add up is a failure waiting to happen on the day.

### Case 26 — The one wrong assumption
- **Scenario:** A plan for an adult birthday assumes an evening indoor venue, but this client specifically wanted a daytime garden event at home.
- **Competency tested:** Planning judgment (G).
- **Strong organizer notices:** One assumption baked into the plan contradicts the client's actual concept. They catch and correct it instead of accepting the default.
- **Weak organizer misses:** Lets the wrong assumption ride because the rest of the plan looks fine.
- **Why it matters:** A single unchecked assumption can mean planning the wrong event end to end.

## Section H — Execution Judgment

### Case 27 — The caterer who doesn't show
- **Scenario:** It's the day of the event and the caterer is two hours late with no answer to calls; 60 guests arrive in 90 minutes.
- **Competency tested:** Execution judgment (H).
- **Strong organizer notices:** They work the problem immediately — chase the caterer, line up a fallback, adjust the timeline — and keep the client and guests from feeling the panic.
- **Weak organizer misses:** Freezes, or broadcasts the crisis to everyone and loses the room.
- **Why it matters:** A good plan still has to survive the day; staying in control under failure is its own skill.

### Case 28 — The weather that turns mid-event
- **Scenario:** An outdoor event is underway when the sky opens unexpectedly.
- **Competency tested:** Execution judgment (H); weather risks.
- **Strong organizer notices:** They move calmly to the backup (the one they planned), get guests dry and comfortable, and keep the event flowing.
- **Weak organizer misses:** Has nowhere to move people and lets the event dissolve.
- **Why it matters:** Execution judgment is tested precisely when the plan meets reality.

### Case 29 — The guest count that doubled
- **Scenario:** The plan was for 40; roughly 75 people walk in.
- **Competency tested:** Execution judgment (H).
- **Strong organizer notices:** They triage on the spot — stretch food and seating, adjust flow, get help — and manage the client's expectations honestly about what's possible now.
- **Weak organizer misses:** Tries to pretend nothing's wrong until the food and seating visibly run out.
- **Why it matters:** Real events go off-script; adapting without losing composure is what execution competence looks like.

## Section I — Post-Event Judgment

### Case 30 — The late complaint about an incident
- **Scenario:** A week after a kids' event, a parent messages that their child was hurt on equipment and the organizer "didn't seem to care."
- **Competency tested:** Post-event judgment (I); children, safety.
- **Strong organizer notices:** This needs a serious, non-defensive response — acknowledge, find out exactly what happened, take responsibility for what's theirs, and fix the process so it can't recur.
- **Weak organizer misses:** Gets defensive, disputes the parent, or goes silent.
- **Why it matters:** How a safety incident is handled afterward determines whether it becomes a resolved issue or lasting damage.

### Case 31 — The quiet client and the mixed feedback
- **Scenario:** The event went mostly well but had a rough patch. The client thanks the organizer briefly and goes quiet; a couple of guests left lukewarm comments.
- **Competency tested:** Post-event judgment (I).
- **Strong organizer notices:** A short "thanks" after a bumpy event can signal unspoken disappointment. They follow up warmly, invite honest feedback, address the rough patch, and learn from it for next time.
- **Weak organizer misses:** Takes the brief thanks at face value and never closes the loop.
- **Why it matters:** Aftercare decides whether a one-time client returns; the work isn't done when the event ends.

The 31 draft cases span all nine exam sections (A–I) and deliberately cover
children (4, 13, 18, 21, 23, 30), seniors (3, 7, 19), accessibility (7, 20),
disabilities (20), allergies (21), weather risks (19, 22, 28), budget
constraints (5, 10, 25), unclear client requests (1, 2, 3, 6), conflicting
participant needs (2, 8), unrealistic expectations (10, 25), and safety concerns
(18–23, 30). This is a first draft case bank for review before any
multiple-choice or scoring design.
