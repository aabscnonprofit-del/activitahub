# Module 3 — Operating the Platform

*Training content. For a person with no event-organizing experience.*

In Module 1 you learned what ActivLife Hub is and what your job is. In Module 2
you learned about OPE, the planning tool. Now you'll learn how the actual work
flows through the platform — from the moment a client first reaches you, all the
way to following up after the event.

One important note about this module: it does **not** teach you which button to
press or where things sit on the screen. The platform's look will change over
time, and memorizing screens would be a waste. What *doesn't* change is the
**workflow** — the order work happens in, what you're responsible for at each
step, and the decisions you have to make. That's what you'll learn here, and it
will still be true no matter how the platform looks.

**What this module builds toward:** Competency 2 — *Operates the platform
confidently.* By the end you'll be able to carry a single piece of business from a
client's first contact through to post-event follow-up, knowing what each step
means and what's expected of you.

**The flow to keep in your head the whole way through:**

```
Client → Organizer → OPE → Plan → Participants → Execution → Follow-up
```

Everything in this module is one step or another along that line. Read the lessons
in order — they follow the flow.

## Lesson 3.1 — Your Organizer Profile and What It Represents

**Learning objective:** Explain what your organizer profile is for and the
responsibility that comes with it.

**Lesson content**

Before any work flows to you, clients see *you* — your organizer profile. It's how
a stranger decides whether to trust you with their event, their money, and their
people. So it isn't decoration; it's the front door to everything that follows.

Your profile represents a promise: that you're a real, accountable person who can
be relied on. Treat it that way. Keep it honest and current — who you are, what
you do, what you can genuinely deliver. Never claim experience or capabilities you
don't have, because the moment a client books you on a false impression, you've
set up a disappointment and risked the trust the whole platform runs on.

The responsibility here is simple but serious: everything on your profile should
be true, and everything a client expects from it should be something you can
actually deliver. Your profile is the start of the relationship — make it an
honest one.

**Real examples**

- An organizer who's great with small children's parties keeps their profile
  focused on exactly that. A client looking for a kids' party feels confident; a
  client wanting a 300-person gala looks elsewhere — which is right for everyone.
- An organizer pads their profile with skills they don't really have to attract
  more work. The bookings that result are events they can't deliver well, and the
  reviews show it. The padding cost them more than it gained.

**Common mistakes**

- *Overstating what you can do* to attract more clients. It leads to events you
  can't deliver and trust you can't rebuild.
- *Letting the profile go stale or vague* so clients can't tell what you actually
  offer.

**Knowledge check**

1. What does your organizer profile represent to a client?
   *(A promise that you're a real, accountable person who can deliver what you say — the basis on which they decide to trust you.)*
2. Why is overstating your abilities on your profile a mistake?
   *(It attracts bookings you can't deliver well, creating disappointment and breaking trust.)*

## Lesson 3.2 — Creating and Managing Activities

**Learning objective:** Explain what an activity is, the difference between
preparing one and publishing it, and your duty to keep it accurate.

**Lesson content**

An **activity** is something you offer through the platform — a kind of event or
experience a client can discover and come to you about. Creating activities is how
you make yourself findable: a client browsing the platform sees your activities
and reaches out about the one that fits.

There are two states that matter. While you're still preparing an activity, it's a
work in progress — not yet visible to clients. Once you **publish** it, clients can
find it and contact you about it. That difference matters: publishing is you
saying, "this is ready, and what it describes is true." Don't publish something
half-finished or inaccurate, because clients will take it at face value.

Managing activities is an ongoing responsibility, not a one-time task. Keep them
accurate as things change. If something you offer is no longer available, or the
details shift, update it. An out-of-date activity sets a false expectation, and the
client who acts on it arrives at a disappointment that's on you, not them.

**Real examples**

- An organizer prepares a "children's craft afternoon" activity, gets every detail
  right, and only then publishes it. Clients who find it know exactly what they're
  contacting about.
- An organizer used to offer outdoor summer parties but no longer has the space.
  They leave the old activity published. A client contacts them excited about it,
  and the organizer has to disappoint them — a problem they created by not keeping
  it current.

**Common mistakes**

- *Publishing an activity before it's truly ready or accurate.* Clients believe
  what they see.
- *Leaving outdated activities live.* They generate the wrong inquiries and waste
  everyone's time.

**Knowledge check**

1. What's the difference between an activity you're preparing and one you've
   published? *(A prepared activity isn't visible to clients yet; publishing makes it findable and signals that it's ready and accurate.)*
2. Why is keeping activities up to date part of your job?
   *(An out-of-date activity sets a false expectation, and the client who acts on it is let down through no fault of their own.)*

## Lesson 3.3 — Requests: How a Client Reaches You

**Learning objective:** Explain what a request is, what it means when one arrives,
and your responsibilities in responding.

**Lesson content**

When a client is interested, they send a **request.** This is the first real
contact — a client raising their hand to say, "I'd like you to help me with an
event." Importantly, a request is not a commitment and not a payment. It's the
start of a conversation. The client is exploring, and there's no upfront charge to
them for reaching out.

When a request arrives, this is where your real work begins — and where everything
from Modules 1 and 2 kicks in. A request almost never contains everything you
need. It's the client's *dream*, in their words, often vague. Your job is to
respond promptly, warmly, and then begin **discovery**: asking the questions that
turn their wish into facts you can plan from.

Two responsibilities stand out here. First, **respond in good time.** A client who
reaches out and hears nothing for days assumes you don't care, and you've lost
them before you began. Second, **start discovering, don't start assuming.** The
request is an invitation to ask good questions — not a complete brief to plan from.

**Real examples**

- A request comes in: "I want a special birthday for my daughter." That's a dream,
  not a plan. A strong organizer replies quickly and warmly and begins asking — her
  age, how many children, the date, the budget, where — turning the request into
  something real.
- A request mentions "a small gathering for my elderly father." The organizer
  responds and, knowing older guests may have needs, asks early about mobility,
  seating, and timing — discovery prompted by the request, not assumed from it.

**Common mistakes**

- *Replying slowly or not at all.* Interest cools fast; silence loses clients.
- *Treating the request as a finished brief* and jumping to a plan instead of
  starting discovery.
- *Answering with a price or a plan before you understand the event.* You can't
  price or plan what you haven't discovered yet.

**Knowledge check**

1. What is a request, and does it commit the client to anything?
   *(It's a client's first expression of interest — the start of a conversation, with no commitment and no upfront charge.)*
2. What two things should you do when a request arrives?
   *(Respond promptly and warmly, and begin discovery — asking the questions that turn the wish into facts.)*

## Lesson 3.4 — Proposals: Turning a Request into an Offer (with OPE)

**Learning objective:** Explain what a proposal is and how you use OPE within your
normal workflow to build one.

**Lesson content**

Once you've done your discovery and shaped a concept the client agrees with, you
turn it into a **proposal** — a concrete offer of what you'll deliver and what it
will cost. The proposal is how the conversation becomes a real, considered offer
the client can say yes to.

This is exactly where **OPE fits into your normal workflow.** You don't price and
plan a proposal by guesswork. You take the clear, true, complete description you
built through discovery, and OPE turns it into a plan — the people, resources,
timing, and budget. That plan is what your proposal is built on, so the offer you
make is realistic and the price reflects a real plan, not a hopeful guess.

Remember everything from Module 2 here: OPE's plan is a strong draft, and your
judgment goes over the top of it. Before the proposal goes to the client, check
that the plan truly fits this event and these people — the right supervision for
children, the access an older or disabled guest needs, the allergy you discovered.
The proposal you send should be something you've checked and can stand behind.

And be honest in it. Promise what you can deliver. A proposal that over-promises to
win the booking is a problem you're scheduling for later.

**Real examples**

- After discovering a kids' party is 20 children and 12 adults at a backyard with a
  $1,500 budget, you let OPE build the plan, check that the supervision and food fit
  young children, and send a proposal you know you can deliver.
- For a family lunch, OPE's plan looks complete, but you know two guests use
  walkers and the plan doesn't reflect easy access. You adjust it before it
  becomes a proposal — your judgment over OPE's draft, exactly as Module 2 taught.

**Common mistakes**

- *Building a proposal before discovery and concept are done.* You'll be offering a
  plan for an event you don't actually understand yet.
- *Sending OPE's plan straight into a proposal without checking it.* The plan is a
  draft; the proposal is your promise.
- *Over-promising to win the booking.* It sets up a disappointment you'll own.

**Knowledge check**

1. What is a proposal, and what is it built on?
   *(A concrete offer of what you'll deliver and what it costs, built on the plan OPE produces from your discovered, confirmed description.)*
2. Where does your judgment come in before a proposal is sent?
   *(You check OPE's plan against reality — safety, access, allergies, fit for these people — and adjust it before it becomes a promise.)*

## Lesson 3.5 — Bookings: The Agreement

**Learning objective:** Explain what a booking is, what it means once it exists,
and how to handle changes to it.

**Lesson content**

When a client accepts your proposal, it becomes a **booking.** A booking is the
agreement — the client has said yes, and now there's a real commitment between you
to make this event happen. It's the moment the event stops being a possibility and
becomes something you're responsible for delivering.

Think of a booking as the handshake, not the cash register. It records what was
agreed — the event, the date, the terms. From here, your job shifts from winning
the work to *delivering* it: lining everything up so the agreed event actually
happens as promised.

Things change, and that's normal. A client may need to adjust a detail, move a
date, or occasionally cancel. Handle changes professionally and honestly: be clear
about what a change means, keep the booking accurate so it always reflects what's
truly agreed, and treat a cancellation with grace rather than friction. A booking
is a living agreement — your job is to keep it honest and keep your side of it.

**Real examples**

- A client accepts your proposal for a 60th birthday lunch. It's now a booking —
  a commitment. Your focus turns to making the agreed event real.
- A week before a kids' party, the client asks to add ten more children. You don't
  just quietly absorb it — you explain what the change means for the plan and the
  cost, update the agreement so it's accurate, and proceed on clear terms.

**Common mistakes**

- *Treating a booking as "done" rather than "now I deliver."* The agreement is the
  start of delivery, not the finish line.
- *Letting the booking drift out of date* as things change, so it no longer
  reflects what's actually agreed.
- *Handling changes or cancellations with friction* instead of professionalism.

**Knowledge check**

1. What is a booking? *(The agreement that exists once a client accepts your
   proposal — a real commitment to deliver the event.)*
2. A client wants to change a detail after booking. What's the right approach?
   *(Be clear about what the change means, keep the agreement accurate, and handle it professionally.)*

## Lesson 3.6 — Working with Participants

**Learning objective:** Explain who participants are within the platform and your
responsibilities for managing them.

**Lesson content**

Once an event is agreed, your attention turns to the **participants** — the actual
people who'll attend. Managing participants is how you keep track of who's coming,
make sure the event fits them, and run the day smoothly.

Depending on the event, participants come to you in different ways — you may add
them yourself from the details the client gave you, or they may confirm their own
attendance. Either way, your responsibility is the same: know who's coming, keep
that picture accurate, and make sure what you discovered about them is carried
through into the event.

This is where the discovery from earlier pays off. The participant list isn't just
names and a headcount — it's the people whose needs you uncovered. The child with
the allergy, the grandparent who needs a seat, the guest who needs step-free
access — managing participants means making sure those needs are remembered and
met on the day, not lost between the booking and the event.

Keeping the count accurate matters too. The plan was built for a certain number;
if attendance shifts, you need to know, because it changes supervision, food,
seating, and safety. An accurate participant picture is what lets you deliver the
event you planned.

**Real examples**

- For a children's party, you keep an accurate list of how many kids are actually
  coming, because the supervision and safety you planned depend on that number
  being right.
- You flagged during discovery that one guest uses a wheelchair and one child has a
  dairy allergy. Working with participants means those facts stay attached to those
  people all the way to the event, so the access and the food are right on the day.

**Common mistakes**

- *Losing track of the real headcount,* so the plan no longer matches who shows up.
- *Letting individual needs get lost* between discovery and the event — the allergy
  or access need you found is no use if it's forgotten by the day.

**Knowledge check**

1. Why does keeping an accurate participant count matter?
   *(The plan — supervision, food, seating, safety — was built for a certain number; if it shifts and you don't know, the event won't fit.)*
2. What should managing participants carry through from your discovery?
   *(The individual needs you uncovered — allergies, access, seating — so they're met on the day.)*

## Lesson 3.7 — Working with Communication Tools

**Learning objective:** Explain how you communicate through the platform and the
standard expected of you.

**Lesson content**

Communication runs through the whole flow — with the client while you plan, and
with participants as the event approaches. The platform gives you tools to do this:
ways to message, to send updates, and to make sure people get timely reminders. The
tools matter less than *how you use them.*

Two simple standards cover most of it. **Be timely:** people who don't hear from
you assume the worst. A client waiting on an answer, or a participant unsure of the
details, should never be left in silence. **Be clear:** say what people need to
know, plainly, before they have to ask. Good communication prevents most problems
from ever starting — confusion, no-shows, last-minute panic all shrink when people
are kept informed.

As an event nears, proactive communication is part of the job: confirming details,
sending reminders so participants arrive prepared, and passing on any changes
promptly. You don't wait to be asked — you keep people informed because it's what a
reliable organizer does, and because it directly protects the event.

And keep your communication on the platform. It keeps a clear record for everyone
and protects both you and the client. Taking things off-platform "because it's
faster" loses that record and the protection it provides.

**Real examples**

- Two days before a party, you send the participants a clear reminder with what
  they need to know. Fewer people are confused, fewer arrive unprepared, and the
  day goes smoother.
- A detail changes — the start time shifts by an hour. You let everyone know
  promptly rather than hoping they notice, so nobody arrives at the wrong time.

**Common mistakes**

- *Going silent* and leaving clients or participants guessing.
- *Communicating only when asked,* instead of proactively keeping people informed.
- *Moving conversations off the platform,* losing the record that protects
  everyone.

**Knowledge check**

1. What two standards cover most good communication?
   *(Be timely and be clear.)*
2. Why keep communication on the platform?
   *(It keeps a clear record for everyone and protects both you and the client.)*

## Lesson 3.8 — Execution and Post-Event Follow-up

**Learning objective:** Explain how the platform supports you through running the
event and what your responsibilities are after it ends.

**Lesson content**

When the day comes, all the earlier work — discovery, planning, participant
management, communication — pays off in **execution.** Running the event is its own
skill (you'll go deep on it later), but within the platform your job is to work
from the accurate picture you've built: the confirmed participants, the plan you
checked, the needs you carried through. The better you kept everything up to date,
the smoother the day runs and the easier it is to handle whatever comes up.

But the work doesn't end when the event does. **Post-event responsibilities** are a
real part of the job, and beginners often forget them. After an event you should:

- **Close it out properly** — make sure everything is accounted for and the event
  is genuinely finished, not just over.
- **Follow up with the client** — thank them, check they were happy, and give them
  an easy way to share how it went.
- **Handle whatever comes after** — feedback, a late question, a complaint, or a
  concern. Handle all of it promptly and fairly, without getting defensive.
- **Learn from it** — notice what went well and what you'd do differently, and
  carry that into your next event.

This is what turns a one-time client into a returning one, and a finished event
into a better next one. An organizer who vanishes the moment the event ends leaves
all of that value on the table — and risks a small, fixable issue growing into
real damage.

**Real examples**

- After a successful children's party, you follow up with the client, thank them,
  and invite their thoughts. They felt looked after, leave a warm review, and come
  back for the next birthday.
- A few days after an event, a client raises a concern about something that went
  wrong. Because you respond promptly and fairly instead of going quiet, it's
  resolved well — and the relationship survives.

**Common mistakes**

- *Disappearing once the event ends.* Follow-up is part of the job, not an extra.
- *Ignoring or arguing with after-the-event feedback,* letting a small issue grow.
- *Never reflecting,* so the same avoidable problems repeat event after event.

**Knowledge check**

1. Name two post-event responsibilities.
   *(Any two of: close the event out properly, follow up with the client, handle feedback/complaints fairly, learn from it.)*
2. Why does post-event follow-up matter?
   *(It turns one-time clients into returning ones, resolves issues before they grow, and makes your next event better.)*

## Module 3 wrap-up

You now understand how work flows through the platform, end to end:

```
Client → Organizer → OPE → Plan → Participants → Execution → Follow-up
```

- **Your profile** is the honest front door clients judge you by.
- **Activities** make you findable; keep them ready and accurate.
- **Requests** are first contact — respond fast and start discovering.
- **Proposals** turn a confirmed concept into an offer, built on an OPE plan you've
  checked.
- **Bookings** are the agreement — the start of delivery, not the finish.
- **Participants** are the real people; keep the count and their needs accurate.
- **Communication tools** keep everyone informed, on time and on the platform.
- **Execution and follow-up** deliver the event and close the loop that brings
  clients back.

### The biggest mistakes organizers make inside the platform

Carry this short list with you — these are the errors that cause the most trouble:

- **Being slow to respond** to a request or a message — interest and trust cool
  fast.
- **Jumping to a plan or a price before discovery** — offering a plan for an event
  you don't yet understand.
- **Sending OPE's plan without checking it** — forwarding a draft as a promise.
- **Over-promising to win a booking** — scheduling a disappointment.
- **Letting things drift out of date** — stale activities, wrong headcounts,
  bookings that no longer match what's agreed.
- **Losing individual needs** — the allergy or access need you discovered, lost by
  the day.
- **Going silent or off-platform** — leaving people guessing and losing the record
  that protects everyone.
- **Disappearing after the event** — skipping the follow-up that turns one event
  into the next.

The one sentence to carry forward: **the platform is just the path your work
travels — respond quickly, keep everything honest and up to date, carry what you
discovered all the way to the day, and never disappear before the loop is closed.**

Next, you'll learn the skill that everything from the first request depends on:
talking with clients well — **Module 4 — Client Communication.**
