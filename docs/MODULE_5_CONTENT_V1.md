# Module 5 — Safety and Responsibility

*Training content. For a person with no event-organizing experience.*

You've learned what the platform is, how OPE helps you plan, how work flows, and
how to talk with clients. Now, before you learn to shape and plan events, you
learn the thing that has to come first: **keeping people safe.**

This module comes before planning on purpose. You can't responsibly design an
event until you understand what could go wrong and what you owe the people in your
care. Once you know that, safety becomes part of every choice you make — you'll
spot an unsafe idea while it's still just an idea, not after someone's been hurt.

Two things to be clear about up front. First, this is **not** a law or compliance
course. We won't cover regulations, legal duties, or rules that differ from one
country to the next — those vary, and you'll learn the specific ones that apply to
you elsewhere. What you'll learn here is **practical judgment**: how to think about
risk and responsibility like a careful, experienced organizer. Second, safety is
not one consideration among many that you balance against cost or a client's
wishes. It's the **floor** — the thing you never trade away. Hold that idea
through every lesson.

**What this module builds toward:** Competency 4 — *Meets the safety and
responsibility baseline.* Read the lessons in order.

## Lesson 5.1 — Duty of Care: The Idea Behind All Safety

**Learning objective:** Explain what "duty of care" means for an organizer and why
it sits underneath every safety decision.

**Lesson content**

When people come to your event, they're putting themselves — and often their
children and their elderly parents — in your hands. They're trusting that you've
thought about their wellbeing so they don't have to. That trust creates what's
called a **duty of care**: a plain responsibility to take reasonable steps to keep
the people at your event safe.

You don't need a legal definition to act on it. Duty of care, in practice, means
one simple habit: *before and during every event, ask yourself what could harm
the people here, and take sensible steps to prevent it.* That's it. Not paranoia,
not imagining every freak accident — just genuinely thinking ahead about real
risks and dealing with them.

This duty is yours, and you can't hand it off. Not to the client, not to OPE, not
to "it'll probably be fine." The client may not think about safety at all — that's
normal, they're not the professional. OPE plans the structure but never judges
whether it's safe. The responsibility to think about harm and prevent it lands on
you, every time. Everything else in this module is just duty of care applied to
specific situations.

**Real examples**

- A client books a party and never once mentions safety — not the pool, not the
  ages of the kids, nothing. That's completely normal. The thinking they didn't do
  is the thinking *you* do.
- An organizer assumes "the venue will have handled all that." The venue handled
  the building; nobody was watching the children near the road. Duty of care means
  *you* don't assume someone else is thinking about harm — you make sure someone
  is, and that someone is you.

**Common mistakes**

- *Assuming safety is someone else's job* — the client's, the venue's, OPE's. It's
  yours.
- *Confusing duty of care with paranoia.* It's not imagining everything; it's
  thinking honestly about real risks and acting on them.

**Knowledge check**

1. In plain terms, what is an organizer's duty of care?
   *(A responsibility to think about what could harm the people at your event and take sensible steps to prevent it.)*
2. Can you hand your duty of care to the client, the venue, or OPE? *(No — it's always yours.)*

## Lesson 5.2 — Caring for Children and Seniors

**Learning objective:** Recognize the heightened responsibilities when children or
older people are at your event.

**Lesson content**

The same activity carries very different risk for different people. Children and
older guests are the two groups that most often need extra care, and a good
organizer adjusts for them automatically.

**Children** need supervision, and more of it than beginners expect. They move
fast, don't sense danger, and get into trouble in seconds. The key questions are
always: *how many children, and how many capable adults are watching them?* A
handful of kids and one distracted host is not supervision. Anything with extra
risk — water, heights, play equipment, a busy road nearby — needs real, dedicated
eyes on the children, not a host who's also serving food and greeting guests.

**Seniors** need a different kind of care. Think about stamina, mobility, heat,
seating, and pace. An event that's fine for energetic adults can exhaust or
endanger older guests: long periods standing, a venue with stairs, the midday sun,
a packed schedule with no rest. The fix is usually simple once you've *thought*
about it — seating, shade, gentler timing, easy access — but only if you thought
about it in advance.

The habit for both groups is the same: when you know children or older people will
be there, picture the event through their eyes and ask what they specifically
need. That picture changes the plan, and it's your job to draw it.

**Real examples**

- A kids' birthday for 25 children has a bouncy castle, and the client has lined up
  two adults total — one of them the busy host. That's not enough. The children
  need dedicated supervision and clear rules, and arranging that is on you, before
  the day.
- An outdoor walk is planned for a group of seniors at midday in real heat. For
  older guests that's dangerous, not just uncomfortable. Moving it to early
  morning, with shade, water, rest stops, and seating, turns a risky plan into a
  safe one.

**Common mistakes**

- *Underestimating how much supervision children need* — treating "there are some
  adults around" as a safety plan.
- *Running a seniors' event on a young, energetic template* — forgetting seating,
  pace, heat, and access.

**Knowledge check**

1. What are the two key questions whenever children are present?
   *(How many children, and how many capable adults are dedicated to watching them?)*
2. Name two things to think about for older guests.
   *(Any two of: stamina, mobility, heat, seating, pace, access.)*

## Lesson 5.3 — Accessibility, Disabilities, Allergies, and Medical Needs

**Learning objective:** Make sure an event is safe and genuinely workable for
guests with access needs, disabilities, allergies, or medical conditions.

**Lesson content**

Some guests have specific needs that decide whether they can take part safely — or
at all. Meeting those needs is both a safety duty and a matter of not leaving
people out.

**Accessibility and disabilities.** If a guest uses a wheelchair, walks with
difficulty, or has a sensory or other disability, the event has to actually work
for them. A venue with stairs and no lift doesn't just inconvenience a wheelchair
user — it excludes them, and "we'll carry them up" is unsafe and humiliating.
Step-free access, an accessible restroom, a quiet space for someone who gets
overwhelmed — these aren't nice extras, they're what make the event possible for
that person. The test is simple: *can everyone invited actually take part safely
and with dignity?*

**Allergies and medical considerations.** These can be life-threatening, so they
get serious care. A severe food allergy means you genuinely control what's served
and how it's prepared — not "we'll try to be careful." If a caterer "can't
guarantee" safety for a severe allergy, that's not good enough; you change the
plan. More broadly, if you learn a guest has a medical condition that could matter
during the event, make sure the right people know and that there's a plan if
something happens.

The thread through all of this: you can only meet a need you know about, which is
why discovering these needs (the next module) matters so much — and once you know,
you're responsible for making sure the need is actually met on the day, not just
noted and forgotten.

**Real examples**

- A guest uses a wheelchair, and the venue's room and only restroom are up a
  flight of stairs with no lift. The venue must change or be adapted — step-free
  access and an accessible restroom aren't optional.
- A child at a party has a severe nut allergy, and the caterer says they can't
  guarantee a nut-free kitchen. "We'll be careful" isn't enough for a severe
  allergy. You change the caterer or the menu, confirm how it's controlled, and
  make sure responsible adults know what to do if there's a reaction.

**Common mistakes**

- *Treating access needs as optional extras* instead of what makes the event
  possible for that person.
- *Accepting "we'll be careful" for a severe allergy.* Serious allergies need real
  control, not good intentions.
- *Noting a need during discovery and then losing it* before the day.

**Knowledge check**

1. What's the simple test for accessibility?
   *(Can everyone invited actually take part safely and with dignity?)*
2. A caterer "can't guarantee" safety for a child's severe allergy. What do you do?
   *(Change the caterer or menu, confirm how it's controlled, and make sure adults know the emergency plan — "we'll be careful" isn't enough.)*

## Lesson 5.4 — Outdoor Events, Weather, and Water

**Learning objective:** Anticipate and plan for the added risks of being outdoors,
weather, and any activity involving water.

**Lesson content**

The outdoors adds risks that an indoor event doesn't have, and they're all
foreseeable — which means they're all your responsibility to plan for.

**Outdoor events** put you at the mercy of ground, sun, terrain, and surroundings.
Think about shade and water in heat, footing and hazards on the site, and what's
nearby — a road, a pond, anything a child could wander to. None of this is exotic;
it's just looking at the actual place and asking what it adds.

**Weather** is the classic foreseeable risk. You always know roughly what's coming,
so "we'll hope it's fine" is never a plan. The real test is having a genuine backup
when bad weather is possible — covered space, a tent, an indoor alternative — and
deciding it in advance, not on the day. Sometimes the safe backup costs money or
means changing the date, and that's a real trade-off you work through honestly with
the client. What you don't do is leave it to luck.

**Water** deserves its own serious attention, because water and people — especially
children — is one of the highest-risk combinations there is. Any activity in or
near water needs proper, trained supervision watching the water, every moment.
Unsupervised swimming, particularly for children, isn't a risk to manage — it's
something you don't allow. We'll come back to that in the "when to say no" lesson,
because water is exactly where saying no can save a life.

**Real examples**

- An outdoor party for 50 is two days out with a 70% chance of rain, and the venue
  has no indoor space and a tight budget. "Decide on the day" isn't safe or
  professional. You weigh the real options — a tent, a date change, a covered
  alternative — and make a clear call with the client.
- A client wants children swimming in a lake during a party, with no lifeguard and
  nobody trained watching. That's not an activity to supervise more carefully —
  it's one you don't run that way.

**Common mistakes**

- *Treating weather as bad luck* instead of a foreseeable risk you plan a backup
  for.
- *"We'll decide on the day"* — leaving a known risk to chance.
- *Underestimating water* — allowing swimming, especially for children, without
  trained eyes on the water at all times.

**Knowledge check**

1. Why is "we'll hope the weather holds" never an acceptable plan?
   *(Weather is foreseeable; the responsibility is to have a real backup decided in advance.)*
2. What does any activity in or near water require?
   *(Proper, trained supervision watching the water at all times — and no unsupervised swimming, especially for children.)*

## Lesson 5.5 — Venues and Capacity

**Learning objective:** Assess a venue for risk and respect the limit on how many
people a space and plan can safely hold.

**Lesson content**

The place an event happens carries its own risks, and so does how many people you
put in it.

**Venue risks.** Every venue should be looked at with safety eyes before you commit
to it. Are there clear ways out? Stairs, slippery areas, sharp edges, an unfenced
pool or pond, a busy road at the boundary? Is there an accessible route for guests
who need one? You don't need to be an inspector — you need to actually look at the
real space and ask, "what here could hurt someone, and what does this place make
harder?" A venue that looks lovely in a photo can be wrong for a group with young
children or older guests.

**Capacity limits.** Every space and plan has a number of people it can safely
hold — set by things like how people get out, how many you can supervise, and how
much staff you have. That number is a **safety limit, not a preference.** When a
client wants to pack in more people than the space and plan can safely handle, the
answer isn't to squeeze them in for the bigger fee. Overcrowding is a classic,
preventable disaster. The right response is a safe alternative — a bigger venue,
fewer guests, or more staff — or you don't take it on as asked.

The habit for both: look at the real place, picture the real number of real people
in it, and ask whether it works safely. If it doesn't, you change something — you
don't hope.

**Real examples**

- A backyard party for children looks fine on paper, but the actual yard is small
  and backs onto an unfenced pond. The plan alone never shows that — walking the
  real space does. You'd add a barrier, dedicated supervision, or move the event.
- A client wants 200 people in a space and a plan you know can only be run safely
  for around 120 — the exits, staffing, and supervision won't cover more. You don't
  overfill it for the bigger booking; you offer a safe alternative or decline it as
  framed.

**Common mistakes**

- *Judging a venue from a photo or description* instead of the real space and the
  real group using it.
- *Treating capacity as flexible* — squeezing in more people for a bigger fee. A
  safe limit is a limit.

**Knowledge check**

1. What should you actually do to assess a venue's risks?
   *(Look at the real space and ask what could hurt someone and what it makes harder — exits, hazards, access, surroundings.)*
2. A client wants more people than the space can safely hold. What's the right
   response? *(Offer a safe alternative — bigger venue, fewer guests, more staff — or don't take it on as asked; capacity is a safety limit, not a preference.)*

## Lesson 5.6 — Emergency Thinking

**Learning objective:** Prepare for things going wrong so that, if they do, you can
respond calmly instead of freezing.

**Lesson content**

Even well-run events can have an emergency — an injury, an allergic reaction,
someone unwell, weather turning dangerous. You can't prevent every one, but you can
make sure you're not caught helpless. That's emergency thinking: deciding, *before*
the event, what you'd do if something went wrong.

It comes down to a few simple questions you answer in advance:

- **If someone is hurt or unwell, what happens?** Who helps them, where's the
  nearest help, how would you get it quickly?
- **Who needs to know what?** Do the right people at the event know about the
  serious allergy, the medical condition, the plan?
- **How do I reach people and get people out** if it comes to that?
- **Who's in charge in the moment?** When something happens, people look for
  someone calm to take the lead. At your event, that's you.

You don't need a thick binder. You need to have *thought it through* so that if the
moment comes, you're acting from a plan instead of panicking. The difference
between an organizer who's thought ahead and one who hasn't is enormous when
seconds matter — one steadies the situation, the other freezes while everyone
waits.

**Real examples**

- Before a party with a guest who has a severe allergy, you make sure the
  responsible adults know about it and know what to do if there's a reaction.
  Because that thinking happened in advance, a scary moment is handled fast instead
  of in panic.
- An outdoor event has weather turn suddenly. The organizer who already decided
  where everyone would go moves people calmly to safety; the one who never thought
  about it stands frozen while guests get soaked or worse.

**Common mistakes**

- *Assuming nothing will go wrong,* so there's no plan when something does.
- *Keeping the emergency knowledge to yourself* instead of making sure the right
  people know.
- *Freezing in the moment* because you never thought it through beforehand.

**Knowledge check**

1. What is emergency thinking?
   *(Deciding in advance what you'd do if something went wrong, so you can respond calmly instead of freezing.)*
2. Name one question you should answer before the event.
   *(Any of: if someone's hurt, what happens? who needs to know what? how do I reach people or get them out? who's in charge in the moment?)*

## Lesson 5.7 — When to Say No, and Holding the Line Under Pressure

**Learning objective:** Recognize when an event or request is too unsafe to run as
asked, and hold firm even when the client pushes.

**Lesson content**

Part of being a responsible organizer is knowing that sometimes the right answer is
**no.** Most safety problems can be planned around — but some requests are unsafe at
their core, and the professional move is to refuse them or fundamentally change
them. Unsupervised children near water. A space packed far past what's safe. A
guest's essential safety or access need dropped to save money. For these, "the
client asked for it" is not permission.

The hard part is rarely *knowing* the right answer — it's **holding it under
pressure.** The client is paying, they're enthusiastic, and they may push: "it'll
be fine, just this once," "can't we skip the lifeguard to save money," "just fit
everyone in." This is exactly where real safety is lost — not from ignorance, but
from an organizer giving in to a paying client. A safety or access measure is never
a line-item to trade away for budget or to please someone.

So when you meet one of these moments, hold firm — kindly, but firmly. Explain why
the measure stays. Offer a safe alternative if there is one. And if the client
insists on something genuinely unsafe, be willing to walk away from the booking. A
booking is never worth someone getting hurt. Saying no well — calm, clear, and
unmovable on the things that matter — is one of the most important skills you have.

**Real examples**

- To save money, a client pushes you to drop the trained lifeguard at a kids' pool
  party — "it'll be fine, just this once." You hold the line: the lifeguard stays,
  or the swimming doesn't happen. You find the saving elsewhere or you don't run it
  that way.
- A client wants to cut the accessible-transport arrangement a disabled guest
  needs, to trim cost. That need isn't a line-item to delete. You keep it and save
  elsewhere, or you decline the event as framed.

**Common mistakes**

- *Treating "the client asked for it" as permission* for something unsafe.
- *Caving to a paying client* and removing a safety or access measure to please
  them or cut cost.
- *Being unwilling to walk away* from a booking that can't be made safe.

**Knowledge check**

1. When is the right answer to a request "no"?
   *(When it's unsafe at its core and can't be made safe — e.g. unsupervised kids near water, dangerous overcrowding, dropping an essential safety or access need.)*
2. A paying client pushes you to cut a safety measure to save money. What do you do?
   *(Hold the line — keep the measure, save elsewhere or offer a safe alternative, and be willing to walk away; safety is never traded for cost.)*

## Lesson 5.8 — Responsibility After You Discover a Risk

**Learning objective:** Act correctly once you become aware of a risk — before,
during, or after the event.

**Lesson content**

There's a moment that defines a responsible organizer: the moment you *become
aware* of a risk. Once you know about a danger, you can't un-know it, and doing
nothing is a choice you own. What you do next matters enormously.

The rule is simple: **the moment you discover a risk, it becomes yours to act on.**

- **Before the event,** if you discover a risk — an unsafe venue feature, a guest's
  serious medical need, a plan that won't keep children safe — you deal with it.
  You don't file it away and hope. Fix it, change the plan, or if it can't be made
  safe, don't run it that way.
- **During the event,** if a risk appears — weather turning, a hazard you didn't
  see, a guest in trouble — you act on it then and there, calmly, even if it means
  changing or stopping something.
- **After the event,** if something went wrong, you take it seriously. If a guest
  was hurt, you respond properly: find out exactly what happened, take
  responsibility for what's yours, look after the person, and fix whatever let it
  happen so it can't happen again. Getting defensive or going quiet after an
  incident isn't just poor service — it abandons the responsibility that's still
  yours.

What you must never do is the thing that's always tempting: notice a risk and let
it slide because dealing with it is inconvenient, costly, or awkward. The whole
point of everything in this module is that when you see the danger, you act. That's
what responsibility means.

**Real examples**

- While setting up, you notice the play area is right beside an unfenced drop you
  hadn't clocked before. You don't shrug — you move the play area or block off the
  drop before any child is near it.
- A week after a children's event, a parent says their child was hurt on the
  equipment and that you "didn't seem to care." You respond seriously and without
  defensiveness — find out what happened, own your part, check on the child, and
  fix the process — instead of disputing it or going silent.

**Common mistakes**

- *Noticing a risk and letting it slide* because acting is inconvenient or costly.
- *Going quiet or defensive after something goes wrong,* instead of owning it and
  fixing it.
- *Treating "I didn't cause it" as the end of your responsibility* — once you're
  aware, acting is on you.

**Knowledge check**

1. What happens the moment you discover a risk?
   *(It becomes yours to act on — before, during, or after the event.)*
2. After an incident where a guest was hurt, what's the responsible response?
   *(Take it seriously — find out what happened, own your part, look after the person, and fix what let it happen — never get defensive or go silent.)*

## Module 5 wrap-up

You now understand how to think about safety and responsibility:

- **Duty of care** — the responsibility to think about harm and prevent it sits
  with you, always.
- **Children and seniors** — the same event carries different risk for different
  people; adjust for the vulnerable.
- **Access, disabilities, allergies, medical needs** — make the event safe and
  workable for everyone; serious needs get serious care.
- **Outdoors, weather, water** — foreseeable risks you plan backups for; water,
  especially with children, needs trained eyes and firm limits.
- **Venues and capacity** — look at the real space and the real number; a safe
  capacity is a limit, not a preference.
- **Emergency thinking** — decide in advance what you'd do, so you act instead of
  freeze.
- **When to say no, and holding the line** — refuse what's unsafe at its core, and
  never trade a safety measure away to please a paying client.
- **Responsibility after discovering a risk** — the moment you know, acting is
  yours, before, during, and after.

The one sentence to carry forward: **safety is the floor you never trade away —
when you see a risk, you act on it, even when it costs money, even when the client
pushes, even when it's inconvenient, because a booking is never worth someone
getting hurt.**

Next, with safety firmly in mind, you'll learn the first real skill of planning:
drawing the facts out of a client — **Module 6 — Discovery and Information
Gathering.**
