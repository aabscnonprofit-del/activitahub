# Module 12 — Integrated Practice: Dream to Delivered Event

*Capstone module. For a learner who has completed Modules 1–11.*

This is where it all comes together. You've learned the platform, OPE,
communication, safety, discovery, concept, requests, planning, execution, and
post-event work — one competency at a time. But real events don't arrive one
competency at a time. A client shows up with a vague dream, and you have to do
*all* of it, woven together, under real conditions. This module is practice at
exactly that.

**There is nothing new here.** No new concepts, no new competencies — every skill
you need has already been taught. This module exists to *integrate* them: to take
a single event from a client's first fuzzy message all the way to a returning
client, and to feel how each stage flows into the next.

**How to use this module.** Work through each scenario slowly, as if it were real
and yours. For every one, walk the full chain in your head — or better, out loud
or on paper:

1. **Communicate** with the client (Module 4)
2. **Discover** the facts that matter (Module 6)
3. **Identify the risks** (Module 5)
4. **Form a concept** (Module 7)
5. **Prepare an OPE-ready request** (Module 8)
6. **Evaluate the OPE plan** (Module 9)
7. **Handle the execution challenges** (Module 10)
8. **Manage the post-event follow-up** (Module 11)

Don't rush to an answer. The reflection questions at the end of each scenario have
no single right answer and no score — they're there to make you think like an
organizer. If you can move a scenario through all eight stages, making sound
decisions and catching the traps, you're ready to do it for real.

**What this module builds toward:** all nine competencies (C1–C9), working
together — the whole job, integrated.

## Scenario A — "A Royal Day" (children · budget · unclear request)

**1. Scenario description**

A father sends a short message: *"I want a royal day for my daughter — something
really special."* That's all. No age, no numbers, no date, no budget. As you
gently discover, the picture fills in: his daughter is turning six; he's imagining
around 15 children and a handful of parents; he'd like it at home in the backyard;
it's for a Saturday afternoon in summer; and when you carefully raise budget, it's
about $800 — modest for the grand "royal" vision in his head. In passing he
mentions, "oh, a couple of her friends are a bit fussy with food."

**2. What the organizer must accomplish**

- **Communicate:** reply warmly and promptly to a vague, emotional message, and
  open a real conversation.
- **Discover:** turn "a royal day" into facts — age, headcount (and *who*: children
  vs. adults), date, place, budget — and chase the "fussy with food" aside, which
  could be a real allergy.
- **Identify risks:** supervision for 15 six-year-olds, the small backyard, any
  food allergy hiding behind "fussy."
- **Form a concept:** a magical-but-cosy afternoon that delivers the *feeling* of a
  royal day within $800 — honest about what the budget can and can't do.
- **Prepare the request:** carry the real numbers, the ages, the budget, the
  supervision need, and any food restriction — without inventing anything.
- **Evaluate the plan:** check the supervision scales to 15 young children, the
  quantities and budget reconcile, nothing's missing.
- **Execution:** on the day, more children arrive than RSVP'd; manage it calmly.
- **Post-event:** follow up with the father, invite feedback, capture lessons.

**3. Key decisions**

- How do you deliver "royal" and "special" on a modest budget without either
  over-promising or quietly cutting the magic? (Concept + expectation-setting.)
- Is "a bit fussy with food" actually an allergy? How hard do you push to find out?
- Is the backyard safe and big enough for 15 running six-year-olds?
- On the day, with extra children, what do you stretch and what do you protect?

**4. Common mistakes**

- Planning a grand "royal" event the $800 can't cover, then disappointing him.
- Skipping the budget question because it felt awkward.
- Treating "fussy" as trivial and missing a real allergy.
- Under-providing supervision for a crowd of young children.
- Being thrown when more kids show up, instead of adapting calmly.

**5. Reflection questions**

- What would you say in your *first* reply to make the father feel both heard and
  guided?
- Which single discovery question, if skipped, could most endanger a child here?
- How would you describe the concept back to him so "royal" survives the budget?
- If 20 children arrive instead of 15, what's your first move?

## Scenario B — A Milestone Anniversary (seniors · accessibility · changing circumstances)

**1. Scenario description**

A woman wants *"an unforgettable evening for my parents' 40th anniversary."*
Through discovery: about 40 guests, mostly older friends and family; the budget is
comfortable; she pictures an evening event at a venue. Almost as an aside she says,
*"Dad tires more easily these days, and his knees aren't great."* Then, two weeks
before the event, she messages: her father has had a health setback — he's frailer
than before and can't manage a long evening or much walking.

**2. What the organizer must accomplish**

- **Communicate:** handle a warm brief, and later handle the sensitive
  health-update conversation with care.
- **Discover:** the real guest mix (mostly seniors), the father's mobility and
  stamina, access needs, timing sensitivities.
- **Identify risks:** access (stairs, distances, restrooms), stamina, timing,
  seating — heightened after the health setback.
- **Form a concept:** originally a warm evening celebration; after the change, an
  honest reshape toward a shorter, daytime, fully seated, easy-access gathering
  that still feels special.
- **Prepare the request:** communicate the access and seating needs and the
  changed timing clearly, so they survive into planning.
- **Evaluate the plan:** check step-free access, an accessible restroom, seating
  for everyone, gentle pace, nothing that strands the father.
- **Execution:** make sure access works in practice; help guests of honor through
  the day comfortably.
- **Post-event:** follow up; the client is emotional about her parents — close with
  warmth.

**3. Key decisions**

- When the father's health changes, how much do you reshape the concept, and how do
  you propose it without making the daughter feel the celebration is "less"?
- Is the chosen venue genuinely accessible, or does it need to change?
- Evening or daytime? What does the father's stamina decide?
- How do you keep the event feeling celebratory while quietly built around frailty?

**4. Common mistakes**

- Hearing "unforgettable evening" and missing the seniors angle inside it.
- Treating "Dad tires easily" as small talk rather than an event-shaping fact.
- Booking a venue with stairs and no accessible restroom.
- Failing to adapt the concept when circumstances change, or adapting it coldly.

**5. Reflection questions**

- The father's setback arrives two weeks out. What's the first thing you say to the
  daughter, and what do you propose?
- Which access details would you personally verify before committing to a venue?
- How do you keep a *seated, daytime, gentle* event from feeling like a downgrade?
- What would you check during execution to make sure the father is comfortable?

## Scenario C — An Outdoor Birthday with an Allergy and a Forecast (allergies · children · weather)

**1. Scenario description**

A parent asks for *"a fun outdoor birthday for my son next Saturday."* Discovery:
20 children, around age seven; it's at home with garden space; budget about $1,500;
and one attending child has a **severe nut allergy.** The forecast for Saturday is
a 70% chance of rain, and the garden has no covered area.

**2. What the organizer must accomplish**

- **Communicate:** confirm details warmly and raise the allergy and weather
  honestly, without alarming the parent.
- **Discover:** the headcount and ages, the severity of the allergy, the catering
  plan, the real garden space and its lack of cover.
- **Identify risks:** a life-threatening allergy; rain with no backup; supervising
  20 seven-year-olds outdoors.
- **Form a concept:** a fun outdoor party *with* a genuine weather backup and a
  controlled, allergy-safe food plan built in from the start.
- **Prepare the request:** make the allergy control, the weather dependency, and the
  supervision need explicit — none of these can be lost in translation.
- **Evaluate the plan:** does it include a real weather backup and proper allergy
  control? If the plan is missing either, fix it before it goes out.
- **Execution:** rain arrives mid-party — move calmly to the backup; stay alert to
  the allergy throughout, with responsible adults briefed.
- **Post-event:** follow up; reflect on how the weather and allergy were handled.

**3. Key decisions**

- The caterer says they "can't guarantee" nut-free. What do you do? (This is a
  safety floor, not a negotiation.)
- With 70% rain and no cover, what's your backup — a tent, an indoor space, a date
  conversation — and how does the budget shape that choice?
- Who, exactly, knows about the allergy and the emergency plan on the day?
- When the rain hits, what's your move, and how do you keep the children happy?

**4. Common mistakes**

- Accepting "we'll be careful" for a severe allergy.
- Leaving the weather to luck because a backup costs money.
- Letting the allergy or the weather backup drop out of the request.
- Freezing when the rain comes instead of moving to a pre-planned backup.

**5. Reflection questions**

- Which of the two risks here — allergy or weather — would you lock down *first*,
  and why?
- If the only weather backup blows the budget, how do you handle that with the
  client honestly?
- How do you keep an eye on one allergic child among 20 without making him feel
  singled out?
- What would you put in your lessons-learned after this event?

## Scenario D — A Family Reunion for All Ages (seniors · children · accessibility · group dynamics)

**1. Scenario description**

A client wants *"a wonderful day to bring the whole family together."* Discovery:
about 50 people spanning toddlers to grandparents; it's an all-day outdoor event;
the budget is moderate; one grandparent uses a wheelchair, and several others are
elderly. The children will want to run and play; the older guests will want shade,
seating, and calm. Everyone, somehow, in one event.

**2. What the organizer must accomplish**

- **Communicate:** understand a warm but broad brief and draw out the real shape of
  the family.
- **Discover:** the full age mix, the wheelchair user's access needs, the elders'
  needs, any allergies, the all-day outdoor reality.
- **Identify risks:** children near outdoor hazards; seniors and heat/stamina;
  accessibility for the wheelchair user; the genuinely competing needs of the two
  groups.
- **Form a concept:** a single event that *holds both groups* — space and energy
  outlets for the kids, shaded seated calm for the elders, with easy access
  throughout.
- **Prepare the request:** communicate the group dynamics, the access needs, the
  seating and shade, and the supervision clearly.
- **Evaluate the plan:** does it actually serve *both* groups, or has it optimized
  for one? Check access, seating, supervision, and pacing.
- **Execution:** all day outdoors — manage children, watch the elders in the heat,
  handle attendance that runs higher than expected.
- **Post-event:** follow up; expect *mixed* feedback (the two groups experienced
  different days) and hear it honestly.

**3. Key decisions**

- How do you design one concept that doesn't make either group an afterthought?
- Is the outdoor site genuinely accessible and safe for a wheelchair user and for
  wandering toddlers?
- How do you handle the heat and the long day for the older guests?
- When feedback splits — kids loved it, some elders found it tiring — what do you
  take from each?

**4. Common mistakes**

- Building the event around one group and stranding the other.
- Planning for an "average" guest who doesn't exist in a 50-person mixed family.
- Missing the wheelchair access on an outdoor site.
- Dismissing the elders' lukewarm feedback because the kids had a great time.

**5. Reflection questions**

- Describe your concept in one or two sentences. Does it visibly hold *both* groups?
- What would you physically check about the site before committing to it?
- How would you pace an all-day event so the older guests aren't exhausted by
  mid-afternoon?
- If 65 people show up instead of 50, where does that pressure land hardest, and
  what do you do?

## Scenario E — A Team Event, Vague and Shifting (unclear request · changing circumstances · budget · conflicting needs)

**1. Scenario description**

A manager messages: *"I want something fun for my team."* Nothing else. Discovery:
about 25 people, mixed fitness and ages; a defined budget; a target date in a few
weeks. She doesn't mention — and you have to draw out — that two team members are
pregnant and one has a knee injury. Then, a week before, things shift: the headcount
jumps to around 35 as another team joins, and the date moves forward by a few days.

**2. What the organizer must accomplish**

- **Communicate:** turn a one-line request into a real conversation, and later
  handle the late changes calmly.
- **Discover:** the real number, the mix, the budget, the date — *and* the hidden
  constraints (pregnant members, the injury) that exclude certain activities.
- **Identify risks:** an activity that excludes or endangers some members; the
  squeeze of a higher headcount on the same budget and plan.
- **Form a concept:** "fun and bonding" that *everyone* can genuinely take part in —
  not a high-intensity activity that sidelines three people.
- **Prepare the request:** carry the real headcount, the inclusivity constraints,
  and the budget clearly.
- **Evaluate the plan:** does it work for the *whole* team, within budget? When the
  numbers change, re-evaluate rather than forcing the old plan.
- **Execution:** absorb the late changes; if a vendor runs late or a piece shifts on
  the day, adapt without unraveling.
- **Post-event:** follow up; read a possibly-quiet client carefully for unspoken
  feedback.

**3. Key decisions**

- "Fun" for whom? How do you choose an activity that includes the pregnant and
  injured members rather than excluding them?
- When the headcount jumps to 35 on the same budget, what do you adjust — and what
  do you tell the client honestly?
- The date moves forward — what becomes harder, and what do you reconfirm?
- If the client is briefly polite and then quiet afterward, what does that signal,
  and what do you do?

**4. Common mistakes**

- Booking a default high-energy activity that three people can't do.
- Assuming the original 25-person plan still works at 35.
- Treating the late changes as the client's problem rather than adapting.
- Taking a brief "thanks" at face value and never closing the loop.

**5. Reflection questions**

- What's the one discovery question that prevents the most damage in this scenario?
- How would you reshape the concept the moment you learn about the pregnant and
  injured members?
- The headcount rises 40% a week out. Walk through how you'd re-evaluate the plan.
- After the event, the manager goes quiet. How do you follow up, and why does it
  matter?

## Using these scenarios well

A few ways to get the most from this capstone:

- **Always walk the whole chain.** The point isn't to solve one stage brilliantly —
  it's to carry an event from first message to follow-up without dropping anything.
  A discovered allergy that vanishes by the plan, or a great plan executed in a
  panic, is a chain that broke.
- **Notice where the stages connect.** The aside in discovery becomes the safety
  decision becomes the line in the request becomes the thing you check in the plan
  becomes what you watch on the day. Feel how one stage feeds the next.
- **Sit with the judgment calls.** The Key Decisions and Reflection Questions rarely
  have one right answer — two good organizers might choose differently. What matters
  is that your choice is sound, safe, honest, and faithful to the client and the
  people.
- **Bring forward the non-negotiables.** Across every scenario, the floors never
  move: safety is never traded away, you never invent facts, you never over-promise,
  and you never go silent. Those hold no matter what the event throws at you.

## Module 12 wrap-up — and the whole program

You've now practised the complete job: a client's vague dream, carried through
communication, discovery, risk, concept, request, plan, execution, and follow-up,
all the way to a delivered event and a client who'd come back. That is what being an
ActivLife Hub organizer *is* — not nine separate skills, but one capable person
weaving them together, event after event.

Look back at where you started, in Module 1, with one sentence: *the client brings
the dream, and you are responsible for everything it takes to make it real —
including finding out what the client never knew to tell you.* Every module since
has been teaching you how to live up to that sentence. These scenarios are where it
all became one motion.

The one thing to carry forward from the entire program: **a great organizer is not
the one whose events are flawless, but the one who — through the platform, with
honest discovery, sound judgment, real care for safety and people, and steady calm
when things shift — reliably turns dreams into real events, and gets a little better
at it every single time.**

You've completed the training path to Certified Organizer.
