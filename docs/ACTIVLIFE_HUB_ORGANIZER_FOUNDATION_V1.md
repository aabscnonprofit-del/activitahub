# ActivLife Hub Organizer Foundation v1

## What this document is

This is the single source of truth for how ActivLife Hub trains, tests,
certifies, and onboards organizers. It merges and reconciles three earlier
documents into one:

- Organizer Learning Structure v1
- Organizer Competency: Converting Client Requests into OPE Inputs
- ActivLife Hub Organizer Competencies v1

It introduces no new concepts, competencies, lessons, exams, certifications, or
pricing. It only consolidates what those three already established. Where they
overlapped, this keeps one clean version.

Everything that follows builds on top of this. If a future lesson, exam, or
certification rule disagrees with this document, this document wins until it is
deliberately updated.

---

## 1. Definitions

These four words mean exactly this throughout all ActivLife Hub material.

- **Participant** — a person who attends or joins an activity. The end user the
  event is for.
- **Client** — the person who asks an organizer to make an event happen. The
  client brings the dream and the budget. A client may also be a participant
  (someone planning their own party) or may be arranging it for others (a parent
  booking a child's birthday, a manager booking a team event). The client is
  **not** responsible for knowing what information is important.
- **Organizer** — the person who works *through* the ActivLife Hub platform to
  turn a client's dream into a delivered event. The organizer is responsible for
  discovering the information that matters, shaping the concept, planning with
  OPE, and executing. An organizer does not work independently of the platform.
- **OPE** — ActivLife Hub's planning engine. Given a structured request, OPE
  produces a real, workable event plan (people, place, resources, timing,
  budget). OPE plans; it does not interview the client and it does not run the
  event. It is only as good as the structured request it's given.

## 2. Organizer Principles

These six principles govern how every organizer is expected to behave. Every
competency below exists to make one of them real.

1. The client brings a **dream**, not a plan.
2. The organizer helps turn that dream into a **concept**.
3. OPE helps turn the concept into a **plan**.
4. The organizer **executes** the plan.
5. The client is **not** responsible for knowing what information is important.
6. The organizer **is** responsible for discovering the information that
   matters.

The first four describe the work. The last two settle who owns the gaps: the
client owns the *want*; the organizer owns the *work* — including finding out
what the client never thought to say.

The organizer also works as part of the platform, not as a freelancer who uses
it. Participants trust ActivLife Hub, and that trust is extended to the
organizer. It can be lost.

## 3. The Core Flow: Dream → Concept → OPE Plan → Execution

This is the spine the whole organizer role hangs on.

```
Client's dream  →  Concept  →  OPE plan  →  Event execution  →  Aftercare
```

What each stage is, and who does it:

- **Dream** — what the client wants, in their own words: "a royal day," "an
  unforgettable birthday," "something fun for my team." Emotionally real,
  operationally empty. Owned by the **client**.
- **Concept** — the dream turned into something concrete and shared: whose
  event, how many people, when, where, roughly what budget, what it should feel
  like. Built by the **organizer** by *discovering* the facts inside the dream
  and confirming them with the client. This is also where the organizer produces
  the **structured request** — the same concept, expressed in the specific facts
  OPE needs.
- **OPE plan** — the structured request turned into a complete, workable plan.
  Produced by **OPE**, then sense-checked and adjusted by the **organizer**
  against reality.
- **Execution** — the plan carried out on the day. Owned by the **organizer**.
- **Aftercare** — closing out, following up, handling feedback, and improving.
  Owned by the **organizer**.

The whole chain only works if the bridge between *dream* and *concept* is done
well. If the organizer guesses instead of discovering, OPE produces a confident
plan for the wrong event, and the trust is lost. That bridge — discovery and
concept-forming — is the organizer's most important skill.

## 4. Learning Structure

Organizers learn in a fixed order. Each section assumes the ones before it. The
order reflects the central rule: an organizer learns the platform before the
craft of running events.

**1. Understanding ActivLife Hub**
What the platform is for, the roles of organizers and participants, the trust
model, and what an organizer is responsible for. *Without this, nothing else
lands.*

**2. Using the Platform**
How to operate every working area: dashboard, activities, requests, proposals,
bookings, participants, messaging, calendar, OPE, and sourcing tools. *They must
be able to operate the platform before doing real work in it.*

**3. Client Communication**
Professionalism, respect, trust, handling difficult situations, the basics of
turning genuine interest into a booking, and the consistent standard expected of
every organizer. *They must be able to talk to clients before they take them
on.*

**4. Safety and Responsibility**
Participant safety, the added duties for children, seniors, and outdoor
settings, emergencies, and organizer liability. *They must know their duties
before designing anything that puts people at risk.*

**5. Event Planning Fundamentals**
Turning a client's request into a workable plan. This section opens with
discovery and concept-forming (the dream → concept bridge) and then builds the
plan with OPE. *Now they can plan.*

**6. Event Execution**
Final preparation and running the event on the day. *Now they can run what they
planned.*

**7. Post-Event Management**
Closing out, follow-up, feedback, and learning. *Now they can close the loop and
improve.*

## 5. Competency Framework

There are nine competencies. They map onto the learning structure and onto the
core flow. Each is required for a specific organizer level.

**The two statuses:**

- **Verified Organizer** — a **trust and identity status**. It confirms the
  person behind the account is real and accountable and meets the baseline for
  operating on the platform honestly and safely. It is *not* a measure of
  craft.
- **Certified Organizer** — proof of the full organizer **craft**: discovery,
  concept, planning, execution, and aftercare. The organizer the platform stands
  behind for real events.

The two are independent. Verified answers *"is this a real, accountable
person?"* Certified answers *"can this person take a dream all the way to a
delivered event?"*

**Certified Organizer can be reached two ways:**

1. Completing ActivLife Hub training and passing certification; **or**
2. Passing the experienced-organizer competency exam (the test-out route, for
   people who already organize real events).

Both routes prove the same craft and grant the same Certified status. Neither
route grants Verified status — that is a separate trust-and-identity check.

| # | Competency | What it proves | Verified | Certified |
|---|---|---|:---:|:---:|
| 1 | Understands ActivLife Hub and acts as part of it | Behaves as part of the platform participants trust | ✓ | ✓ |
| 2 | Operates the platform confidently | Can do real work through the platform end to end | ✓ | ✓ |
| 3 | Communicates professionally and builds trust | Can be trusted in front of a client | ✓ | ✓ |
| 4 | Meets the safety and responsibility baseline | Won't put people at avoidable risk | ✓ | ✓ |
| 5 | Discovers the information that matters | Pulls the real facts out of a vague dream | | ✓ |
| 6 | Turns a dream into a concept and a structured request | Produces something the client confirms and OPE can plan | | ✓ |
| 7 | Builds a real, workable plan with OPE | Turns the request into a deliverable plan | | ✓ |
| 8 | Executes the plan on the day | Runs the event without losing control | | ✓ |
| 9 | Closes out and learns from the event | Finishes well and improves | | ✓ |

For the full purpose, required behaviors, and good/bad examples of each
competency, the competency definitions stand as written; this framework is the
reconciled map of them.

**How the columns split:** Verified proves you are *safe to operate* on the
platform (1–4). Certified proves you can *take a client's dream all the way to a
delivered event* (5–9).

**How the competencies relate to the core flow:**

- Competencies 1–4 are the **foundation** — the platform, the people, and the
  safety duties the work rests on.
- Competency 5 is the **Dream → Concept** discovery step.
- Competency 6 finishes the concept and produces the **structured request**.
- Competency 7 is the **OPE plan**.
- Competency 8 is **Execution**.
- Competency 9 is **Aftercare**.

## 6. Competency Progression: Beginner to Professional

An organizer becomes professional in the same order they learn. There are three
recognizable stages.

**Stage 1 — Beginner (working toward Verified)**
Learning who ActivLife Hub is, how the platform works, how to communicate, and
the safety baseline. Building competencies 1–4. Not yet trusted with real
clients on their own.

**Stage 2 — Verified Organizer (real and accountable)**
Identity confirmed and the baseline for honest, safe operation met
(competencies 1–4). Verified is a trust-and-identity status, not a craft status:
it says the person is real and accountable, not that they have proven they can
carry a dream all the way to a delivered event.

**Stage 3 — Certified Organizer (professional)**
Has all nine competencies — the full craft on top of the foundation:

- discover the information that matters from a vague request (5),
- turn it into a concept and a structured request the client confirms (6),
- build a real, workable plan with OPE (7),
- execute it on the day (8),
- and close out and learn from it (9).

This is the full professional organizer: a client brings a dream, and this
organizer — through the platform and OPE — turns it into a real event and a
client who wants to come back.

**Certified can be reached two ways:** by completing ActivLife Hub training and
passing certification, or by passing the experienced-organizer competency exam
(the test-out route for people who already organize real events). Both prove the
same craft and grant the same Certified status.

The progression is cumulative in competence: a Certified Organizer never stops
needing competencies 1–4; the safety baseline and platform trust hold at every
stage. Note that Verified (Stage 2) is a separate trust-and-identity status, not
a required gate on the way to Certified — an experienced organizer may prove the
craft directly through the test-out route.

## 7. How to use this document

This foundation is what future work is built on:

- **Training** follows the learning structure (Section 4) and teaches toward the
  competencies (Section 5).
- **Exams** test the competencies (Section 5) and the progression (Section 6).
- **Certification** grants **Certified Organizer** status and can be reached two
  ways — ActivLife Hub training plus certification, or the experienced-organizer
  competency exam. **Verified Organizer** is a separate trust-and-identity status
  and is not granted by either certification route.
- **Onboarding** introduces new organizers to the principles (Section 2), the
  core flow (Section 3), and the definitions (Section 1) first.

Lessons, exams, certifications, and pricing are defined elsewhere, on top of
this. None of them should contradict it.
