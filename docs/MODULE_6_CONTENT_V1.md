# Module 6 — Discovery and Information Gathering

*Training content. For a person with no event-organizing experience.*

This is the module where you learn the single most important skill an organizer
has. Everything before this prepared you for it: you understand the platform, you
know OPE plans from what it's given, you can communicate, and you know your safety
duties. Now you learn how to take a client's vague wish and turn it into the real,
true facts that everything else depends on. This is **discovery.**

Hold these principles the whole way through, because they shape everything:

- **Clients are not responsible for knowing what information matters. You are.**
- **Discovery is not an interrogation.** You're having a warm conversation, not
  firing questions at someone.
- **Discovery is not a checklist.** A list of questions is a starting point, not
  the skill. The skill is understanding the person in front of you.
- **Discovery is understanding people well enough to plan responsibly.** That's the
  real definition. Everything else is technique.

You met some of this in Module 4, where you learned communication and trust.
Module 4 was about *how to talk with clients.* Module 6 points that skill at one
specific job: *finding out what you actually need to know.* Read the lessons in
order.

**What this module builds toward:** Competency 5 — *Discovers the information that
matters.*

---

## Lesson 6.1 — What Discovery Really Is

**Learning objective:** Explain what discovery is, why it's the organizer's job,
and why a vague request is the normal starting point.

**Lesson content**

Remember the chain from Module 1: Dream → Concept → Plan → Event. Discovery is the
work that turns the **dream into facts** — the bridge from "a royal day" to "a
7-year-old's party, 15 children, this date, this budget, at home." Without it, you
have nothing real to plan from. With it, everything downstream becomes possible.

Here's the thing beginners find hardest to accept: **the vague request is normal,
and it's not the client's fault.** Clients come to you with feelings — "make it
special," "something fun," "an unforgettable day" — because they're not
professionals. They don't know that the guest count changes everything, or that
"birthday" could mean a toddler or a grandmother, or that the budget decides what's
possible. Expecting them to hand you a clear brief is like expecting a patient to
diagnose themselves. The whole reason you exist is that they *can't* do this part.

So discovery isn't an annoying step before the real work — it *is* the real work,
the part only a person can do. And it's entirely yours. You don't wait for the
client to volunteer what matters; you go and find it, gently and skilfully,
because they don't know what "what matters" even is.

One more thing: discovery is not a form you fill in. Two clients can give the same
answers and need completely different events, because the facts live inside
*people* — their hopes, their guests, their situation. Your job isn't to collect
answers; it's to understand the client and their event well enough to plan it
responsibly. The questions are just how you get there.

**Real examples**

- A client says, "I want a royal day for my daughter." That's a pure dream — no
  age, no number, no budget, no date. This is the *normal* starting point, not a
  difficult client. Your discovery turns it into something real.
- A client says, "just make it amazing, I trust you." It's flattering, and it's a
  trap if you take it as permission to skip discovery. "Amazing" is still a
  feeling. You still need the facts — being trusted doesn't mean guessing.

**Common mistakes**

- *Treating a vague request as the client failing to do their job.* It's not their
  job — it's yours.
- *Thinking discovery is a box to tick before planning.* It's the core of the
  work, not a preliminary.
- *Collecting answers without understanding the person.* The same answers can mean
  different events.

**Knowledge check**

1. What does discovery turn a dream into?
   *(Real, true facts you can plan from.)*
2. Whose responsibility is discovery, and why?
   *(The organizer's — because clients aren't professionals and don't know what information matters.)*

## Lesson 6.2 — Asking Useful Questions

**Learning objective:** Ask questions that turn a vague request into clear facts,
warmly and without interrogating.

**Lesson content**

Discovery happens mostly through questions, but *how* you ask matters as much as
*what* you ask. A useful question is specific, gentle, and aimed at something you
genuinely need to know.

**Make questions specific.** "Tell me about your event" gives you a vague answer to
a vague question. "How many children are you expecting?" gives you a fact. Trade
broad for specific whenever you can.

**Keep it a conversation, not an interrogation.** Don't machine-gun a list of
questions. Space them out, react to the answers, explain why you're asking when it
helps ("I ask about ages because it changes how we keep everyone safe"). A client
should feel looked after, not processed.

**Aim at what you actually need.** Most events need the same core facts: what kind
of event, how many people, who's coming, the date, the budget, the place, and any
special needs. You'll go deep on these in the next lessons. The point here is that
your questions should *go somewhere* — each one earning a fact that changes the
plan.

**Follow the thread.** The best questions often come from the last answer. A client
mentions "mostly family" — you ask who, and learn there are toddlers and
grandparents. One good follow-up beats ten generic questions.

Asked this way, questions don't feel like an interrogation — they feel like someone
who cares enough to get it right. That builds trust *and* gets you the facts. Both
at once.

**Real examples**

- Client: "I want a birthday party." You: "Lovely — whose birthday, and how old
  will they be?" Client: "My son, he's turning five." Now you ask about numbers,
  date, budget, place. Each specific question turns the fog into facts, and it
  feels like a friendly chat.
- A client gives a one-word answer — "soonish" — to "when were you thinking?" You
  don't leave it. Warmly: "Do you have a particular weekend in mind, or a date
  it needs to be before?" Specific beats vague, kindly.

**Common mistakes**

- *Asking broad questions* that get broad, useless answers.
- *Firing a list of questions* so it feels like an interrogation.
- *Not following up* on an answer that clearly opens a door.

**Knowledge check**

1. What makes a question useful in discovery?
   *(It's specific, gently asked, and aimed at a fact you genuinely need.)*
2. Why is following up on an answer so powerful?
   *(The best questions come from the last answer — one good follow-up uncovers more than many generic questions.)*

## Lesson 6.3 — Listening for What Isn't Said

**Learning objective:** Catch the important information clients reveal indirectly,
forget to mention, or don't know is important.

**Lesson content**

Some of the most important things you'll learn, the client never directly tells
you. They mention them in passing, they leave them out entirely, or they don't
realize they matter. A great organizer hears these anyway. This is listening at a
deeper level than Module 4 — listening specifically for the gold that's half-hidden.

Three things to listen for:

**The throwaway line.** Clients bury crucial facts in casual asides. "Oh, and
Grandma will be there." "A couple of the kids are fussy." "It's right after the
school run." These sound like small talk, but they often change the whole event.
Train yourself to *catch* these and ask about them, not nod past them.

**The thing they forgot.** Clients forget details that are obvious to them but
invisible to you — that two guests don't drive, that the venue is up three flights,
that half the group are children. They're not hiding it; it just didn't occur to
them to say. You catch these by picturing the actual event and noticing what's
missing.

**The thing they don't know matters.** This is the deepest one. A client won't
mention their father's failing health as an "event detail" because they don't
connect it to the party — but it changes the timing and pace completely. You
surface these by gently exploring around the people involved, not just the logistics.

Listening for what isn't said is how you discover critical information *indirectly*
— and it's often where the make-or-break facts live.

**Real examples (these are the heart of discovery)**

- *Discovered indirectly:* A client planning an anniversary says, almost as an
  afterthought, "Dad tires more easily these days." They meant it as small talk.
  You hear an event-changing fact: a long evening event may be wrong, and timing,
  pace, and seating now matter. One aside reshaped the whole plan.
- *The client forgot:* A manager books a team activity and never mentions that two
  members are pregnant and one has a knee injury. They didn't hide it — it didn't
  cross their mind. You uncover it by asking, "is there anyone I should know about
  who can't do something physical?" — and avoid planning an event that sidelines
  three people.
- *The client didn't know it mattered:* A family books a big celebration and says
  nothing about food or drink, because to them it's obvious the guests keep certain
  dietary and alcohol customs. You ask, and discover a standard menu and open bar
  would have offended the very guests it was meant to honour.
- *A small detail, a whole new event:* "I've invited 40." You almost write down 40
  — then ask, and learn "invited" means the list; they actually expect about 25,
  and most are children. That one question changed the size, the supervision, and
  the budget.

**Common mistakes**

- *Nodding past the throwaway lines* where the real information hides.
- *Only recording what the client states directly,* missing what they forgot or
  didn't know to say.
- *Treating a casual comment as unimportant* because the client said it casually.

**Knowledge check**

1. Name the three kinds of unsaid information to listen for.
   *(The throwaway line; the thing they forgot; the thing they don't know matters.)*
2. A client mentions in passing "Dad tires more easily these days." Why does it
   matter? *(It's an event-changing fact — timing, pace, and seating may all need to change — hidden inside small talk.)*

## Lesson 6.4 — The Facts Every Event Needs: Numbers, Budget, Location, Timing

**Learning objective:** Reliably discover the core facts — how many people, budget,
where, and when — and recognise when one is missing.

**Lesson content**

Almost every event rests on a handful of core facts. If any one is missing or
wrong, the plan is built on sand. Your job is to discover all of them, and to
*notice* when one is still unknown.

**Numbers — how many, and who.** The headcount drives nearly everything: staff,
food, space, cost, safety. But a number alone isn't enough — *who* those people are
matters as much. "40 people" could be 40 adults or 40 toddlers. And beware the soft
number: "about 30," "I've invited 40," "a few." Push gently for a real expected
figure, and remember that "invited" isn't "attending."

**Budget — the one beginners skip.** Budget feels awkward to ask, so new organizers
avoid it — and then build a plan the client can't afford. Don't. Ask it kindly,
clearly, and early, because it decides what's possible. If a client won't name a
number, help them with ranges. A plan without a real budget is a guess.

**Location — where, really.** Where an event happens shapes everything from cost to
safety. Is it at a home, a park, a hall? Indoors or out? You also need enough about
the actual place to spot problems — stairs for a guest who can't manage them, an
unfenced pond near children. "At my place" may need follow-up: *what's your place
like?*

**Timing — when, and what's around it.** The date, the time of day, and how long.
Timing carries hidden facts: midday heat is hard on older guests, an evening event
is wrong for someone who tires easily, "right after the school run" tells you about
the children coming. And check the date actually works before you build around it.

The skill isn't just collecting these — it's *noticing the hole.* If you reach the
end of a conversation and you still don't know the budget, or you're not sure of
the real headcount, that's a flashing light. Go back and get it.

**Real examples**

- *Numbers:* A client says "around 30 guests." You ask more and it's really closer
  to 55, half of them children. Had you planned for 30 adults, the supervision,
  food, and cost would all have been wrong.
- *Budget:* A client describes a lavish vision and never mentions money. You ask
  warmly, and learn the budget is a third of what the vision needs — now, while
  it's easy to reshape, instead of after a plan everyone loved.
- *Location:* "At my place" turns out to be a third-floor flat with no lift — which
  matters enormously once you know an older guest is coming. You only learned it
  because you asked what "my place" was like.
- *Timing:* A client picks midday for a seniors' garden lunch in summer. You flag
  the heat and move it earlier — a timing fact that was also a safety fact.

**Common mistakes**

- *Accepting a soft number* ("about 30," "I invited 40") as the real headcount.
- *Skipping the budget question* because it feels uncomfortable.
- *Not learning enough about the actual location* to spot its problems.
- *Reaching the end of discovery without noticing a core fact is still missing.*

**Knowledge check**

1. What are the four core facts nearly every event needs?
   *(How many people / who, budget, location, timing.)*
2. Why is "I've invited 40" not a usable headcount?
   *(Invited isn't attending — you need the real expected number.)*

## Lesson 6.5 — Discovering Who's Coming: Participant Needs

**Learning objective:** Discover the specific needs of the people attending —
including children, seniors, access, disability, allergy, and group dynamics — so
the event works safely for everyone.

**Lesson content**

A headcount tells you how many. This lesson is about *who* — and what each of them
needs. This is where discovery and safety meet, because the needs you uncover here
are exactly what keep people safe and included. You can only meet a need you've
discovered, which is why this part of discovery is so important.

Go looking, gently, for:

- **Children.** How many, and how young? Children change supervision, safety, food,
  and the whole shape of an event. "Some kids will be there" is not enough — find
  out how many and how old.
- **Seniors.** Older guests may need seating, gentler pace, easy access, and care
  about heat and timing. Often nobody mentions it — you ask.
- **Accessibility and disabilities.** Will anyone need step-free access, an
  accessible restroom, a quiet space, or other support? A venue that excludes a
  guest is a discovery failure, not just a logistics one. Ask directly and kindly.
- **Allergies and medical needs.** Ask about food allergies and anything medical
  that could matter on the day — these can be life-or-death, and clients routinely
  forget to mention them because "everyone knows."
- **Group dynamics.** Who are these people *together*? A team with two new parents
  and an injured colleague; a family of toddlers and grandparents in one room; a
  group that doesn't all know each other. The mix shapes what will actually work.

The reason to dig here is simple: these facts are almost never volunteered, and
they're often the ones that matter most. A single discovered need — one allergy,
one wheelchair, one guest who needs quiet — can be the difference between an event
that works for everyone and one that endangers or excludes someone.

**Real examples**

- *Small detail, whole new event:* A client books a party and casually says "Grandma
  will be there." You ask, and learn she uses a walker. Suddenly the venue's stairs
  are a real problem, and you change the venue. One guest, mentioned in passing,
  changed the location.
- *They forgot:* A parent booking a kids' party doesn't mention a child's severe nut
  allergy — they assumed people would be careful. You ask about food restrictions
  anyway, and discover it. That question may prevent a medical emergency.
- *Group dynamics:* A family reunion will have toddlers and elderly grandparents
  together, all day, outdoors. Once you discover the mix, you know the event needs
  to serve two very different groups at once — something a headcount alone would
  never tell you.
- *Inclusion you'd otherwise miss:* A client mentions one guest "doesn't speak much
  English" and another "gets overwhelmed in crowds." You ask what each needs, and
  build in a quieter space and clear, simple instructions — so nobody's left lost.

**Common mistakes**

- *Stopping at the headcount* and never discovering who the people actually are.
- *Waiting for the client to volunteer* allergies, access needs, or medical facts —
  they usually won't.
- *Ignoring the group mix,* and planning one event for people who need different
  things.

**Knowledge check**

1. Why do you have to actively discover participant needs rather than wait for them?
   *(They're almost never volunteered, yet they're often the facts that matter most for safety and inclusion.)*
2. A client says "Grandma will be there." What should you do?
   *(Ask about her needs — mobility, access, seating — because one guest's needs can change the whole plan.)*

## Lesson 6.6 — Detecting Assumptions and Confirming Understanding

**Learning objective:** Catch the assumptions — yours and the client's — and
confirm your understanding before you plan.

**Lesson content**

Assumptions are where discovery quietly goes wrong. Both you and the client make
them, and unspoken assumptions cause events to be planned for the wrong reality.

**Catch your own assumptions.** You will fill gaps automatically — "birthday" so you
assume children; "party" so you assume evening; "team event" so you assume the
office crowd. Notice when you've *assumed* a fact rather than *learned* it. The test
is simple: for each important fact, can you point to the moment the client actually
told you? If not, you assumed it — go check.

**Catch the client's assumptions.** Clients assume *you* know things that are obvious
to them. They assume you know the guests are mostly children, that it's a religious
family, that the venue is hard to reach. Surface these by reflecting back what you
*think* you know and letting them correct you.

**Catch contradictions.** When two facts don't fit — "small intimate dinner" and "60
people," "tight budget" and "a lavish theme" — don't quietly pick one. Name it
gently and ask which matters more.

Then, before you move to planning, **confirm your understanding.** Say back the
whole picture in plain words and get a clear "yes." This is not a formality — it's
where assumptions get caught and corrected for free, before they cost anything. "So,
to make sure I've got this right: your mum's 60th, about 40 adults, mid-July, at
your home, around $3,000, relaxed and warm — is that it?" That one sentence,
confirmed, is worth more than pages of notes.

**Real examples**

- *Your assumption:* A client says "birthday party" and you start picturing
  children — then catch yourself: you never asked whose birthday. You do, and it's
  their 70-year-old mother. Catching your own assumption saved you from planning a
  kids' party for seniors.
- *Their assumption / contradiction:* A client says "a small, intimate dinner" and
  later "about 60 people." Rather than guess, you ask, "when you say intimate, is the
  priority a cosy feel or fitting all 60 comfortably?" — and learn what really
  matters.
- *Confirming:* You read the whole picture back to a client, and they say, "yes —
  oh, except it's Saturday, not Sunday." A small correction caught in ten seconds
  that would have wrecked the event if you'd planned around the wrong day.

**Common mistakes**

- *Filling gaps with assumptions* and not noticing you've done it.
- *Leaving contradictions unresolved,* silently picking one side.
- *Skipping the confirmation step* and planning on what you *think* you heard.

**Knowledge check**

1. How do you test whether you've assumed a fact or actually learned it?
   *(Ask whether you can point to the moment the client told you; if not, you assumed it — go check.)*
2. Why is confirming your understanding before planning so valuable?
   *(It catches assumptions, contradictions, and errors for free, before they cost anything.)*

## Lesson 6.7 — Knowing When You Have Enough

**Learning objective:** Judge when discovery is complete enough to plan — neither
guessing too early nor over-questioning.

**Lesson content**

Discovery has two failure modes. One is stopping too early — planning on guesses
and gaps. The other, less obvious, is never stopping — questioning the client into
exhaustion, chasing every tiny detail, never moving forward. A good organizer knows
when they have *enough.*

You have enough when you can answer "yes" to a simple test: **could I now describe
this event clearly, truthfully, and completely enough to plan it responsibly?** In
practice that means you know the core facts (numbers and who, budget, location,
timing), you've discovered the participant needs that matter for safety and
inclusion, you've resolved the contradictions, and you've confirmed the picture
with the client.

It does *not* mean you know every tiny preference. You don't need to have settled
the colour of the napkins to start planning — those details can come later. The
line is responsibility: do you know enough that your plan won't be wrong in a way
that matters — wrong size, wrong budget, wrong for the people, unsafe? If yes, you
have enough. If a *material* fact is still missing or guessed, you don't — go get
it.

Two quick checks before you call it:

- **The missing-fact check:** is any core fact still soft or unknown? (A guessed
  headcount or an unknown budget means you're not done.)
- **The safety check:** do I know enough about who's coming to keep them safe? (An
  undiscovered allergy or access need means you're not done.)

When both check out and the client has confirmed the picture, stop. You're ready to
shape the concept and plan. Over-questioning past this point doesn't make the event
better — it just wears out the client and delays the work.

**Real examples**

- *Stopped too early:* An organizer takes "a party for about 30" and starts
  planning, never pinning the number, the budget, or the ages. The plan is wrong in
  every direction. They didn't have enough and didn't notice.
- *Knew when to stop:* An organizer has the real headcount, the budget, the place,
  the date, knows there are young children and one guest with an allergy, has
  resolved the "intimate vs 60" question, and the client has confirmed the picture.
  They stop asking and start planning — they have enough, and chasing more would
  just tire the client.

**Common mistakes**

- *Starting to plan while a core fact is still a guess.*
- *Over-questioning* long past the point of having what you need, exhausting the
  client.
- *Skipping the two checks* (missing-fact and safety) before deciding you're done.

**Knowledge check**

1. What's the simple test for "enough" discovery?
   *(Could I now describe this event clearly, truthfully, and completely enough to plan it responsibly?)*
2. What are the two quick checks before you stop?
   *(The missing-fact check — is any core fact still soft? — and the safety check — do I know enough to keep everyone safe?)*

## Module 6 wrap-up

You now know how to discover the information that actually matters:

- **What discovery is** — turning the dream into facts; the organizer's job, not the
  client's; understanding people, not filling a form.
- **Asking useful questions** — specific, gentle, aimed, and following the thread.
- **Listening for what isn't said** — the throwaway line, the forgotten detail, the
  thing they don't know matters.
- **The core facts** — numbers and who, budget, location, timing — and noticing when
  one is missing.
- **Participant needs** — children, seniors, access, disability, allergies, group
  dynamics; discovered, never assumed.
- **Detecting assumptions and confirming** — catch what you and the client assumed,
  resolve contradictions, and confirm the picture before planning.
- **Knowing when you have enough** — when you could describe the event well enough
  to plan it responsibly, and the missing-fact and safety checks pass.

The one sentence to carry forward: **the client brings a feeling, and your job is
to discover the truth behind it — every fact that matters, including the ones they
forgot, said in passing, or never knew were important — until you understand the
event well enough to plan it responsibly.**

Next, you'll take everything you've discovered and shape it into something real and
agreed: **Module 7 — Concept Formation.**
